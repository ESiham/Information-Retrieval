<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Instability</h1>

<p>In numerous fields of study, the component of <b>instability</b> within a <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> is generally characterized by some of the outputs or internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_(controls)" title="State (controls)">states</a> growing without <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Not all systems that are not <a href="/wiki/Stability_theory" title="Stability theory">stable</a> are unstable; systems can also be <a href="/wiki/Marginal_stability" title="Marginal stability">marginally stable</a> or exhibit <a href="/wiki/Limit_cycle" title="Limit cycle">limit cycle</a> behavior.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">structural engineering</a>, a structure can become unstable when excessive load is applied. Beyond a certain threshold, structural <a href="/wiki/Deflection_(engineering)" title="Deflection (engineering)">deflections</a> magnify <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stresses</a>, which in turn increases deflections. This can take the form of <a href="/wiki/Buckling" title="Buckling">buckling</a> or crippling. The general field of study is called <a href="/wiki/Structural_stability" title="Structural stability">structural stability</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">Atmospheric instability</a> is a major component of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_system" title="Weather system">weather systems</a> on Earth.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instability_in_control_systems">Instability in control systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Instability in control systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stabilizability" title="Stabilizability">Stabilizability</a></div>
<p>In the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamical_systems" title="Dynamical systems">dynamical systems</a>, a <a href="/wiki/State_variable" title="State variable">state variable</a> in a system is said to be unstable if it evolves without bounds. A system itself is said to be unstable if at least one of its state variables is unstable.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_time" title="Continuous time">continuous time</a> <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a>, a system is unstable if any of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_of_a_function" title="Root of a function">roots</a> of its <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_equation_(calculus)" title="Characteristic equation (calculus)">characteristic equation</a> has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_part" title="Real part">real part</a> greater than zero (or if zero is a repeated root). This is equivalent to any of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvalues" title="Eigenvalues">eigenvalues</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_space_(controls)" title="State space (controls)">state matrix</a> having either real part greater than zero, or, for the eigenvalues on the imaginary axis, the algebraic multiplicity being larger than the geometric multiplicity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="what is the difference between these two types of multiplicity? (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The equivalent condition in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discrete_time" title="Discrete time">discrete time</a> is that at least one of the eigenvalues is greater than 1 in absolute value, or that two or more eigenvalues are equal and of unit absolute value.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasma_instabilities">Plasma instabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plasma instabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plasma_stability#Plasma_instabilities" title="Plasma stability">Plasma stability § Plasma instabilities</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">Plasma</a> instabilities can be divided into two general groups (1) hydrodynamic instabilities (2) kinetic instabilities. Plasma instabilities are also categorised into different modes – see <a href="/wiki/Plasma_stability#Plasma_instabilities" title="Plasma stability">this paragraph in plasma stability</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instabilities_of_stellar_systems">Instabilities of stellar systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Instabilities of stellar systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">Galaxies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a> can be unstable, if small perturbations in the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential</a> cause changes in the density that reinforce the original perturbation. Such instabilities usually require that the motions of stars be highly correlated, so that the perturbation is not "smeared out" by random motions. After the instability has run its course, the system is typically "hotter" (the motions are more random) or rounder than before. Instabilities in stellar systems include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_instability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bar instability (page does not exist)">Bar instability</a> of rapidly rotating disks</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jeans_instability" title="Jeans instability">Jeans instability</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Firehose_instability" title="Firehose instability">Firehose instability</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MS94_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MS94-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gravothermal_instability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gravothermal instability (page does not exist)">Gravothermal instability</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_instabilities">Joint instabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Joint instabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common residual disability after any sprain in the body is instability. Mechanical instability includes insufficient stabilizing structures and mobility that exceed the physiological limits. Functional instability involves recurrent sprains or a feeling of giving way of the injured joint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Injuries cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proprioceptive" title="Proprioceptive">proprioceptive</a> deficits and impaired postural control in the joint. Individuals with muscular weakness, occult instability, and decreased postural control are more susceptible to injury than those with better postural control. Instability leads to an increase in postural sway, the measurement of the time and distance a subject spends away from an ideal <a href="/wiki/Center_of_pressure_(terrestrial_locomotion)" title="Center of pressure (terrestrial locomotion)">center of pressure</a>. The measurement of a subject’s postural sway can be calculated through testing center of pressure (CoP), which is defined as the vertical projection of center of mass on the ground. Investigators have theorized that if injuries to joints cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafferentation" title="Deafferentation">deafferentation</a>, the interruption of sensory nerve fibers, and functional instability, then a subject’s postural sway should be altered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Joint stability can be enhanced by the use of an external support system, like a brace, to alter body mechanics. The mechanical support provided by a brace provides cutaneous afferent feedback in maintaining postural control and increasing stability.</p>
