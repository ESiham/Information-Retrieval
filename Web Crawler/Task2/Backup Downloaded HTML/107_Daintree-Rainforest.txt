<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Daintree Rainforest</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Daintree_Rainforest&amp;params=16.2_S_145.4_E_source:dewiki"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">16°12′S</span> <span class="longitude">145°24′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">16.2°S 145.4°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-16.2; 145.4</span></span></span></a></span></span></span> The <b>Daintree Rainforest</b> (strictly a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_seasonal_forest" title="Tropical seasonal forest">tropical seasonal forest</a>), is a region located on the north east coast of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia, north of <a href="/wiki/Mossman,_Queensland" title="Mossman, Queensland">Mossman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairns" title="Cairns">Cairns</a>. At around 1,200 square kilometres (460 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the Daintree is the largest continuous area of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australian continent</a>. Along the coastline north of the <a href="/wiki/Daintree_River" title="Daintree River">Daintree River</a>, tropical forest grows right down to the edge of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Q150" title="Q150">Q150</a> celebrations, the Daintree Rainforest was announced as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q150_Icons" title="Q150 Icons">Q150 Icons</a> of Queensland for its role as a "Natural attraction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="About_Daintree_Rainforest">About Daintree Rainforest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daintree_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: About Daintree Rainforest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg/220px-Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg/330px-Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg/440px-Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Daintree_Rainforest_3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Daintree Rainforest</div>
</div>

<p>The rainforest is named after <a href="/wiki/Richard_Daintree" title="Richard Daintree">Richard Daintree</a>, an Australian geologist and photographer (1832-1878).</p>
<p>The area includes the Daintree National Park, some areas of State Forest, and some privately owned land, including a residential community of upwards of 5 people. Some of the privately owned land north of the Peninsula Range is being progressively purchased for <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a> purposes under a $15 million government scheme involving equal contributions from the municipal (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cairns_Regional_Council" title="Cairns Regional Council">Cairns Regional Council</a>, which includes the former Douglas Shire council), State (<a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Federal_Government" title="Australian Federal Government">Australian Federal governments</a>. As of May 2011, 72% of the properties earmarked for buyback or compensation had been secured. These 'buyback' areas of tropical rainforest included 215 blocks of land purchased by the Queensland Parks and Wildlife Service, and 13 purchased by private conservation agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Daintree Rainforest contains 30% of the <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a> species in Australia, and 90% of Australia's <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a> species. 7% of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species in the country can be found in this area. There are also over 12,000 species of insects in the rainforest. All of this <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">diversity</a> is contained within an area that takes up 0.1% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landmass" title="Landmass">landmass</a> of Australia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Part of the forest is protected by the <a href="/wiki/Daintree_National_Park" title="Daintree National Park">Daintree National Park</a> and drained by the <a href="/wiki/Daintree_River" title="Daintree River">Daintree River</a>. The roads north of the river wind through areas of lush forest, and have been designed to minimize impacts on this ancient ecosystem.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Daintree_Rainforest">Daintree Rainforest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daintree_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Daintree Rainforest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Daintree_Rainforest.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="657" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Daintree_Rainforest.JPG/220px-Daintree_Rainforest.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Daintree_Rainforest.JPG/330px-Daintree_Rainforest.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Daintree_Rainforest.JPG/440px-Daintree_Rainforest.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Daintree_Rainforest.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Daintree Rainforest</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The tropical rainforest ecosystem of the Daintree Rainforest is one of the most complex on Earth. Its plant diversity and structural complexity is unrivalled on the Australian continent and represents the origins of its more ‘Australian’ flora.</p>
<p>Millions of years ago<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (April 2016)">quantify</span></a></i>]</sup>, the Australian continent was warm and humid and rainfall was plentiful. During this time rainforest thrived in places such as the Uluru (Ayers Rock) region. As Australia became more arid, there were fewer and fewer places rainforests were able to survive. In the Daintree region, however the climate and topography were ideal, so the area became a last remaining refuge for rainforest. Within this refuge many species were able to live comfortably without reason to change. Their descendants that are still living today retained many of their ancestors' primitive characteristics, some dating back 110 million years.</p>
<p>One species in particular, <a href="/wiki/Idiospermum" title="Idiospermum"><i>Idiospermum australiense</i></a>, commonly known as the idiot fruit, is one of the rarest and most primitive of the flowering plants. Its discovery in 1970 was arguably Australia’s most significant botanical find, greatly increasing scientists awareness of just how ancient these forests really are.</p>
<p>From a total of 19 primitive flowering plant families on Earth, 12 families are represented in the Daintree region making the highest concentration of these plants worldwide. These ancient plant families may well hold the secret to a number of unanswered questions regarding the origins of the flowering plants – plants on which the human race depends for food and medicines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintree-rec.com.au_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-daintree-rec.com.au-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploring">Exploring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daintree_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exploring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_(22533827153).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg/220px-Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg/330px-Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg/440px-Camping_Daintree_National_Parc_Nationalpark_%2822533827153%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>There is outstanding coastal scenery that combines tropical rainforest, white sandy beaches and fringing reefs just offshore. This is an extremely rare combination.</p>
<p>To the west of Cape Tribulation stands Mt Pieter Botte with its massive granite outcrops. The summit providing expansive vistas of undisturbed forest and to the south the skyline is dominated by the giant granite boulders of Thornton Peak – one of Queensland’s highest mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintree-rec.com.au_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-daintree-rec.com.au-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the Daintree Rainforest is part of the <a href="/wiki/Wet_Tropics_of_Queensland" title="Wet Tropics of Queensland">Wet Tropics</a> of Queensland <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>, being listed by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 2015 in recognition of its universal natural values highlighted by the rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amongst the attributes provided as evidence for the World Heritage value of the Wet Tropics, which include the Daintree Rainforest, the Australian Government <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> lists the following:</p>
<p><i>They preserve major stages of the earth's evolutionary history -</i></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>ancient plants representing some of the earliest land plants, the <i><a href="/wiki/Psilotopsida" title="Psilotopsida">Psilotopsida</a></i> (whisk ferns) and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycopsida" title="Lycopsida">Lycopsida</a></i> (club mosses or tassel ferns);</li>
<li>7 ancient <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a> of true <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Marattiaceae" title="Marattiaceae">Marattiaceae</a> (giant or king ferns), <a href="/wiki/Osmundaceae" title="Osmundaceae">Osmundaceae</a> (royal ferns), <a href="/wiki/Schizaeaceae" title="Schizaeaceae">Schizaeaceae</a> (comb ferns) and <a href="/wiki/Gleicheniaceae" title="Gleicheniaceae">Gleicheniaceae</a> (coral ferns);</li>
<li>fern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondwanan" title="Gondwanan">East Gondwanan</a> origins, including <i><a href="/wiki/Polystichum" title="Polystichum">Polystichum</a></i> (shield ferns), <i><a href="/wiki/Leptopteris" title="Leptopteris">Leptopteris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Todea" title="Todea">Todea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tmesipteris" title="Tmesipteris">Tmesipteris</a></i> (fork ferns), <i><a href="/wiki/Lycopodiella" title="Lycopodiella">Lycopodiella</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Huperzia" title="Huperzia">Huperzia</a></i> (club mosses and tassel ferns);</li>
<li>the ancient, fern-like <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycad</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bowenia_spectabilis" title="Bowenia spectabilis">Bowenia spectabilis</a></i> (zamia fern) and other cycads including <i><a href="/wiki/Cycas" title="Cycas">Cycas</a></i>, and the giant <i><a href="/wiki/Lepidozamia_hopei" title="Lepidozamia hopei">Lepidozamia hopei</a></i> (zamia palm);</li>
<li>ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifers" title="Conifers">conifers</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Podocarpus" title="Podocarpus">Podocarpus</a></i> (plum pine or brown pine), <i><a href="/wiki/Prumnopitys" title="Prumnopitys">Prumnopitys</a></i> (brown pine or southern yew), <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria" title="Araucaria">Araucaria</a></i> (hoop and bunya pines), and <i><a href="/wiki/Agathis" title="Agathis">Agathis</a></i> (kauri) which are living counterparts of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>-age fossils (i.e., age of the dinosaurs);</li>
<li>12 primitive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperm</a> (flowering plant) <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">families</a>, including small, primitive, relict angiosperm families such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrobaileyaceae" title="Austrobaileyaceae">Austrobaileyaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiospermaceae" title="Idiospermaceae">Idiospermaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eupomatiaceae" title="Eupomatiaceae">Eupomatiaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Himantandraceae" title="Himantandraceae">Himantandraceae</a>;</li>
<li>relict angiosperm plant families that are known as fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> (last age of the dinosaurs) including <a href="/wiki/Cunoniaceae" title="Cunoniaceae">Cunoniaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a> (banksia and macadamia family), <a href="/wiki/Winteraceae" title="Winteraceae">Winteraceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a> (eucalypt and lilly pilly), <a href="/wiki/Monimiaceae" title="Monimiaceae">Monimiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapindaceae" title="Sapindaceae">Sapindaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquifoliaceae" title="Aquifoliaceae">Aquifoliaceae</a> (holly family), <a href="/wiki/Chloranthaceae" title="Chloranthaceae">Chloranthaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trimeniaceae" title="Trimeniaceae">Trimeniaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epacridaceae" title="Epacridaceae">Epacridaceae</a> (heath family), <a href="/wiki/Olacaceae" title="Olacaceae">Olacaceae</a> and families of angiosperms to represent the longest continuous history associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondwanan" title="Gondwanan">Gondwanan</a> landmass.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>

<p><i>They preserve unique, rare or superlative natural phenomena, formations or features of exceptional natural beauty</i> –</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>exceptional coastal scenery unusual in the world (and Australia) where tropical rainforest extends to white sandy beaches with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fringing_Reefs" title="Fringing Reefs">fringing coral reefs</a> just offshore;</li>
<li>rugged mountain peaks and gorges with swiftly flowing rivers and spectacular waterfalls (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Thornton_Peak" title="Thornton Peak">Thornton Peak</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mossman_Gorge" title="Mossman Gorge">Mossman Gorge</a>, Roaring Meg Falls);</li>
<li>extensive vistas of undisturbed forest and valleys.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The Daintree rainforest contains important and significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitats" title="Habitats">habitats</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" title="Conservation ethic">conservation</a> of biological diversity. Approximately 430 species of birds live among the trees. The primitive flowering plants <i><a href="/wiki/Austrobaileya" title="Austrobaileya">Austrobaileya scandens</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiospermum_australiense" title="Idiospermum australiense">Idiospermum australiense</a></i> are also <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to the Daintree.</p>
<p>The Daintree Region is home to a number of rare and endangered species, including the <a href="/wiki/Southern_cassowary" title="Southern cassowary">southern cassowary</a> (<i>Casuarius casuarius</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Bennett%27s_tree-kangaroo" title="Bennett's tree-kangaroo">Bennett's tree-kangaroo</a> (<i>Dendrolagus bennettianus</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Daintree_Important_Bird_Area">Daintree Important Bird Area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daintree_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Daintree Important Bird Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Daintree <a href="/wiki/Important_Bird_Area" title="Important Bird Area">Important Bird Area</a> (IBA) is a 2656 km<sup>2</sup> tract of land that largely coincides with the northernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Wet_Tropics_of_Queensland" title="Wet Tropics of Queensland">Wet Tropics of Queensland</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. It encompasses, or overlaps, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain_(Kalkajaka)_National_Park" title="Black Mountain (Kalkajaka) National Park">Black Mountain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Bay_National_Park" title="Cedar Bay National Park">Cedar Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daintree_National_Park" title="Daintree National Park">Daintree</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Windsor_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Windsor National Park (page does not exist)">Mount Windsor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mowbray_National_Park" title="Mowbray National Park">Mowbray National Parks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bli_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bli-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been identified as an IBA by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> because it supports a population of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_cassowaries" title="Southern cassowaries">southern cassowaries</a>. It also contains populations of the locally endemic <a href="/wiki/Tooth-billed_bowerbird" title="Tooth-billed bowerbird">tooth-billed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_bowerbird" title="Golden bowerbird">golden bowerbirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lovely_fairywren" title="Lovely fairywren">lovely fairywrens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macleay%27s_honeyeater" title="Macleay's honeyeater">Macleay's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridled_honeyeater" title="Bridled honeyeater">bridled</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow-spotted_honeyeater" title="Yellow-spotted honeyeater">yellow-spotted</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-streaked_honeyeater" title="White-streaked honeyeater">white-streaked honeyeaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernwren" title="Fernwren">fernwrens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atherton_scrubwren" title="Atherton scrubwren">Atherton scrubwrens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_thornbill" title="Mountain thornbill">mountain thornbills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chowchilla" title="Chowchilla">chowchillas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bower%27s_shrike-thrush" title="Bower's shrike-thrush">Bower's shrike-thrushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pied_monarch" title="Pied monarch">pied monarchs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_riflebird" title="Victoria's riflebird">Victoria's riflebirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pale-yellow_robin" title="Pale-yellow robin">pale-yellow robins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
