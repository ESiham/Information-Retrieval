<p>The <b>Hawaiian tropical rainforests</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests">tropical moist broadleaf forest</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>. They cover an area of 6,700 km<sup>2</sup> (2,600 sq mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">windward</a> lowlands and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">montane</a> regions of the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup> Coastal mesic forests are found at elevations from sea level to 300 m (980 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kay_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kay-3">[3]</a></sup> Mixed <a href="/wiki/Mesic_habitat" title="Mesic habitat">mesic</a> forests occur at elevations of 750 to 1,250 m (2,460 to 4,100 ft), while wet forests are found from 1,250 to 1,700 m (4,100 to 5,580 ft). Moist <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrublands</a> exist on montane <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateaus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">depressions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup> For the 28 million years of existence of the Hawaiian Islands, they have been isolated from the rest of the world by vast stretches of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, and this isolation has resulted in the evolution of an incredible diversity of endemic species, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>. In the lush, moist forests high in the mountains, trees are draped with <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid">orchids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, and mosses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WildWord_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WildWord-4">[4]</a></sup> This ecoregion includes one of the world's wettest places, the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Waialeale" title="Mount Waialeale">Mount Wai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ale<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ale</a>, which average 460 in (12,000 mm) of rainfall per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_mesic_forests">Coastal mesic forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_tropical_rainforests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Coastal mesic forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">Coastal</a> <a href="/wiki/Mesic_habitat" title="Mesic habitat">mesic</a> forests are found on the windward slopes of the major islands from sea level to 300 m (980 ft). These forests have been dominated by the native hala (<i><a href="/wiki/Pandanus_tectorius" title="Pandanus tectorius">Pandanus tectorius</a></i>) and hau (<i><a href="/wiki/Hibiscus_tiliaceus" title="Hibiscus tiliaceus">Hibiscus tiliaceus</a></i>) and naturalized (<a href="/wiki/Canoe_plants" title="Canoe plants">Polynesian introductions</a>) kukui (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candlenut" title="Candlenut">Aleurites moluccana</a></i>) and milo (<i><a href="/wiki/Thespesia_populnea" title="Thespesia populnea">Thespesia populnea</a></i>) for the past 1,000–2,000 years. The Polynesian-introduced noni (<i><a href="/wiki/Morinda_citrifolia" title="Morinda citrifolia">Morinda citrifolia</a></i>), pia (<i><a href="/wiki/Tacca_leontopetaloides" title="Tacca leontopetaloides">Tacca leontopetaloides</a></i>), and kī (<i><a href="/wiki/Cordyline_fruticosa" title="Cordyline fruticosa">Cordyline fruticosa</a></i>) are also common in this zone. Other native species include pololei (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum" title="Ophioglossum">Ophioglossum</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ophioglossum_concinnum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ophioglossum concinnum (page does not exist)">concinnum</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kay_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kay-3">[3]</a></sup> <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ākia (<i><a href="/wiki/Wikstroemia" title="Wikstroemia">Wikstroemia</a></i> spp.), loulu fan <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pritchardia" title="Pritchardia">Pritchardia</a></i> spp.), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhi<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>a lehua (<i><a href="/wiki/Metrosideros_polymorpha" title="Metrosideros polymorpha">Metrosideros polymorpha</a></i>), and lama (<i><a href="/wiki/Diospyros_sandwicensis" title="Diospyros sandwicensis">Diospyros sandwicensis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_mesic_forests">Mixed mesic forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_tropical_rainforests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mixed mesic forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mixed <a href="/wiki/Mesic_habitat" title="Mesic habitat">mesic</a> forests, at 750 to 1,250 m (2,460 to 4,100 ft) on the <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">windward</a> slopes of the large islands in addition to the summit of Mount Lāna<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ihale on <a href="/wiki/Lanai" title="Lanai">Lāna<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i</a>, receive 1,000 to 2,500 mm (39 to 98 in) of rainfall annually and thus may not be true <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>. The forest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a>, dominated by koa (<i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_koa" title="Acacia koa">Acacia koa</a></i>) and <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhi<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>a lehua (<i><a href="/wiki/Metrosideros_polymorpha" title="Metrosideros polymorpha">Metrosideros polymorpha</a></i>), is somewhat open, but tree density is rather high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziegler_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ziegler-7">[7]</a></sup> Other trees and shrubs include pāpala (<i><a href="/wiki/Charpentiera_obovata" title="Charpentiera obovata">Charpentiera obovata</a></i>), olopua (<i><a href="/wiki/Nestegis_sandwicensis" title="Nestegis sandwicensis">Nestegis sandwicensis</a></i>), hame (<i><a href="/wiki/Antidesma_platyphyllum" title="Antidesma platyphyllum">Antidesma platyphyllum</a></i>), mēhame (<i><a href="/wiki/Antidesma" title="Antidesma">A.</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antidesma_pulvinatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antidesma pulvinatum (page does not exist)">pulvinatum</a></i>), kōpiko (<i><a href="/wiki/Psychotria_mariniana" title="Psychotria mariniana">Psychotria mariniana</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōpiko (<i><a href="/wiki/Psychotria" title="Psychotria">P.</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Psychotria_mauiensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Psychotria mauiensis (page does not exist)">mauiensis</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>iliahi (<i><a href="/wiki/Santalum_freycinetianum" title="Santalum freycinetianum">Santalum freycinetianum</a></i>), hōlei (<i><a href="/wiki/Ochrosia" title="Ochrosia">Ochrosia</a></i> spp.), po<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>olā (<i><a href="/wiki/Claoxylon" title="Claoxylon">Claoxylon</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Claoxylon_sandwicense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Claoxylon sandwicense (page does not exist)">sandwicense</a></i>), kōlea lau nui (<i><a href="/wiki/Myrsine_lessertiana" title="Myrsine lessertiana">Myrsine lessertiana</a></i>), kauila (<i><a href="/wiki/Alphitonia_ponderosa" title="Alphitonia ponderosa">Alphitonia ponderosa</a></i>), nioi (<i><a href="/wiki/Eugenia_reinwardtiana" title="Eugenia reinwardtiana">Eugenia reinwardtiana</a></i>), a<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ia<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i (<i><a href="/wiki/Streblus_pendulinus" title="Streblus pendulinus">Streblus pendulinus</a></i>), and hō<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>awa (<i><a href="/wiki/Pittosporum" title="Pittosporum">Pittosporum</a></i> spp.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohmer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sohmer-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wet_forests">Wet forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_tropical_rainforests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wet forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wet forests generally occur from 1,250 to 1,700 m (4,100 to 5,580 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup> but may be as low as 200 m (660 ft). They receive 3,000 to 11,250 mm (118 to 443 in) of rain per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziegler_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ziegler-7">[7]</a></sup> <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>Ōhi<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>a lehua (<i><a href="/wiki/Metrosideros_polymorpha" title="Metrosideros polymorpha">Metrosideros polymorpha</a></i>) is the dominant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a> species in wet forests, but koa (<i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_koa" title="Acacia koa">Acacia koa</a></i>) is also very common. Other trees include kāwa<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>u (<i><a href="/wiki/Ilex_anomala" title="Ilex anomala">Ilex anomala</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>alani (<i><a href="/wiki/Melicope" title="Melicope">Melicope</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Melicope_clusiifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Melicope clusiifolia (page does not exist)">clusiifolia</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhi<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>a ha (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syzygium_sandwicensis" title="Syzygium sandwicensis">Syzygium sandwicensis</a></i>), kōlea lau nui (<i><a href="/wiki/Myrsine_lessertiana" title="Myrsine lessertiana">Myrsine lessertiana</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ohe (<i><a href="/wiki/Tetraplasandra" title="Tetraplasandra">Tetraplasandra</a></i> spp.), and olomea (<i><a href="/wiki/Perrottetia" title="Perrottetia">Perrottetia</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Perrottetia_sandwicensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Perrottetia sandwicensis (page does not exist)">sandwicensis</a></i>) as well as hāpu<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>u (<i><a href="/wiki/Cibotium" title="Cibotium">Cibotium</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">tree ferns</a>). <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>Ape<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ape (<i><a href="/wiki/Gunnera" title="Gunnera">Gunnera</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gunnera_petaloidea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gunnera petaloidea (page does not exist)">petaloidea</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>oha wai (<i><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_lobelioids#Clermontia" title="Hawaiian lobelioids">Clermontia</a></i> spp.), hāhā (<i><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_lobelioids#Cyanea" title="Hawaiian lobelioids">Cyanea</a></i> spp.), kāmakahala (<i><a href="/wiki/Labordia_hirtella" title="Labordia hirtella">Labordia hirtella</a></i>), kanawao (<i><a href="/wiki/Broussaisia" title="Broussaisia">Broussaisia arguta</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostegia" title="Phyllostegia">Phyllostegia</a></i> spp., <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ākala (<i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_hawaiensis" title="Rubus hawaiensis">Rubus hawaiensis</a></i>), kāmanamana (<i><a href="/wiki/Adenostemma" title="Adenostemma">Adenostemma</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adenostemma_lavenia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adenostemma lavenia (page does not exist)">lavenia</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Pilea" title="Pilea">Pilea</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pilea_peploides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pilea peploides (page does not exist)">peploides</a></i>, māmaki (<i><a href="/wiki/Pipturus_albidus" title="Pipturus albidus">Pipturus albidus</a></i>), olonā (<i><a href="/wiki/Touchardia_latifolia" title="Touchardia latifolia">Touchardia latifolia</a></i>), and <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ala<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ala wai nui (<i><a href="/wiki/Peperomia" title="Peperomia">Peperomia</a></i> spp.) are common <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">understory</a> plants. <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">Vines</a> include maile (<i><a href="/wiki/Alyxia_oliviformis" title="Alyxia oliviformis">Alyxia oliviformis</a></i>) and hoi kuahiwi (<i><a href="/wiki/Smilax" title="Smilax">Smilax</a> <a href="/wiki/Smilax_melastomifolia" title="Smilax melastomifolia">melastomifolia</a></i>). <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>Ie<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i.e. (<i><a href="/wiki/Freycinetia_arborea" title="Freycinetia arborea">Freycinetia arborea</a></i>), pua<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>akuhinia (<i><a href="/wiki/Astelia" title="Astelia">Astelia</a> <a href="/wiki/Astelia_menziesiana" title="Astelia menziesiana">menziesiana</a></i>) and <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōlapa (<i><a href="/wiki/Cheirodendron_trigynum" title="Cheirodendron trigynum">Cheirodendron trigynum</a></i>) are <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytic</a> <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> found in wet forests. Epiphytic <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Adenophorus" title="Adenophorus">Adenophorus</a></i> spp., ohiaku (<i><a href="/wiki/Hymenophyllum" title="Hymenophyllum">Hymenophyllum</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hymenophyllum_recurvum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hymenophyllum recurvum (page does not exist)">recurvum</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum" title="Ophioglossum">Ophioglossum</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ophioglossum_pendulum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ophioglossum pendulum (page does not exist)">pendulum</a></i>, <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ākaha (<i><a href="/wiki/Asplenium_nidus" title="Asplenium nidus">Asplenium nidus</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ēkaha (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elaphoglossum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elaphoglossum (page does not exist)">Elaphoglossum</a> hirtum</i>), and makue lau lii (<i><a href="/wiki/Grammitis" title="Grammitis">Grammitis</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grammitis_hookeri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grammitis hookeri (page does not exist)">hookeri</a></i>), cover trees. Epyphytic <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a> include <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acroporium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acroporium (page does not exist)">Acroporium</a> fuscoflavum</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rhizogonium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rhizogonium (page does not exist)">Rhizogonium</a> spiniforme</i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Macromitrium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Macromitrium (page does not exist)">Macromitrium</a> owahiense</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohmer_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sohmer-8">[8]</a></sup> Loulu fan <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pritchardia" title="Pritchardia">Pritchardia</a></i> spp.) may tower over the forest canopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bogs">Bogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_tropical_rainforests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">Bogs</a> are found in montane regions where rainfall exceeds drainage. Dominant vegetation in bogs are <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a>. Larger shrubs and small trees grow on bog perimeters or on raised hummocks. <i><a href="/wiki/Carex" title="Carex">Carex</a></i> spp., <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oreobolus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oreobolus (page does not exist)">Oreobolus</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oreobolus_furcatus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oreobolus furcatus (page does not exist)">furcatus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhynchospora" title="Rhynchospora">Rhynchospora</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rhynchospora_rugosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rhynchospora rugosa (page does not exist)">rugosa</a></i> are common sedges, shrubs include <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhelo kau la<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>au (<i><a href="/wiki/Vaccinium" title="Vaccinium">Vaccinium</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vaccinium_calycinum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vaccinium calycinum (page does not exist)">calycinum</a></i>) and <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhelo (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vaccinium_dentatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vaccinium dentatum (page does not exist)">V. dentatum</a></i>), while grasses are represented by <i><a href="/wiki/Dichanthelium" title="Dichanthelium">Dichanthelium</a></i> spp. and <i><a href="/wiki/Deschampsia" title="Deschampsia">Deschampsia</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deschampsia_nubigena&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deschampsia nubigena (page does not exist)">nubigena</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohmer_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sohmer-8">[8]</a></sup> Dwarf <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a> of <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ōhi<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>a lehua (<i><a href="/wiki/Metrosideros_polymorpha" title="Metrosideros polymorpha">Metrosideros polymorpha</a></i> vars. <i>incana</i> and <i>glabriofolia</i>) are the most seen trees on the edges of bogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbour_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barbour-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> wāwae<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>iole (<i><a href="/wiki/Lycopodiella_cernua" title="Lycopodiella cernua">Lycopodiella cernua</a></i>), <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ama<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>u (<i><a href="/wiki/Sadleria" title="Sadleria">Sadleria</a></i> spp.),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohmer_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sohmer-8">[8]</a></sup> and uluhe (<i><a href="/wiki/Dicranopteris_linearis" title="Dicranopteris linearis">Dicranopteris linearis</a></i>) grow in bogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbour_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barbour-10">[10]</a></sup> Rare plants include liliwai (<i><a href="/wiki/Acaena_exigua" title="Acaena exigua">Acaena exigua</a></i>), na<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ena<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>e (<i><a href="/wiki/Dubautia" title="Dubautia">Dubautia</a></i> spp.), and <i><a href="/wiki/Argyroxiphium" title="Argyroxiphium">Argyroxiphium</a></i> spp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohmer_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sohmer-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
