<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Temperate rainforests of the Russian Far East</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Primorsky_Krai_Map.png"><img alt="A" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1640" data-file-width="1640" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Primorsky_Krai_Map.png/150px-Primorsky_Krai_Map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Primorsky_Krai_Map.png/225px-Primorsky_Krai_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Primorsky_Krai_Map.png/300px-Primorsky_Krai_Map.png 2x" width="150"/></a>

<p>The <b>temperate rainforests of the Russian Far East</b> are found within the Russian states of <a href="/wiki/Primorsky_Krai" title="Primorsky Krai">Primorsky Krai</a> and Khabarovski Krai and contains the <a href="/wiki/Sikhote-Alin" title="Sikhote-Alin">Sikhote-Alin</a> mountain range. Found within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, this area is one of the most productive and diverse forests in the world and also contains one of the highest <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> densities on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2000-1">[1]</a></sup> While most <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforests</a> around the world have retained only a fraction of their historical range, these forests maintain the majority of their former range and almost all of their historical <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2000_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2000-1">[1]</a></sup> The region is also notable for having what has become the last remaining large tract of viable habitat for the critically endangered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amur_tiger" title="Amur tiger">Amur tiger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amur_leopard" title="Amur leopard">Amur leopard</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maximovka_valley.jpg"><img alt="A" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Maximovka_valley.jpg/220px-Maximovka_valley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Maximovka_valley.jpg/330px-Maximovka_valley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Maximovka_valley.jpg/440px-Maximovka_valley.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maximovka_valley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Maximovka valley</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Russian Federation contains one sixth of the world’s land mass and around half of the country’s territory is what could be considered continuous <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>, with large expanses of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forests" title="Boreal forests">boreal forests</a> providing relatively untouched habitat for an extremely wide diversity of species. The temperate rainforests are located at the intersection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_tectonic_plate" title="Pacific tectonic plate">Pacific tectonic plate</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian_continent" title="Eurasian continent">Eurasian continent</a>’s plate in far southeastern Russia, forming an area with a unique set of characteristics seen no where else in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> During the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_maximum" title="Glacial maximum">glacial maximum</a>, the area was not glaciated, allowing for the development of a complex <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> containing species with origins in Siberia’s boreal forest and Manchuria’s subtropical forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Historically these forests ranged from the southeastern Pacific coast of Russia, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, and into northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, however large amounts of human development (in China especially) has limited the forest to its current range in the Russian Far East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2001, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> recognized a 1.5 million hectare area of forest in the central part of the Sikhote-Alin mountains as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in Russia, citing the area as one of the most unique and valuable areas of intact forest in the world <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These forests are found dispersed throughout the Sikhote-Alin mountain range, which extend over 1000 km along the west coast of the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>. The mountains have a maximum height of 1850 m, while the average elevation within the range is around 1000 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2000_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2000-1">[1]</a></sup> The major rivers include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ussuri" title="Ussuri">Ussuri</a>, which acts as the western border with China and empties into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amur_river" title="Amur river">Amur river</a>, which in turn flows northeast to empties into the Sea of Okhostk. The <a href="/wiki/Bikin_River" title="Bikin River">Bikin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iman_River" title="Iman River">Iman</a> rivers act as tributaries to the Ussuri, flowing west from the Sikhote-Alin mountain range.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg"><img alt="A" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg/300px-%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg/450px-%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg/600px-%D0%AF%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%82-%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B0.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Known as the "Ussuri taiga," this region of Russia has long, cold winters and fairly mild summers to go along with a mean <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> of 800–1000 mm per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> During the summer and fall, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsoonal" title="Monsoonal">monsoonal</a> influence brings <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storms" title="Tropical storms">tropical storms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a> coming from the southeast, resulting in substantial rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fog</a> occurs as a result of difference in temperatures between the continent and the ocean, resulting in more moderate temperatures, higher humidities; helping to prevent substantial water loss from occurring due to <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Continental winds coming in from Siberia bring cooler, dry air during winter, resulting in lighter precipitation (30–50 cm of snow).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Winters can be long and bitterly cold with January mean temperatures ranging from -15 to -20 °C and snow covering the forest floor from October to April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kobayashi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kobayashi-4">[4]</a></sup> The driest parts of the year are from April to June and September to October, which also happen to coincide with the greatest threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fire" title="Forest fire">forest fire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The forests fall in the transition zone between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomes" title="Biomes">biomes</a>: the southern Asian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardwood_forest" title="Hardwood forest">hardwood forest</a> and the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coniferous_forest" title="Coniferous forest">coniferous forest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2002_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2002-5">[5]</a></sup> There have been around 2500 species of vascular plants and 300 vertebrate species recorded in Primorsky Krai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2002_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2002-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Larix_gmelinii0.jpg"><img alt="A" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="335" data-file-width="479" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Larix_gmelinii0.jpg/300px-Larix_gmelinii0.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Larix_gmelinii0.jpg/450px-Larix_gmelinii0.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Larix_gmelinii0.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Larix_gmelinii0.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Gmelin larch (<i>Larix gmelini</i>) stand in the Russian Far East</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The forest is a mix of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadleaf" title="Broadleaf">broadleaf</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a>, with the forest type becoming more conifer dominated at higher elevations and more broad-leaf-conifer mixed at the lower elevations and within valleys. The most common species include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_pine" title="Korean pine">Korean pine</a> (<i>Pinus koraiensis</i>) and needle fir (<i>Abies holophylla</i>) at the lowest elevations and coastlines. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jezo_spruce" title="Jezo spruce">Jezo spruce</a> (<i>Picea jezoensis</i>) and Manchurian fir (<i>Abies nephrolepis</i>) are common species to be found from 700–1400 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala2011_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Other tree species include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongolian_oak" title="Mongolian oak">Mongolian oak</a> (<i>Quercus mongolica</i>), <a href="/wiki/Betula_pendula" title="Betula pendula">white birch</a> (<i>Betula platyphylla</i>), <a href="/wiki/Scots_pine" title="Scots pine">Scots pine</a> (<i>Pinus sylvestris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Populus_tremula" title="Populus tremula">trembling aspen</a> (<i>Populus tremula</i>), Dahurian birch (<i>Betula davurica</i>), and Gmelin larch (<i>Larix gemlinii</i>), a deciduous evergreen tree common throughout, but dominant in the northern reaches of the forest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2002_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2002-5">[5]</a></sup> The Amur region of Russia holds the last remaining habitats of the critically endangered Amur tiger, Amur leopard, and Manchurian sika deer. It has been estimated that there are less the 600 tigers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2006-6">[6]</a></sup> and around 40 leopards left in the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The area also contains populations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asiatic_black_bears" title="Asiatic black bears">Asiatic black bears</a> and Kamchatka brown bears, as the Russian Far East is the only place in the world where tigers, brown bears, and leopards coexist. This region also happens to be some of the last of habitat of the Blakiston’s fish owl (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bubo_blakistoni" title="Bubo blakistoni">bubo blakistoni</a>); along with being the world’s largest owl, it is unique in the way that it eats fish (primarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masu_salmon" title="Masu salmon">Masu salmon</a>) and relies on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth_forests" title="Old growth forests">old growth forests</a> along river banks to feed, nest, and breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Common ungulates include <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">roe deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, Manchurian moose, and <a href="/wiki/Musk_deer" title="Musk deer">musk deer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_structure_and_disturbances">Forest structure and disturbances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Forest structure and disturbances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fire and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> constitute the primary disturbances in this area, both of which are occurring at an increasing frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kobayashi_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kobayashi-4">[4]</a></sup> Illegal logging practices, along with a political climate more favorable to multinational logging corporations has drastically increased the amount of timber taken per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2002_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2002-5">[5]</a></sup> The occurrence of Korean pine in dominant forests has been declining as more common and more intense wildfires start to take their toll on the species, limiting its ability to recover and changing areas that were once dominated by old growth Korean pine to Mongolian oak and birch forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman2002_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2006-6">[6]</a></sup> Above 700–800 meters in altitude, the forest type transitions from broadleaf to coniferous, dominated by Jezo spruce and Manchurian fir. The further to the north, the altitudinal gradient decreases with the increase in latitude, resulting in coniferous forests at sea level at 47° N latitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2006_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2006-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperate_rainforests_of_the_Russian_Far_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under the USSR control, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation</a> strategies focused on the creation and a network of strictly protected nature reserves, called <a href="/wiki/Zapovednik" title="Zapovednik">zapovednik</a> and partially protected wildlife refuges, called <a href="/wiki/Zakaznik" title="Zakaznik">zakaznik</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2006_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2006-6">[6]</a></sup> These conservation strategies were largely successful and well maintained, as 85 zapovedniks and 26 national parks remained within Russian control after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Fall of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a> in December 1991. Following the break up, a period of economic hardship has ensued in Russia, resulting in conservation funding cuts and increases in illegal logging and poaching, casting serious doubt on the viabilities of the protected areas and the many rare and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> species that they contain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinerstein_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dinerstein-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg"><img alt="A" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2004" data-file-width="3000" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg/400px-Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg/600px-Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg/800px-Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siberian_tiger_hanging_at_bar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Amur (Siberian) tiger</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Conservation policies have been falling from federal control down to a regional level, which creates funding and enforcement challenges, but also allows for a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass-roots" title="Grass-roots">grass-roots</a>, bottom up approach to conservation, which despite the challenges has seen some success. Much of the recent funding for conservation studies and implementation of legislation comes from donors and organizations in the United States, where investing in conserving the biodiversity in the Russian Far East is seen as a good economic investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinerstein_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dinerstein-9">[9]</a></sup> At a species level, Korean pine is a highly valued type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, and although the logging of the species is restricted, <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a> practices thrive due to a lack of enforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinerstein_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dinerstein-9">[9]</a></sup> It should be noted that Korean pine forests also receive the most interest when it comes to conservationists, as they have been found to have the highest densities of tigers and their prey. Because of this, it appears that the longevity of the temperate rain forests in the Russian Far East will most likely depend on the success of the habitat conservation efforts for the endangered species found there, particularly those for the Amur tiger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinerstein_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dinerstein-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
