<p><b>Deforestation of the Amazon rainforest</b> accelerated significantly between 1991 and 2004, reaching an annual forest loss rate of 27,423 km² in 2004. Though the rate of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> has been slowing since 2004 (with re-accelerations in 2008 and 2013), the remaining forest cover continues to dwindle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rates_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-rates-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> represents over half of the planet's remaining rainforests, and comprises the largest and most biodiverse tract of tropical rainforest in the world. 60% of the <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> is contained within <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> with 13%, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> with 10%, and with minor amounts in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> sector of the Brazilian Amazon, incentivized by the international <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> trades,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saved_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-saved-2">[2]</a></sup> has been responsible for about 80% of all <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in the region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cattle1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cattle1-3">[3]</a></sup> or about 14% of the world's total annual deforestation, making it the world's largest single driver of deforestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slaughtering_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-slaughtering-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1995, 70% of formerly forested land in the Amazon, and 91% of land deforested since 1970, had been converted to cattle ranching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fao-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Much of the remaining deforestation within the Amazon has resulted from farmers clearing land for small-scale <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence agriculture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subsistence_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-subsistence-7">[7]</a></sup> or mechanized <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">cropland</a> producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soy" title="Soy">soy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm</a>, and other crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:149372.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1772" data-file-width="2193" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/149372.jpeg/220px-149372.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/149372.jpeg/330px-149372.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/149372.jpeg/440px-149372.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:149372.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Raoni" title="Chief Raoni">Chief Raoni</a>, one of the main opponents of deforestation of the Amazon rainforest</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">pre-Columbian era</a>, parts of the Amazon Rainforest were a densely populated open agriculturae. After the European colonization in the 16th century, with the hunt for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, Western diseases, slavery and later the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> boom, the Amazon Rainforest was depopulated and the forest grew larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the 1970s, access to the forest's largely roadless interior was difficult, and aside from partial clearing along rivers the forest remained intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-10">[10]</a></sup> Deforestation accelerated greatly following the opening of highways deep into the forest, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-Amazonian_highway" title="Trans-Amazonian highway">Trans-Amazonian highway</a> in 1972.</p>
<p>In parts of the Amazon the poor soil made plantation-based agriculture unprofitable. The key turning point in deforestation of the Brazilian Amazon was when colonists began to establish farms within the forest during the 1960s. Their farming system was based on crop cultivation and the <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> method. However, the colonists were unable to successfully manage their fields and the crops due to the loss of <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">soil fertility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a> invasion due to this method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watkins_griffiths_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-watkins_griffiths-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In indigenous areas of the Peruvian Amazon, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urarina" title="Urarina">Urarina</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chambira_River" title="Chambira River">Chambira River</a> <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">Basin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the soils are productive for only relatively short periods of time, therefore causing indigenous horticulturalists like the Urarina to move to new areas and clear more and more land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watkins_griffiths_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-watkins_griffiths-11">[11]</a></sup> Amazonian colonization was ruled by cattle raising because ranching required little labor, generated decent profits, and awarded social status in the community. Additionally, grass is able to grow in the poor Amazon soil. However, the abundance of cattle ranching led to extensive deforestation, causing extensive environmental damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>An estimated 30 percent of the deforestation is due to the actions of small farmers. Although small farmers possess smaller total land area than medium and large ranchers, who possess 89% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_Amazon" title="Legal Amazon">Legal Amazon</a>'s private land, the intensity of deforestation within the areas that they inhabit is greater than that within the areas occupied by the larger ranchers. This emphasizes the importance of using previously cleared land for agricultural use, rather the typically easier political path of distributing still-forested areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-14">[14]</a></sup> In the Brazilian Amazon, the proportion of small farmers to large landholders changes frequently with economic and demographic pressures.</p>
<p>In 2009, Peruvian President <a href="/wiki/Alan_Garc%C3%ADa" title="Alan García">Alan García</a> pushed through by executive decree Law 840<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-april2009_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-april2009-15">[15]</a></sup> (also known as "Ley de la Selva," "the Law of the Jungle" or simply the "Forest Law"), which allowed the sale of uncultivated Amazon land under state ownership to private companies, without term limits on the property rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> While the law was promoted as a "reforestation" measure, critics claimed the privatization measure would in fact encourage further deforestation of the Amazon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> while surrendering the nation's rights over natural resources to foreign investors and leaving uncertain the fate of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Peru" title="Indigenous peoples in Peru">Peru's indigenous people</a>, who do not typically hold formal title to the forestlands on which they subsist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-april2009_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-april2009-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Law 840 met widespread resistance and was eventually repealed by Peru's legislature for being unconstitutional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-april2009_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-april2009-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 illegal deforestation of the amazon was on the rise again for the first time in decades, this was largely a result of consumer demand for products like <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As consumer pressure increases, Brazilian farmers clear their land to make more space for crops like palm oil, and soy <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Also studies done by Greenpeace showed that 300 billion tons of carbon, 40 times the emissions from fossil fuels released into the atmosphere annually by deforestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livescience.com_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-livescience.com-21">[21]</a></sup> In addition to the carbon release associated with deforestation, NASA has estimated that if deforestation levels proceed, the remaining worlds forests will disappear in about 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livescience.com_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-livescience.com-21">[21]</a></sup> The Brazilian government adopted a program called RED (United Nations Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation Program) in order to help prevent deforestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican.com-22">[22]</a></sup> The RED program has helped more than 44 countries across Africa with the development of education programs, and has donated more than 117 million to the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican.com_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican.com-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_deforestation">Causes of deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes of deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Deforestation of the Amazon rainforest can be attributed to many different factors at local, national, and international levels. The rainforest is seen as a resource for cattle pasture, valuable hardwoods, housing space, farming space (especially for soybeans), road works (such as highways and smaller roads) and medicines.</p>
<p>A 2009 Greenpeace report found that the <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> sector in the Brazilian Amazon, supported by the international <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> trades, was responsible for about 80% of all deforestation in the region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cattle1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-cattle1-3">[3]</a></sup> or about 14% of the world's total annual deforestation, making it the largest single driver of deforestation in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slaughtering_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-slaughtering-4">[4]</a></sup> According to a 2006 report by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization_of_the_United_Nations" title="Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations">Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations</a>, 70% of formerly forested land in the Amazon, and 91% of land deforested since 1970, is used for livestock <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">pasture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-fao-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additional deforestation in the Amazon has resulted from farmers clearing land for small-scale <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence agriculture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subsistence_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-subsistence-7">[7]</a></sup> or for mechanized <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">cropland</a>. Scientists using <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> satellite data found in 2006 that clearing for mechanized cropland had become a significant force in Brazilian Amazon deforestation. This change in land use may alter the region's climate. Researchers found that in 2003, a peak year of deforestation, more than 20 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso" title="Mato Grosso">Mato Grosso</a> state's forests were converted to cropland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2005, soybean prices fell by more than 25 percent and some areas of Mato Grosso showed a decrease in large deforestation events, suggesting that the rise and fall of prices for other crops, beef and timber may also have a significant impact on future land use in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 2006, a major driver of forest loss in the Amazon was the cultivation of <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soy</a>, mainly for export and production of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel_by_region#Brazil" title="Biodiesel by region">biodiesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">animal feed</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> as soybean prices have risen, soy farmers pushed northwards into forested areas of the Amazon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soy1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-soy1-25">[25]</a></sup> However, a private sector agreement referred to as the Soy Moratorium has helped drastically reduce the deforestation linked to soy production in the region. In 2006, a number of major commodity trading companies such as <a href="/wiki/Cargill" title="Cargill">Cargill</a> agreed to not purchase soybeans produced in the Brazilian Amazon on recently deforested areas. Before the moratorium, 30 percent of soy field expansion had occurred through deforestation, contributing to record deforestation rates. After eight years of the moratorium, a 2015 study found that although soy production area had expanded another 1.3 million hectares, only about 1 percent of the new soy expansion had come at the expense of forest. In response to the moratorium, farmers were choosing to plant on already cleared land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soy1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-soy1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The needs of soy farmers have been used to validate some controversial transportation projects that have developed in the Amazon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-10">[10]</a></sup> The first two highways, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodovia_Bel%C3%A9m-Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Rodovia Belém-Brasília">Belém-Brasília</a> (1958) and the Cuiaba-Porto Velho (1968), were the only federal highways in the <a href="/wiki/Amaz%C3%B4nia_Legal" title="Amazônia Legal">Legal Amazon</a> to be paved and passable year-round before the late 1990s. These two highways are said to be "at the heart of the 'arc of deforestation'", which at present is the focal point area of deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon. The Belém-Brasília highway attracted nearly two million settlers in the first twenty years. The success of the Belém-Brasília highway in opening up the forest was reenacted as paved roads continued to be developed, unleashing the irrepressible spread of settlement. The completions of the roads were followed by a wave of resettlement; these settlers had a significant effect on the forest as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research conducted by Leydimere Oliveira et al. has shown that the more rainforest is logged in the Amazon, the less precipitation reaches the area and so the lower the yield per hectare becomes. Thus for Brazil as a whole, there is no economic gain to be made by logging and selling trees and using the logged land for pastoral purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>A September 2016 <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Watch" title="Amazon Watch">Amazon Watch</a> report concludes that imports of crude oil by the US are driving rainforest destruction in the Amazon and releasing significant greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milman-2016_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-milman-2016-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zuckerman-and-koenig-2016_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-zuckerman-and-koenig-2016-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_loss_rates">Forest loss rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forest loss rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The annual rate of deforestation in the Amazon region dramatically increased from 1991 to 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-10">[10]</a></sup> In the nine years from 1991 to 2000, the total area of <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> cleared since 1970 rose from 419,010 to 575,903 km<sup>2</sup> (161,781 to 222,357 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rates_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-rates-1">[1]</a></sup> comparable to the land area of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>. Most of this lost forest was replaced by pasture for cattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIFOR2004_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIFOR2004-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deforestation of the Amazon rainforest continued to accelerate in the early 2000s, reaching an annual rate of 27,423 km² of forest loss in the year 2004. Today the remaining forest cover continues to dwindle, though the annual rate of forest loss has generally been slowing since 2004. However, rates of deforestation jumped again in 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>In Brazil, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_National_Institute_for_Space_Research" title="Brazilian National Institute for Space Research">Instituto Nacional de Pesquisas Espaciais</a> (INPE, or National Institute of Space Research) produces deforestation figures annually. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_Indigenous_peoples">Impact on Indigenous peoples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact on Indigenous peoples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Genocide_of_indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Genocide of indigenous peoples in Brazil">Genocide of indigenous peoples in Brazil</a></div>
<p>The native tribes of the Amazon have often been abused during the Amazon's deforestation. Loggers have killed natives, and encroached on to their land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Uncontacted_peoples" title="Uncontacted peoples">uncontacted peoples</a> have come out of the jungles to mingle with mainstream society after threats from outsiders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uncontacted_peoples" title="Uncontacted peoples">Uncontacted peoples</a> making <a href="/wiki/First_contact_(anthropology)" title="First contact (anthropology)">first contact</a> with outsiders are susceptible to diseases to which they have little immunity. Tribes can easily be decimated, the deaths resulting have been compared to a <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_the_Amazon_rainforest">Future of the Amazon rainforest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future of the Amazon rainforest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Using the 2005 deforestation rates, it was estimated that the Amazon rainforest would be reduced by 40% in two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The rate of deforestation is now slowing; rates of forest loss in 2012 were the slowest on record. However, the forest is still shrinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INPE.2FProdes_satelite_data_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-INPE.2FProdes_satelite_data-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norwegian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Jens_Stoltenberg" title="Jens Stoltenberg">Jens Stoltenberg</a> announced on September 16, 2008, that Norway's government would donate US $1 billion to the newly established Amazon fund. The money from this fund would go to projects aimed at slowing down the deforestation of the Amazon rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2015, Brazilian president <a href="/wiki/Dilma_Rousseff" title="Dilma Rousseff">Dilma Rousseff</a> told the United Nations that Brazil had effectively reduced the rate of deforestation in the Amazon by 82 percent. She also announced that over the next 15 years, Brazil aimed to eliminate illegal deforestation, restore and reforest 120,000 km<sup>2</sup> (46,000 sq mi), and recover 150,000 km<sup>2</sup> (58,000 sq mi) of degraded pastures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2017, Brazilian president <a href="/wiki/Michel_Temer" title="Michel Temer">Michel Temer</a> has abolished an Amazonian nature reserve the size of Denmark in Brazil's northern states of <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amap%C3%A1" title="Amapá">Amapá</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
