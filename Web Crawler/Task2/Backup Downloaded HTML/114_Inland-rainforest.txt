<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Inland rainforest</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>inland rainforest</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> also known as the <b>inland temperate rainforest</b> in the classification system of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Interior" title="British Columbia Interior">Central Interior of British Columbia</a>. It is part of the Interior Cedar Hemlock (ICH) zone of the <a href="/wiki/Biogeoclimatic_zones_of_British_Columbia" title="Biogeoclimatic zones of British Columbia">biogeoclimatic zones system developed by the BC Ministry of Forests</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Trench" title="Rocky Mountain Trench">Rocky Mountain Trench</a>. One of the richest parts of this wet belt lies 110 kilometres (68Â mi) east of the city of <a href="/wiki/Prince_George,_British_Columbia" title="Prince George, British Columbia">Prince George</a> and nearly a thousand kilometres (600 miles) east of the coastal rainforests. The oldest and most diverse parts of the forest are typically found on northeasterly aspect wet toe slopes, with <a href="/wiki/Thuja_plicata" title="Thuja plicata">Western Red Cedar (<i>Thuja plicata</i>)</a> trees over 1,000 years old and undisturbed forest stands much older than that. Some of these toe-slope benches were cleared in the 1960s to develop the <a href="/wiki/Yellowhead_Highway" title="Yellowhead Highway">Yellowhead Highway</a>, with the added result that most of the remainder became easily accessible to industrial logging and recreation, and more recently to research and interpretation. As a consequence there are only a handful of the best sites left undisturbed in 2008.</p>
<p>In 1987, a science-based conservation group <i>Save-The-Cedar League</i> was formed. With the establishment of the new <a href="/wiki/University_of_Northern_British_Columbia" title="University of Northern British Columbia">University of Northern British Columbia</a> (UNBC) in Prince George the 1990s, further research continued to highlight the significance of the ICH zone east of the city. The arrival of a defoliating insect outbreak, the Hemlock Looper, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lambdina_fiscellaria_fiscellaria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lambdina fiscellaria fiscellaria (page does not exist)">Lambdina fiscellaria fiscellaria</a></i>, resulted in increased salvage logging of old cedar stands along the Yellowhead Highway. This was reduced by the provinceâs chief forester in the early 2000s as the appreciation of non-timber values of the zone began to be realized and as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_Pine_Beetle" title="Mountain Pine Beetle">Mountain Pine Beetle</a> catastrophe spread across the <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plateau" title="Interior Plateau">Interior Plateau</a> to the west and large-scale salvage logging moved in that direction.</p>
<p>As the Mountain Pine Beetle wave passes, attention is returning to the mountain wet zones. Some of this came to a head in 2006 with the development of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth_forest" title="Old growth forest">old growth forest</a> trail by the nearby small community of <a href="/wiki/Dome_Creek,_British_Columbia" title="Dome Creek, British Columbia">Dome Creek</a> in the richest site yet found, a place that was also scheduled to be logged. The resulting socio-economic and ecological issues were investigated in a documentary film, <i>Block 486</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_effects">Climate change effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inland_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate change effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Interior Cedar Hemlock (ICH) zone is of particular significance with respect to climate change. Climatic warming projections suggest that by mid-century or soon afterwards, this location may be at the southerly extent of the ICH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The ICH zone will also have spread throughout much of the Prince George area and farther north. The <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Trench" title="Rocky Mountain Trench">Rocky Mountain Trench</a> east of Prince George therefore offers the last best chance to preserve some of the rare 'Tier-1' ICH sites.</p>
