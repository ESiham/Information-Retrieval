<p>The <b>Rainforest Alliance</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organization</a> (NGO) working to conserve <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and ensure sustainable livelihoods by transforming land-use practices, business practices and consumer behavior. Based in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> with offices throughout North and South America, Asia, Africa and Europe, it operates in more than 70 countries. It was founded in 1987 by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Katz_(environmental_activist)" title="Daniel Katz (environmental activist)">Daniel Katz</a>, who serves on its board of directors, and is led by President Nigel Sizer.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_forestry_certification">Sustainable forestry certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sustainable forestry certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rainforest Alliance launched the world’s first sustainable forestry certification program in 1989 to encourage market-driven and environmentally and socially responsible management of forests, tree farms, and forest resources. The organization helped to found the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC), the non-profit international body that manages the standard, in 1993. Through its certification arm, RA-Cert, the Rainforest Alliance is accredited to certify forestry operations that meet the FSC's standards. The Rainforest Alliance has certified more than 113 million acres (45.9 million hectares) of forest worldwide, as of 2016, making it the largest FSC certifier of forestlands in the world. The Rainforest Alliance's forest certification program was ranked "top of the class" according to "Wood Products Legality Verification Systems: An Assessment," an independent report compiled by <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>, a global environmental organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_offset_verification">Carbon offset verification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbon offset verification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organization verifies carbon offset projects to standards that address greenhouse gas sequestration, biodiversity conservation and sustainable livelihoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Rainforest Alliance verifies projects to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Carbon_Registry_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Carbon Registry Standard (page does not exist)">American Carbon Registry Standard</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_Fix_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbon Fix Standard (page does not exist)">Carbon Fix Standard</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Action_Reserve_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Climate Action Reserve Standard (page does not exist)">Climate Action Reserve Standard</a>, the <a href="/wiki/CDM_Gold_Standard" title="CDM Gold Standard">CDM Gold Standard</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Verified_Carbon_Standard" title="Verified Carbon Standard">Verified Carbon Standard</a>, the standards of the <a href="/wiki/Climate,_Community_%26_Biodiversity_Alliance" title="Climate, Community &amp; Biodiversity Alliance">Climate, Community &amp; Biodiversity Alliance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Climate_Exchange" title="Chicago Climate Exchange">Chicago Climate Exchange</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plan_Vivo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plan Vivo (page does not exist)">Plan Vivo</a>. As of 2015, 10,756,000 acres (4,352,800 hectares) have been protected by forest carbon projects that have been verified or validated by the Rainforest Alliance—removing or avoiding greenhouse gas emissions equivalent to those produced by 7.13 million cars in one year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_agriculture_certification">Sustainable agriculture certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sustainable agriculture certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rainforest Alliance's sustainable agriculture program includes training programs for farmers and the certification of small, medium and large farms that produce more than 100 different crops, including avocado, cattle, cinnamon, <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, palm oil, and potatoes, as well as <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, cocoa, and <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a>. In recent years, the Rainforest Alliance has greatly expanded its work with smallholders, who now account for 75% of the farms (more than 783,000 farmers in all) certified by the organization. To obtain certification, farms must meet the Sustainable Agriculture Network (SAN) standard,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which is designed to conserve ecosystems, protect biodiversity and waterways, conserve forests, reduce agrochemical use, and safeguard the well-being of workers and local communities. The Rainforest Alliance is a member of the Sustainable Agriculture Network (SAN), an international group works to promote and increase the use of sustainable agricultural practices and manage the certification program. The Rainforest Alliance encourages businesses and consumers to support sustainable agriculture by source or choose products grown on certified farms. More than 1.2 million farms and cooperatives across more than 42 countries—covering nearly 8.6 million acres (3.5 million hectares) of land—are being managed sustainably under Rainforest Alliance certification, as of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crop_standards_and_criteria">Crop standards and criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crop standards and criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The organization requires that 50% of criteria under a certain principle (group of criteria) be achieved, and 80% overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Several of these criteria are "critical" and must be complied with for a farm to earn certification. They include an ecosystem conservation program, protection of wild animals and waterways, the prohibition of discrimination in work and hiring practices, the prohibition of contracting children under the age of 15, the use of protective gear for workers, guidelines about agrochemical use and the prohibition of transgenic crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainforest_Alliance_Certified_Seal">Rainforest Alliance Certified Seal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rainforest Alliance Certified Seal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Rainforest Alliance Certified Seal appears only on products that meet the crop standards and criteria detailed above. According to <i>Consumer Reports</i>, "The Rainforest Alliance Certified label is clear and meaningful in support of sustainable agriculture, social responsibility and integrated pest management. The label is consistent in meaning among all certified. The label does not consist of farmers and none of the members are certified by the Rainforest Alliance. In this sense, the organizations behind these labels are independent from the products they certify."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In February 2008, <i>Ethical Corporation</i> called Rainforest Alliance certification a "rigorous, independently verified scheme".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, more than 4,300 companies buy or sell products from Rainforest Alliance Certified farms, and the Rainforest Alliance Certified seal can be seen in more than 120 countries. As of June 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 13.6 percent of the world’s cocoa, 5.4 percent of coffee and 15.1 percent of tea comes from Rainforest Alliance Certified farms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_tourism">Sustainable tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sustainable tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organization launched a sustainable tourism program in 2000 and provides small- and medium-sized tourism businesses in Latin America with training and tools to minimize their impacts on the environment and local communities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_response">Criticism and response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism and response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some academics, environmental groups, and media sources have criticized Rainforest Alliance agricultural certification, mostly with accusations of <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>. The <i>Manchester Evening News</i> notes that some critics have dubbed the Rainforest Alliance "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairtrade" title="Fairtrade">Fairtrade</a> lite",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manchester_Evening_News_2007_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manchester_Evening_News_2007-9">[9]</a></sup> offering companies such as <a href="/wiki/Chiquita_Brands_International" title="Chiquita Brands International">Chiquita</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft</a> a way to tap into the <a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumer market</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alex_Nicholls_(academic)" title="Alex Nicholls (academic)">Alex Nicholls</a>, professor of <a href="/wiki/Social_entrepreneurship" title="Social entrepreneurship">social entrepreneurship</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>, called Rainforest Alliance certification "a less expensive way for companies to answer consumers’ concerns about sustainability than to achieve Fair Trade certification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minimum_price_issues">Minimum price issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Minimum price issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rainforest Alliance sustainable agriculture certification, like the certification schemes <a href="/wiki/UTZ_Certified" title="UTZ Certified">UTZ Certified</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_certification" title="Organic certification">organic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> does not offer producers minimum or guaranteed price,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> therefore leaving them vulnerable to market price variations: as an example, in the 1980s, a pound of standard-grade coffee sold for around US $1.20. In 2003, a pound sold for about $0.50, which was not enough to cover the costs of production in much of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The price of coffee has since rebounded somewhat, with prices for <a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">arabica</a> reaching $1.18/pound by the end of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although many Rainforest Alliance Certified farms do in fact achieve price premiums for high-quality product, Rainforest Alliance focuses on improving the entire spectrum of farming practices. Third-party studies have shown the organization’s approach to be effective in raising both income and net revenue for farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a> professor of sociology Daniel Jaffee has criticized Rainforest Alliance certification, claiming that its standards are "arguably far lower than fair trade's" and saying "they establish minimum housing and sanitary conditions but do not stipulate a minimum price for coffee. Critically, they require plantation owners only to pay laborers the national minimum wage, a notoriously inadequate standard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaffee.2C_Daniel_2007_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jaffee.2C_Daniel_2007-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> favors the Rainforest Alliance's method and notes that "guaranteeing a minimum price [as Fairtrade does] means there is no incentive to improve quality." They also note that coffee drinkers say "the quality of Fairtrade brews varies widely. The Rainforest Alliance does things differently. It does not guarantee a minimum price or offer a premium but provides training advice. That consumers are often willing to pay more for a product with the [Rainforest Alliance] logo on it is an added bonus, not the result of a formal subsidy scheme; such products must still fend for themselves in the marketplace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_seal">Use of seal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainforest_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use of seal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organization certification has been criticized for allowing the use of the seal on products containing a minimum of 30% of certified content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-18">[18]</a></sup> According to Michael Conroy, former chairman of the board for Fair Trade USA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transfairusa1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-transfairusa1-19">[19]</a></sup> this use of the seal is the "most damaging dimension" of [Rainforest Alliance's] agricultural certification program and "a serious blow to the integrity of certification".</p>
