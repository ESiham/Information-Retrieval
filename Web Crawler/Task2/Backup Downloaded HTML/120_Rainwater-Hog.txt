<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rainwater Hog</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Rainwater HOG</b> is a modular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainwater" title="Rainwater">rainwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Storage_tank" title="Storage tank">storage tank</a> which can be used horizontally or vertically for <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">rainwater harvesting</a>, emergency water storage, and post-filter <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a> storage. Rainwater HOG was named a USA Top-10 Green Building Product in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and won a Spark Design Award<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and two additional sustainable product awards, a California Home &amp; Design award<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and a Green Log Award<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in 2009.</p>
<p>The Rainwater HOG was designed by the award-winning architectural innovator <a href="/wiki/Sally_Dominguez" title="Sally Dominguez">Sally Dominguez</a> for use in tight spaces and is sized to allow designers and engineers to use the stored water as effective thermal mass inside a structure.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_Hog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainwater HOG modules were used by David Gottfried, founder of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a>, to achieve the USA's first <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> Platinum Home Remodel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Gottfried used HOGs to store rainwater for flushing ultra-low-flush toilets, and additional HOGs to store filtered <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">Greywater</a> for use on the non-edible portion of the landscape.</p>
