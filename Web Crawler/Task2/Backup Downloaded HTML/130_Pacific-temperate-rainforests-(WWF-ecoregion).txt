<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pacific temperate rainforests (WWF ecoregion)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Pacific temperate rainforests ecoregion</b> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> is the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_rain_forest" title="Temperate rain forest">temperate rain forest</a> ecoregion on the planet as defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> (other definitions exist). The Pacific temperate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forests</a> lie along the western side of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Ranges" title="Pacific Coast Ranges">Pacific Coast Ranges</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> Coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Prince_William_Sound" title="Prince William Sound">Prince William Sound</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> through the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Coast" title="British Columbia Coast">British Columbia Coast</a> to <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a>, and are part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nearctic_ecozone" title="Nearctic ecozone">Nearctic ecozone</a>, as also defined by the World Wildlife Fund. The Pacific temperate rain forests are characterized by a high amount of rainfall, in some areas more than 300 cm (10 ft) per year and moderate temperatures in both the summer and winter months (10–24 °C or 50–75 °F).</p>
<p>This ecoregion is a subregion of the <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_(bioregion)" title="Cascadia (bioregion)">Cascadia bioregion</a>.</p>
<p>These rain forests occur in a number of <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a>, which vary in their species composition, but are predominantly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>, sometimes with an understory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadleaf" title="Broadleaf">broadleaf</a> trees, <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>. In the WWF's system, sub-ecoregions of the Pacific Temperate Rain Forests Ecoregion are the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Pacific_coastal_forests_(WWF_ecoregion)" title="Northern Pacific coastal forests (WWF ecoregion)">Northern Pacific coastal forests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Mainland_Coastal_Forests_ecoregion" title="British Columbia Mainland Coastal Forests ecoregion">British Columbia mainland coastal forests</a>, Central Pacific coastal forests, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_and_Southern_Cascades_forests_(WWF_ecoregion)" title="Central and Southern Cascades forests (WWF ecoregion)">Central and Southern Cascades forests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klamath-Siskiyou_forests_(WWF_ecoregion)" title="Klamath-Siskiyou forests (WWF ecoregion)">Klamath-Siskiyou forests</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_California_coastal_forests_(WWF_ecoregion)" title="Northern California coastal forests (WWF ecoregion)">Northern California coastal forests ecoregions</a>.</p>
<p>The forests in the north contain predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sitka_spruce" title="Sitka spruce">Sitka spruce</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemlock" title="Western hemlock">western hemlock</a>, while those in the coastal forests are home as well to <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">coast redwood</a> (<i>Sequoia sempervirens</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Douglas-fir" title="Coast Douglas-fir">coast Douglas-fir</a> (<i>Pseudotsuga menziesii</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_redcedar" title="Western redcedar">western redcedar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shore_pine" title="Shore pine">shore pine</a>. Notably, the three tallest species of trees are found here. Dense growths of <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a> cover the trees, and lush vegetation is present everywhere.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hardwood" title="Hardwood">Hardwood</a> trees such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bigleaf_maple" title="Bigleaf maple">bigleaf maple</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a> are also common, especially at lower elevations and along stream banks, and are vital to the ecosystem, in part because of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixing" title="Nitrogen fixing">nitrogen fixing</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_temperate_rainforests_(WWF_ecoregion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg/220px-HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg/330px-HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg/440px-HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HohRiverTrail_7322.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dense growths of epiphytes and mosses cover the trees, and the understory vegetation is lush.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>About 200 million years ago (during the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> periods), the landscape was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>, which were the most diverse group of trees and constituted the greatest majority of large trees. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperms" title="Angiosperms">flowering plants</a> emerged (in the following <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period), they quickly prevailed, causing most conifers to become extinct, and those that survived to adapt to harsh conditions. Perhaps the most significant difference in this change is that the primitive conifers invested their energy in the basic food supply for every seed, with no certainty of fertilization; by contrast, flowering plants create the food supply for a seed only after it is triggered by fertilization. The Pacific temperate rain forest now remains the only region on Earth of noteworthy size and significance where, due to unique climatic conditions, the conifers flourish as they did before being displaced by flowering plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-1">[1]</a></sup> The northern Pacific temperate rain forests are relatively young, emerging in the past few thousand years following the retreat of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_temperate_rainforests_(WWF_ecoregion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ecosystem of Pacific temperate rain forests is so productive that the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> on the best sites is at least four times greater than that of any comparable area in the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-1">[1]</a></sup> In sheer mass of living and decaying material - trees, mosses, shrubs, and soil - these forests are more massive than any other ecosystem on the planet. In part, this is due to the rarity of fire. Unlike drier forests, which burn periodically, temperate rain forests are naturally subject to only small-scale disturbances, such as blow-downs and avalanches.</p>
<p>This rain forest spans a wide range of latitude - from about <a href="/wiki/40th_parallel_north" title="40th parallel north">40 degrees north</a> to about <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_north" title="60th parallel north">60 degrees north</a>. The differences in climate from south to north create several major forest zones, characterized by different species.</p>
<ul>
<li>At the southern limit in northern California is the "coast redwood zone".</li>
<li>Beginning at the California/Oregon border, and extending through the north end of Vancouver Island is the "seasonal rain forest zone". The major tree species here are <a href="/wiki/Douglas_fir" title="Douglas fir">Douglas fir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_red_cedar" title="Western red cedar">western red cedar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sitka_spruce" title="Sitka spruce">Sitka spruce</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemlock" title="Western hemlock">western hemlock</a>.</li>
<li>Beyond the northern end of Vancouver Island, is the "perhumid rain forest zone". Douglas fir wanes as a dominant species, and the forest is primarily made up of western red cedar, Sitka spruce, and western hemlock.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a> begins where the fjords of southeast Alaska end, and marks the transition into "sub-polar rain forest". Here the forest occupies only a very narrow strip between the ocean and the icy alpine zone. The cedar trees no longer thrive in this harsher climate, and the dominant trees are limited to Sitka spruce, and western and mountain hemlock.</li>
<li>The northern limits of the rain forest are scattered in thin bands in the northern <a href="/wiki/Prince_William_Sound" title="Prince William Sound">Prince William Sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenai_Fjords_National_Park" title="Kenai Fjords National Park">Kenai Fjords</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Kodiak_Island" title="Kodiak Island">Kodiak Island</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Cook_Inlet" title="Cook Inlet">Cook Inlet</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife">Wildlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_temperate_rainforests_(WWF_ecoregion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first survey to systematically explore the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">forest canopy</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmanah_Valley" title="Carmanah Valley">Carmanah Valley</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> yielded 15,000 new species, a third of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a> known to exist in all of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Among the collection were 500 species previously unknown to science.</p>
<p>The rain forest exists in a complicated landscape of islands and fjords, and many species depend on both the forest and the ocean. <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">Salmon</a> are one of the primary species of the rainforest, spawning in the forest streams. The <a href="/wiki/Marbled_murrelet" title="Marbled murrelet">marbled murrelet</a> nests in old growth trees at night, but feeds in the ocean during the day.</p>
<p>Many of the most iconic photos of these forests include a large bear somewhere in the frame. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grizzly_bears" title="Grizzly bears">Grizzly bears</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bears</a> once thrived throughout the rain forest zone and beyond. Black bears can still be found throughout the forest's range, while grizzlies are largely confined to areas north of the Canada–US border. These forests have some of the largest concentrations of grizzly bears in the world, mainly due to the region's rich salmon streams. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Bear_Rainforest" title="Great Bear Rainforest">Great Bear Rainforest</a> in Canada is home to the rare white variant of the black bear known as the "<a href="/wiki/Kermode_bear" title="Kermode bear">spirit bear</a>." The endangered <a href="/wiki/Spotted_owl" title="Spotted owl">spotted owl</a> was at the center of logging controversies in Oregon and Washington. Other wildlife species of note include the <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sitka_deer" title="Sitka deer">sitka deer</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Logging">Logging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_temperate_rainforests_(WWF_ecoregion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Logging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pacific temperate rain forests have been subject to ongoing large-scale industrial <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> since the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, cutting over half of their total area. In California, only 4% of the redwoods have been protected. In <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, less than 10% of the original coastal rain forest area remains.</p>
<p>An even larger percentage of the productive forest has been logged. Much of the land is rock, ice, muskeg, or less productive forest on steep slopes. The stereotypical old growth is limited to lowland flats and valleys, which have been preferentially targeted for logging. Historically, the most common protocol has been to place protected areas in the mountains, leaving the valleys to the timber industry. So while some very large areas are protected as parks and monuments, very little of the highest-value habitat has been protected, and much of it has already been cut.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Tongass_National_Forest" title="Tongass National Forest">Tongass National Forest</a>, in the 1950s, in part to aid in Japanese recovery from World War II, the US Forest Service set up long term contracts with two pulp mills: the <a href="/wiki/Ketchikan_Pulp_Company" title="Ketchikan Pulp Company">Ketchikan Pulp Company</a> (KPC) and the Alaska Pulp Company (APC). These contracts were for 50 years, and divided up the forest into areas slated for APC logs and areas slated for KPC logs. These two companies conspired to drive log prices down, conspired to drive smaller logging operations out of business, and were major and recalcitrant polluters of their local areas. These long term contracts guaranteed low prices to the pulp companies — in some cases resulting in trees being given away for less than the price of a hamburger. Since 1980, the US Forest Service has lost over a billion dollars in Tongass timber sales.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Half a million acres (2,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of the Tongass was selected by native corporations under the 1971 <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Native_Claims_Settlement_Act" title="Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act">Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act</a>. Much of this area has been clearcut.</p>
<p>The most controversial timber sales in the Tongass are in the roadless areas. In September 2006, a landmark court decision overturned President George W. Bush's repeal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roadless_rule" title="Roadless rule">Roadless Rule</a>, reverting to the 2001 roadless area protections established under President Clinton. (The Clinton Administration had declined to adopt the rule during its eight years, waiting until after the 2000 elections and until only days were remaining in the administration, then adopting the rule both to improve environmental protection.) However, the Tongass was exempted from that ruling and it is unclear what the fate of its vast roadless areas will be.</p>
