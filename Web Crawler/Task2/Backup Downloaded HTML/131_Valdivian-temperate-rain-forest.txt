<p>The <b>Valdivian temperate forests</b> (NT0404) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregion" title="Terrestrial ecoregion">ecoregion</a> on the west coast of southern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and extending into <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Neotropical_realm" title="Neotropical realm">Neotropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Biogeographic_realm" title="Biogeographic realm">ecozone</a>. The forests are named after the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivia,_Chile" title="Valdivia, Chile">Valdivia</a>. The Valdivian temperate rainforests are characterized by their dense <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">understories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chusquea" title="Chusquea">bamboos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, and for being mostly dominated by <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">angiosperm</a> trees with some deciduous specimens, though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a> trees are also common.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valdivian_temperate_rain_forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temperate rain forests comprise a relatively narrow coastal strip between the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to the west, and the southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andes_Mountains" title="Andes Mountains">Andes Mountains</a> to the east, from roughly 37° to 48° south latitude. North of 42°, the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Coast_Range" title="Chilean Coast Range">Chilean coastal range</a> runs along the coast, and the north-south running <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Central_Valley" title="Chilean Central Valley">Chilean Central Valley</a> lies between the coastal range and the Andes. South of 42°, the coast range continues as a chain of offshore islands, including <a href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Island" title="Chiloé Island">Chiloé Island</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chonos_Archipelago" title="Chonos Archipelago">Chonos Archipelago</a>, while the "Central Valley" is submerged and continues as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Corcovado" title="Gulf of Corcovado">Gulf of Corcovado</a>. Much of the ecoregion was covered by the <a href="/wiki/Patagonian_Ice_Sheet" title="Patagonian Ice Sheet">Patagonian Ice Sheet</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> at the peak of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, which descended from the Andes mountains, and the numerous lakes of the Chilean lakes district in the central part of the ecoregion were originally glacial valleys, while the southern part of the region has many glacier-carved <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a>.</p>

<p>To the north the Valdivian forests give way to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_forests,_woodlands,_and_scrub" title="Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub">Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Matorral" title="Chilean Matorral">Chilean Matorral</a> ecoregion. Some few Valdivian forests grows in northern Chile such as that one in <a href="/wiki/Bosque_de_Fray_Jorge_National_Park" title="Bosque de Fray Jorge National Park">Bosque de Fray Jorge National Park</a> as remains of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a>. To the south lies the <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_subpolar_forests" title="Magellanic subpolar forests">Magellanic subpolar forests</a> ecoregion. The temperate Valdivian, Matorral, and Magellanic ecoregions are isolated from the subtropical and tropical forests of northern South America by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atacama" title="Atacama">Atacama</a> desert north of the Matorral, the Andes mountains, and dry rain-shadow Argentine grasslands east of the Andes. As a result, the temperate forest regions have evolved in relative isolation, with a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> species.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Llanquihue_glaciation" title="Llanquihue glaciation">Llanquihue glaciation</a> much of the area west of <a href="/wiki/Llanquihue_Lake" title="Llanquihue Lake">Llanquihue Lake</a> remained ice-free during the Last Glacial Maximum and had sparsely distributed vegetation dominated by <i><a href="/wiki/Nothofagus" title="Nothofagus">Nothofagus</a></i> species. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivian_temperate_rainforest" title="Valdivian temperate rainforest">Valdivian temperate rainforest</a> proper was reduced to scattered remnants in the western side of the Andes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valdivian_temperate_rain_forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the forest is located at around 40 degrees south, it is strongly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>. The water vapour held by the westerlies condenses as they arrive at the higher part of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">windward slope</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, thus creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_rainfall" title="Orographic rainfall">rainfalls</a>. At the same time, the northward-flowing oceanic Humboldt Current creates humid and foggy conditions near the coast. The tree line is at about 2,400 m in the northern part of the ecoregion (35° S), and descends to 1,000 m in the south of the Valdivian region. In the summer the temperature can climb to 16.5 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> (62 °F), while during winter the temperature can drop below 7 °C (45 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Valdivian temperate rain forests are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_broadleaf_and_mixed_forests" title="Temperate broadleaf and mixed forests">temperate broadleaf and mixed forests</a>. The Valdivian and Magellanic temperate rainforests are the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_rain_forest" title="Temperate rain forest">temperate rain forests</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and one of a small number of temperate rain forests in the world. Together they are the second largest in the world, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_temperate_rain_forests" title="Pacific temperate rain forests">Pacific temperate rain forests</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (which stretches from Alaska to northern California). The Valdivian forests are a refuge for the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_flora" title="Antarctic flora">Antarctic flora</a>, and share many plant families with the temperate rainforests of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Fully half the species of woody plants are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to this ecoregion.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Chusquea_quila" title="Chusquea quila">Chusquea quila</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> that grows in humid areas below 500 m, where <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chusquea_coleou" title="Chusquea coleou">Chusquea coleou</a></i> becomes more dominant above. <i>Chusquea quila</i> can form pure stands called <i>quilantales</i>. Very few plants can grow under this species. Other notable species are the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nalca" title="Nalca">nalca</a></i> or Chilean rhubarb (<i>Gunnera tinctoria</i>) and the ferns <i><a href="/wiki/Lophosoria_quadripinnata" title="Lophosoria quadripinnata">Lophosoria quadripinnata</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Blechnum_chilense" title="Blechnum chilense">Blechnum chilense</a></i>. Chile's national flower, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copihue" title="Copihue">copihue</a></i> (<i>Lapageria rosea</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_species" title="Pioneer species">pioneer species</a> that grows in disturbed areas of the Valdivian rain forest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forests_ecosystems">Forests ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valdivian_temperate_rain_forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forests ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alerce_chileno.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alerce_chileno.JPG/220px-Alerce_chileno.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alerce_chileno.JPG/330px-Alerce_chileno.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Alerce_chileno.JPG/440px-Alerce_chileno.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alerce_chileno.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fitzroya_cupressoides" title="Fitzroya cupressoides">Alerce</a></i></div>

<p>There are four main types of forest ecosystems in the Valdivian ecoregion. At the northern end of the ecoregion are deciduous forests, dominated by two deciduous species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_beech" title="Southern beech">southern beech</a>: <i>rauli</i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nothofagus_alpina" title="Nothofagus alpina">Nothofagus alpina</a></i>) and <i>roble</i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nothofagus_obliqua" title="Nothofagus obliqua">N. obliqua</a></i>); this is a transitional zone to the Mediterranean-climate forests to the north.</p>
<p>The second type are the Valdivian <a href="/wiki/Laurel_forest" title="Laurel forest">laurel-leaved forests</a>, characterized by a variety of broadleaf evergreen trees, including <i><a href="/wiki/Laureliopsis" title="Laureliopsis">Laureliopsis philippiana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aextoxicon" title="Aextoxicon">Aextoxicon punctatum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucryphia_cordifolia" title="Eucryphia cordifolia">Eucryphia cordifolia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Caldcluvia_paniculata" title="Caldcluvia paniculata">Caldcluvia paniculata</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Weinmannia_trichosperma" title="Weinmannia trichosperma">Weinmannia trichosperma</a></i>, with an understory of <i><a href="/wiki/Myrceugenia" title="Myrceugenia">Myrceugenia</a> planipes</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Luma_apiculata" title="Luma apiculata">arrayán</a></i> (<i>Luma apiculata</i>) and other plants.</p>
<p>The third forest type is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patagonian_Andean_forest" title="Patagonian Andean forest">Patagonian Andean forests</a>, which are distributed at higher elevations along the Andes mountain front, and are dominated by evergreen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria_araucana" title="Araucaria araucana">pehuén</a></i> or monkey-puzzle (<i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria_araucana" title="Araucaria araucana">Araucaria araucana</a></i>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Fitzroya" title="Fitzroya">alerce</a></i> (<i>Fitzroya cupressoides</i>). The <i>alerce</i> looks like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_sequoia" title="Giant sequoia">giant sequoia</a>, and is a rival in longevity to the <a href="/wiki/Bristlecone_pine" title="Bristlecone pine">bristlecone pine</a>, some with growth rings recording 3,625 years of local weather cycles. Closer to the treeline, the conifers give way to Andean scrublands of deciduous <i><a href="/wiki/Nothofagus" title="Nothofagus">Nothofagus</a></i>.</p>
<p>The fourth and last type is the Northern Patagonian forests, which dominate the southern half of the ecoregion, with evergreen species such as the broadleaf <i><a href="/wiki/Nothofagus_dombeyi" title="Nothofagus dombeyi">Nothofagus dombeyi</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Drimys_winteri" title="Drimys winteri">Drimys winteri</a></i> and the coniferous <a href="/wiki/Podocarpaceae" title="Podocarpaceae">podocarps</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Podocarpus_nubigenus" title="Podocarpus nubigenus">Podocarpus nubigenus</a></i>.</p>

<p>Some of the threatened mammals of the Valdivian forests include the <i><a href="/wiki/Monito_del_monte" title="Monito del monte">monito del monte</a></i> <i>(Dromiciops gliroides)</i>, an arboreal <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pud%C3%BA" title="Pudú">southern <i>pudú</i></a> <i>(Pudu pudu)</i> the world's smallest <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kodkod" title="Kodkod">kodkod</a> <i>(Leopardus guigna)</i>, South America's smallest <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a>. Since the beginning of the 20th century, there have also been nonnative <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boars</a> living in the Valdivian forests. Hummingbirds are common on Valdivian forests because of the presence of plants like the <i><a href="/wiki/Aristotelia_chilensis" title="Aristotelia chilensis">maqui</a></i> (<i>Aristotelia chilensis</i>) and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copihue" title="Copihue">copihue</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valdivian_temperate_rain_forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sanpablotregua.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="656" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/220px-Sanpablotregua.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/330px-Sanpablotregua.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/440px-Sanpablotregua.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Native_Forest_Law" title="Native Forest Law">Native Forest Law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism_in_the_Valdivian_Temperate_Rainforest" title="Ecotourism in the Valdivian Temperate Rainforest">Ecotourism in the Valdivian Temperate Rainforest</a></div>
<p>The Valdivian forests include stands of huge trees, especially <i>Nothofagus</i> and <i>Fitzroya</i>, which can live to a great age. These magnificent rainforests are endangered by extensive logging and their replacement by fast-growing <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a>, which are more sought-after by the pulp and paper industry. The native trees that are cleared to make way for these monocultures are often exported as <a href="/wiki/Woodchips" title="Woodchips">woodchips</a> to Japan. A start at conservation was made in November 2003 when a consortium of conservation groups, both local and international, bought at auction of a bankrupt logging firm 147,500 acres (600 km²) of biologically rich rainforest in the Valdivian Coastal Range. Gianni Lopez, Executive Director of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CONAMA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CONAMA (page does not exist)">CONAMA</a>, Chile’s national environmental agency remarked, "Ten years ago the existence of protected areas not owned by the government was unthinkable." Among the efforts supporting conservation, has been a growing <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism_in_the_Valdivian_Temperate_Rainforest" title="Ecotourism in the Valdivian Temperate Rainforest">ecotourism industry</a>.</p>
