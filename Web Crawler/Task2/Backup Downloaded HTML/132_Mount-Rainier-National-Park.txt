<p><b>Mount Rainier National Park</b> is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_the_United_States" title="List of national parks of the United States">United States National Park</a> located in southeast <a href="/wiki/Pierce_County,_Washington" title="Pierce County, Washington">Pierce County</a> and northeast <a href="/wiki/Lewis_County,_Washington" title="Lewis County, Washington">Lewis County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StanleyMaps2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-StanleyMaps2000-3">[3]</a></sup> It was established on March 2, 1899 as the fifth national park in the United States. The park encompasses 236,381 acres (369.35 sq mi; 956.60 km<sup>2</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-area_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-area-1">[1]</a></sup> including all of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier" title="Mount Rainier">Mount Rainier</a>, a 14,411-foot (4,392 m) <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The mountain rises abruptly from the surrounding land with elevations in the park ranging from 1,600 feet to over 14,000 feet (490 - 4,300 m). The highest point in the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a>, around it are valleys, waterfalls, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_ecology#Subalpine_zone" title="Montane ecology">subalpine</a> <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">old-growth forest</a> and more than 25 <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>. The volcano is often shrouded in clouds that dump enormous amounts of rain and snow on the peak every year.</p>
<p>Mount Rainier is circled by the <a href="/wiki/Wonderland_Trail" title="Wonderland Trail">Wonderland Trail</a> and is covered by several glaciers and snowfields totaling some 35 square miles (91 km<sup>2</sup>). <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Glacier" title="Carbon Glacier">Carbon Glacier</a> is the largest glacier by volume in the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Emmons_Glacier" title="Emmons Glacier">Emmons Glacier</a> is the largest glacier by area. Mount Rainier is a popular peak for <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineering</a> with some 10,000 attempts per year with approximately 50% making it to the summit.</p>
<p>The park contains outstanding subalpine meadows and 91,000 acres (37,000 ha) of old growth forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1993OldGrowthEstimates_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-1993OldGrowthEstimates-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ninety-seven percent of the park is preserved as <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> under the <a href="/wiki/National_Wilderness_Preservation_System" title="National Wilderness Preservation System">National Wilderness Preservation System</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Clearwater_Wilderness" title="Clearwater Wilderness">Clearwater Wilderness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier_Wilderness" title="Mount Rainier Wilderness">Mount Rainier Wilderness</a>, a designation it received in 1988. It is abutted by the <a href="/wiki/Tatoosh_Wilderness" title="Tatoosh Wilderness">Tatoosh Wilderness</a>. The park was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> on February 18, 1997 as a showcase for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Park_Service_Rustic" title="National Park Service Rustic">National Park Service Rustic</a> style architecture (or <i>parkitecture</i>) of the 1920s and 1930s, exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Inn_(Washington)" title="Paradise Inn (Washington)">Paradise Inn</a> and a masterpiece of early NPS master planning. As an Historic Landmark district, the park was administratively listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhlsum_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/Track_of_the_Cat" title="Track of the Cat">Track of the Cat</a></i> (1954) was filmed in and around Mount Rainer National Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_Americans">Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest evidence of human activity in the area which is now Mount Rainier National Park, a projectile point dated to circa 4,000-5,800 BP (<a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">before present</a>) found along Bench Lake Trail (the first section of <a href="/wiki/Snow_Lake_(Mount_Rainier)" title="Snow Lake (Mount Rainier)">Snow Lake</a> Trail).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsAdminHistoryI_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsAdminHistoryI-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsBurtchard4a_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsBurtchard4a-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more substantial archeological find was a rock shelter near Fryingpan Creek, east of Goat Island Mountain. Hunting artifacts were found in the shelter. The shelter would not have been used all year round. Cultural affinities suggest the site was used by <a href="/wiki/Confederated_Tribes_of_the_Umatilla_Indian_Reservation" title="Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation">Columbia Plateau Tribes</a> from 1000 to 300 BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsAdminHistoryI_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NpsAdminHistoryI-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsBurtchard4a_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NpsBurtchard4a-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963 the National Park Service contracted the <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a> to study <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> use of the Mount Rainier area. Richard D. Daugherty lead an archeological study of the area and concluded that prehistoric humans used the area most heavily between 8000 and 4500 BP. Allan H. Smith interviewed elderly Native Americans and studied ethnographic literature. He found no evidence of permanent habitation in the park area. The park was used for <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunting and gathering</a> and for occasional <a href="/wiki/Vision_quest" title="Vision quest">spirit quests</a>. Smith also came to tentative conclusions that the park was divided among five tribes along watershed boundaries; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nisqually_(tribe)" title="Nisqually (tribe)">Nisqually</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puyallup_(tribe)" title="Puyallup (tribe)">Puyallup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muckleshoot" title="Muckleshoot">Muckleshoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yakama" title="Yakama">Yakama</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taidnapam" title="Taidnapam">Taidnapam</a> (Upper Cowlitz). Subsequent studies cast doubt on Smith's theory that the tribes had agreed upon boundaries before they entered into treaties with the United States in 1854-55.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsAdminHistoryI_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-NpsAdminHistoryI-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mammals that inhabit this national park are especially the <a href="/wiki/North_American_cougar" title="North American cougar">cougar</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobcat" title="Bobcat">bobcat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe_hare" title="Snowshoe hare">snowshoe hare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">mole</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_American_beaver" title="North American beaver">beaver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_American_porcupine" title="North American porcupine">porcupine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marmot" title="Marmot">marmot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marten" title="Marten">marten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_pika" title="American pika">pika</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mountain_goat" title="Mountain goat">mountain goat</a>. The common birds of this park including raptors are the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickadee" title="Chickadee">chickadee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kinglet" title="Kinglet">kinglet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willow_flycatcher" title="Willow flycatcher">willow flycatcher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_spotted_owl" title="Northern spotted owl">spotted owl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_jay" title="Steller's jay">steller's jay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clark%27s_nutcracker" title="Clark's nutcracker">Clark's nutcracker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagopus" title="Lagopus">ptarmigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harlequin_duck" title="Harlequin duck">harlequin duck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_jay" title="Gray jay">gray jay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grosbeak" title="Grosbeak">grosbeak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Park_creation">Park creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Park creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:350px; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The Mount Rainier Forest Preserve should be made a national park and guarded while yet its bloom is on; for if in the making of the West Nature had what we call parks in mind—places for rest, inspiration, and prayers—this Rainier region must surely be one of them.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duncan_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-duncan-10">[10]</a></sup></cite></div>

<p>On March 2, 1899, President <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a> signed a bill passed by Congress authorizing the creation of Mount Rainier National Park, the nation's fifth national park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was the first national park created from a <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" title="United States National Forest">national forest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duncan_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-duncan-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Forest_Reserve" title="Pacific Forest Reserve">Pacific Forest Reserve</a> had been created in 1893 and included Mount Rainier. It was enlarged in 1897 and renamed <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier_Forest_Reserve" title="Mount Rainier Forest Reserve">Mount Rainier Forest Reserve</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> had visited Mount Rainier in 1888. Muir and nine others, including <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sturgis_Ingraham" title="Edward Sturgis Ingraham">Edward Sturgis Ingraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Piper" title="Charles Piper">Charles Piper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/P._B._Van_Trump" title="P. B. Van Trump">P. B. Van Trump</a>, climbed to the summit in what became the fifth recorded ascent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The trip to Mount Rainier had played a role in reinvigorating Muir and convincing him to rededicate his life to the preservation of nature as national parks. At the time national forests, called forest reserves at first, were being created throughout the American West, under the utilitarian "conservation-through-use" view of <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>. Muir came to be known as a "preservationist". He wanted nature preserved under the more protected status of national parks. But during the 1890s there was more public support for creating national forests than national parks. During that decade, Muir and his supporters were only able to protect one national forest<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2017)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> as a national park. When the Pacific Forest Reserve was created in 1893, Muir quickly persuaded the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> to support a movement to protect Rainier as a national park. Other groups soon joined, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> and scientific associations wanting Mount Rainier preserved as a place to study <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>. Commercial leaders in <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> were also in support, as was the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Pacific_Railway" title="Northern Pacific Railway">Northern Pacific Railway</a>. The effort lasted over five years and involved six different attempts to push a bill through Congress. Congress eventually agreed, but only after acquiring assurances that none of the new park was suitable for farming or mining and that no federal appropriations would be necessary for its management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duncan_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-duncan-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_flooding">2006 flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2006 flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mount Rainier National Park closed because of extensive flooding as a result of the 6 November 2006 <a href="/wiki/Pineapple_Express" title="Pineapple Express">Pineapple Express</a> rainstorm when 18 inches (460 mm) of rain fell in a 36-hour period. Campsites and roads throughout the park were washed away. Power to Paradise and Longmire was disrupted. Sunshine Point Campground, shortly inside the Nisqually Entrance, was destroyed and has not reopened. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-parknews-11-06_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nps-parknews-11-06-14">[14]</a></sup> On 5 May 2007, the park reopened to automobile traffic via <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Route_706" title="Washington State Route 706">State Route 706</a> at the Nisqually Entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeattlePI07-05-05_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SeattlePI07-05-05-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Paradise</b> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;params=46.79_N_121.74_W_type:landmark&amp;title=Paradise"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°47′N</span> <span class="longitude">121°44′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.79°N 121.74°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.79; -121.74</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Paradise</span>)</span></span></span></a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSRainierEastMap_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSRainierEastMap-17">[17]</a></sup>) is the name of an area at approximately 5,400 feet (1,600 m) on the south slope of Mount Rainier in the national park. Paradise is the most popular destination for visitors to Mount Rainier National Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitcher_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitcher-18">[18]</a></sup> 62% of the over 1.3 million people who visited the park in 2000 went to Paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsVisitorStudy_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsVisitorStudy-19">[19]</a></sup> Paradise, near the subalpine valley of the <a href="/wiki/Paradise_River" title="Paradise River">Paradise River</a>, is the location of the historic <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Inn_(Washington)" title="Paradise Inn (Washington)">Paradise Inn</a> (built 1916),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paradise_Guide_House&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paradise Guide House (page does not exist)">Paradise Guide House</a> (built 1920) and <a href="/wiki/Henry_M._Jackson_Visitor_Center" title="Henry M. Jackson Visitor Center">Henry M. Jackson Visitor Center</a> (built 1966;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsCentennial_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsCentennial-21">[21]</a></sup> demolished and replaced, 2008). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewLodge_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewLodge-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> says that "Paradise is the snowiest place on Earth where snowfall is measured regularly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsFaq_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsFaq-23">[23]</a></sup> 1,122 inches (28.5 m) of snow fell during the winter of 1971/72, setting a world record for that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsFaq_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-NpsFaq-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsSnowfall_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NpsSnowfall-24">[24]</a></sup> Subsequently, in the winter of 1998/99, Mount Baker Ski Area received 1,140 inches (29 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_report_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_report-25">[25]</a></sup> Paradise holds the Cascade Range record for most snow on the ground with 367 inches (9.32 m) on 10 March 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andalkar_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andalkar-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>, the park has a <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subarctic climate</a> (<i>Dfc</i>) with a drying trend in the summer.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Longmire">Longmire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Longmire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Longmire,_Washington" title="Longmire, Washington">Longmire, Washington</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Longmire_Administration_Building_(2015).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4500" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg/220px-Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg/330px-Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg/440px-Longmire_Administration_Building_%282015%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Longmire_Administration_Building_(2015).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
National Park Service 1928 Administration Building at Longmire</div>
</div>

<p><b>Longmire</b> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;params=46.75_N_121.81_W_type:landmark&amp;title=Longmire+Visitors+Centre"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°45′N</span> <span class="longitude">121°49′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.75°N 121.81°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.75; -121.81</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Longmire Visitors Centre</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Visitor_center" title="Visitor center">visitor center</a> in Mount Rainier National Park, located 6.5 miles (10.5 km) east of the Nisqually Entrance. The area is in the <a href="/wiki/Nisqually_River" title="Nisqually River">Nisqually River</a> valley at an elevation of 2,761 feet (842 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filley_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filley-29">[29]</a></sup> between The Ramparts Ridge and the <a href="/wiki/Tatoosh_Range" title="Tatoosh Range">Tatoosh Range</a>. Longmire is surrounded by old-growth <a href="/wiki/Douglas_fir" title="Douglas fir">Douglas fir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_red_cedar" title="Western red cedar">western red cedar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemlock" title="Western hemlock">western hemlock</a>.</p>
<p>Longmire is the location of Mount Rainier's National Park Inn, the Longmire Museum, and the 1928 National Park Service Administration Building, which is now a Wilderness Information Center. The National Park Inn is the only accommodation in the park open all year round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Longmire is the second most popular destination for visitors to Mount Rainier National Park after Paradise. Of the more than 1.3 million people who visited the park in 2000, 38% visited Longmire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NpsVisitorStudy_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NpsVisitorStudy-19">[19]</a></sup> The Cougar Rock <a href="/wiki/Campsite" title="Campsite">Campground</a> is about 2 miles (3.2 km) north west of Longmire <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StanleyMaps2000_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-StanleyMaps2000-3">[3]</a></sup> with 173 individual campsites and 5 group sites and open from late May through late September. Longmire is one of the starting points of the <a href="/wiki/Wonderland_Trail" title="Wonderland Trail">Wonderland Trail</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunrise">Sunrise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sunrise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunrise,_Washington" title="Sunrise, Washington">Sunrise, Washington</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4899" data-file-width="7349" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG/220px-Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG/330px-Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG/440px-Mount_Rainier_6909h.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><b>Sunrise</b> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mount_Rainier_National_Park&amp;params=46.91_N_121.64_W_type:landmark&amp;title=Sunrise+Visitors+Centre"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">46°55′N</span> <span class="longitude">121°38′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">46.91°N 121.64°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">46.91; -121.64</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Sunrise Visitors Centre</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>) is a lodge and <a href="/wiki/Visitor_center" title="Visitor center">visitor center</a> located in the northeastern part of the park. At an elevation of 6,400 feet (1,950 m), it is the highest point in the park that is accessible by vehicle. There are <a href="/wiki/Wonderland_Trail" title="Wonderland Trail">miles of trails</a> located all around Sunrise, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Fremont&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Fremont (page does not exist)">Mount Fremont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burroughs_Mountain" title="Burroughs Mountain">Burroughs Mountain</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sourdough_Ridge" title="Sourdough Ridge">Sourdough Ridge</a>. The lodge is reachable via a 10-mile (16 km) turnoff from <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Route_410" title="Washington State Route 410">SR 410</a> near the <a href="/wiki/White_River_(Washington)" title="White River (Washington)">White River</a> entrance.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Christine_Falls" title="Christine Falls">Christine Falls</a>, one of many waterfalls visible from a short walk from the main road.</div>

<p><i>Ohanapecosh</i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɔː/: 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a campground (with 188 individual sites and 2 group sites, open from late May through late September), visitor center (closed during the 2013 season), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Park_Ranger" title="National Park Ranger">ranger</a> station located in the southeastern portion of the park, approximately 3 miles (4.8 km) from the park boundary off <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Route_123" title="Washington State Route 123">Highway 123</a>. Located in a deep valley among old growth forest at an elevation below 2,000 feet (610 m), it is the only developed area of the park without a view of Mount Rainier. The Ohanapecosh Hot Springs, Grove of the Patriarchs, and Silver Falls are all located in the Ohanapecosh area.</p>
<p>The <i>Carbon River Entrance Station</i> is located in the northwest corner of the park off <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Route_165" title="Washington State Route 165">Highway 165</a> and is the site of the only <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> at Mount Rainier. There is a campground and a short trail through the rainforest, as well as a trail to the Carbon Glacier, one of the lowest glaciers in the contiguous United States.</p>
<p><i>Mowich Lake</i> is the largest and deepest lake in the park, located south of Carbon at the south end of Highway 165. A campground, picnic area, and hiking trail are located near the lake.</p>
<p>The two major roads into the northwest quadrant of the Park were severely damaged by the floods of 2006. The ranger station at the Carbon River entrance is staffed during the summer. No motor vehicles are permitted beyond that point.</p>
