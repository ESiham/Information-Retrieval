<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Great Bear Rainforest</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <b>Great Bear Rainforest</b> (also known as the Central and North Coast forest)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatbearrainforest_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-greatbearrainforest-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coastlandusedecision_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-coastlandusedecision-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_rain_forest" title="Temperate rain forest">temperate rain forest</a> on the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Coast" title="British Columbia Coast">Pacific coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Originally a name coined by environmental groups in the mid-1990s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armstrong_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seattle_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birch_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birch-5">[5]</a></sup> it is a 6.4 million hectare area along British Columbia’s north and central coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCActIntro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BCActIntro-6">[6]</a></sup> It is part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Pacific_temperate_rainforests_(WWF_ecoregion)" title="Pacific temperate rainforests (WWF ecoregion)">Pacific temperate rainforest ecoregion</a>, which is the largest coastal temperate rainforest in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coastlandusedecision_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-coastlandusedecision-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobeProtect_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlobeProtect-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Bear Rainforest was officially recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_British_Columbia" title="Government of British Columbia">Government of British Columbia</a> in February 2016, when it announced an agreement to permanently protect 85% of the old-growth forested area from industrial <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobeProtect_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-GlobeProtect-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCProtect_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBCProtect-8">[8]</a></sup> The forest was admitted to the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Commonwealth_Canopy" title="Queen's Commonwealth Canopy">Queen's Commonwealth Canopy</a> in September of the same year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The size of the Great Bear Rainforest, also called the North and Central Coast land use planning area or the Central and North Coast LRMP area, is roughly 32,000 km<sup>2</sup> (12,000 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As part of the 2006 North and Central Coast Land Use Decision three new land use zones were created: Protected Areas; Biodiversity, Mining, and Tourism Areas (BMTAs); and Ecosystem-based Management Operating Areas (EBMs). As of 2009, approximately 16,000 km<sup>2</sup> (6,200 sq mi) of the region has been designated as protected areas (in a form called conservancies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>), and 3,000 km<sup>2</sup> (1,200 sq mi) as BMTAs. Commercial timber harvesting and commercial hydro-electric power projects are prohibited within BMTAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsrelease2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsrelease2009-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biodiversity_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversity-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Bear Rainforest extends from the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Islands" title="Discovery Islands">Discovery Islands</a> in the south to the BC-Alaska boundary in the north. It includes all offshore islands within this range except <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> and the archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a>. Its northern end reaches up <a href="/wiki/Portland_Canal" title="Portland Canal">Portland Canal</a> to the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Stewart,_British_Columbia" title="Stewart, British Columbia">Stewart</a>. To the south it includes <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert,_British_Columbia" title="Prince Rupert, British Columbia">Prince Rupert</a>, most of <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Channel" title="Douglas Channel">Douglas Channel</a>, half of <a href="/wiki/Hawkesbury_Island" title="Hawkesbury Island">Hawkesbury Island</a>, and part of <a href="/wiki/Gardner_Canal" title="Gardner Canal">Gardner Canal</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitimat,_British_Columbia" title="Kitimat, British Columbia">Kitimat</a> is outside the region, to the east. Farther south, the region includes all of the coast west and south of the <a href="/wiki/Fiordland_Conservancy" title="Fiordland Conservancy">Fiordland Conservancy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitlope_Heritage_Conservancy_Protected_Area" title="Kitlope Heritage Conservancy Protected Area">Kitlope Heritage Conservancy Protected Area</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tweedsmuir_North_Provincial_Park_and_Protected_Area" title="Tweedsmuir North Provincial Park and Protected Area">Tweedsmuir North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tweedsmuir_South_Provincial_Park" title="Tweedsmuir South Provincial Park">Tweedsmuir South Provincial Parks</a>—which includes <a href="/wiki/Dean_Channel" title="Dean Channel">Dean Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burke_Channel" title="Burke Channel">Burke Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rivers_Inlet" title="Rivers Inlet">Rivers Inlet</a>, and the communities of <a href="/wiki/Bella_Bella,_British_Columbia" title="Bella Bella, British Columbia">Bella Bella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bella_Coola,_British_Columbia" title="Bella Coola, British Columbia">Bella Coola</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hagensborg,_British_Columbia" title="Hagensborg, British Columbia">Hagensborg</a>. The southern end of the region includes <a href="/wiki/Knight_Inlet" title="Knight Inlet">Knight Inlet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bute_Inlet" title="Bute Inlet">Bute Inlet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-map2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EBMmap_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-EBMmap-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ursus_americanus_kermodei,_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="640" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg/220px-Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg/330px-Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg/440px-Ursus_americanus_kermodei%2C_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ursus_americanus_kermodei,_Great_Bear_Rainforest_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Kermode_bear" title="Kermode bear">Kermode bear</a> from the Great Bear Rainforest</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Great Bear Rainforest is one of the largest remaining tracts of unspoiled temperate rainforest left in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Living_Oceans_Society_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Living_Oceans_Society-15">[15]</a></sup> The area is home to species such as <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">cougars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bears</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kermode_bear" title="Kermode bear">Kermode ("spirit") bear</a>, a unique subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bear</a>, in which one in ten cubs displays a <a href="/wiki/Dominance_(genetics)" title="Dominance (genetics)">recessive</a> white coloured coat.</p>
<p>The forest features 1,000-year-old <a href="/wiki/Thuja_plicata" title="Thuja plicata">western red cedar</a> and 90-metre <a href="/wiki/Picea_sitchensis" title="Picea sitchensis">Sitka spruce</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coastal temperate rainforests are characterized by their proximity to both ocean and mountains. Abundant rainfall results when the atmospheric flow of moist air off the ocean collides with mountain ranges. Much of the Pacific coastline of North America shares this climate pattern, including portions of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_for_protection">Campaign for protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Campaign for protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1990s environmentalists launched a large scale campaign to protect the <a href="/wiki/Clayoquot_Sound" title="Clayoquot Sound">Clayoquot Sound</a> region of Vancouver Island. After years of conflict the British Columbia government announced a ban on clear-cutting in the Clayoquot rainforests and began a local planning process that incorporated First Nations of the area and independent scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009-17">[17]</a></sup> The Clayoquot Sound campaign became the model for the Great Bear Rainforest campaign. Techniques used at Clayoquot Sound were further developed and new approaches adopted, such as international marketing campaigns, improved mapping technologies, and the use of large-scale holistic ecosystem-based management models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1997 the central and northern BC coastal region was renamed "Great Bear Rainforest" by a network of <a href="/wiki/ENGO" title="ENGO">ENGOs</a> (environmental nongovernmental organizations), for the purpose of galvanizing an international campaign for its protection. The name, which was chosen without consulting local residents, was by 2005 being used by many organizations, including news media outlets. As Maureen Gail Reed writes, "the emotive significance of such a name cannot be underestimated".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed2004_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reed2004-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2004, after years of conflict and negotiation, the various stakeholders agreed to recommend the BC government that about 3,500,000 acres (14,000 km<sup>2</sup>), about 33% of the Great Bear Rainforest, be put under some form of protection, and that new forms of ecosystem-based forestry be required throughout the rainforest. This fell short of the scientific recommendations, which had concluded that 44%–70% should be protected. The recommendation given to the BC government was a compromise solution agreed to by the many stakeholders after years of difficult negotiations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009p471_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009p471-19">[19]</a></sup> The stakeholders include provincial and local governments; many <a href="/wiki/First_Nations_in_British_Columbia" title="First Nations in British Columbia">BC First Nations</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Heiltsuk_Nation" title="Heiltsuk Nation">Heiltsuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homalco_First_Nation" title="Homalco First Nation">Homalco</a>; the ENGOs <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ForestEthics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ForestEthics (page does not exist)">ForestEthics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Action_Network" title="Rainforest Action Network">Rainforest Action Network</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://sierraclub.bc.ca/" rel="nofollow">Sierra Club BC</a>; and forestry corporations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forest_Products" title="Canadian Forest Products">Canadian Forest Products</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst_Paper_Corporation" title="Catalyst Paper Corporation">Catalyst Paper Corporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Forest_Products" title="International Forest Products">International Forest Products</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Forest_Products" title="Western Forest Products">Western Forest Products</a>; and many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decision2006_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-decision2006-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 February 2006 a comprehensive protection package was announced for the Great Bear Rainforest, which was defined to include the central and north coasts of BC and <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a> (Queen Charlotte Islands). The Great Bear Rainforest Agreement included four key elements: rainforest protection, improved logging practices, the involvement of First Nations in decision making, and conservation financing to enable economic diversification. The final agreement banned logging in 33% of the Great Bear Rainforest and made a commitment to implement ecosystem-based forestry management for the entire Great Bear Rainforest by 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009p471_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Esbj.C3.B6rn-HargensZimmerman2009p471-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2006 agreement between the BC government and a wide coalition of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservationists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumberjack" title="Lumberjack">loggers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter" title="Hunter">hunters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> established a series of conservancies stretching 400 kilometres (250 mi) along the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-21">[21]</a></sup> The proposed protected areas will contain 18,000 square kilometres (6,900 sq mi), and another 46,900 square kilometres (18,100 sq mi) that is to be run under a management plan that is expected to ensure <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_forest_management" title="Sustainable forest management">sustainable forest management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Canadian government</a> announced on 21 January 2007 that it will spend <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>$30m for protection of this rainforest. This matches a pledge made previously by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_British_Columbia" title="Government of British Columbia">British Columbia provincial government</a>, as well as private donations of $60 million, making the total funding for the new reserve $120 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2007-01-22_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2007-01-22-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the autumn of 2008, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://sierraclub.bc.ca/" rel="nofollow">Sierra Club BC</a> and ForestEthics (jointly known as <a class="external text" href="http://www.savethegreatbear.org/" rel="nofollow">Rainforest Solutions Project</a>) launched an online campaign titled, "Keep the Promise," to put public pressure on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon_Campbell_(Canadian_politician)" title="Gordon Campbell (Canadian politician)">Gordon Campbell</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_British_Columbia" title="Premier of British Columbia">Premier of British Columbia</a>, to honour the Great Bear Rainforest agreement in its entirety. The groups were concerned certain aspects of the agreement, including implementation of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem-based_management" title="Ecosystem-based management">ecosystem-based management (EBM)</a>, would not materialize in time for the government's own final implementation deadline of March 31, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_2008-11-28_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_2008-11-28-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_recognition_and_protection">Government recognition and protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Government recognition and protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 1, 2016, <a href="/wiki/Premier" title="Premier">Premier</a> <a href="/wiki/Christy_Clark" title="Christy Clark">Christy Clark</a> announced an agreement had been reached between the province of British Columbia, <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, environmentalists and the forestry industry to protect 85% of the 6.4 million hectare Great Bear Rainforest from industrial logging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobeProtect_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-GlobeProtect-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCProtect_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBCProtect-8">[8]</a></sup> The remaining 15% would still be subject to logging under stringent conditions. The agreement also recognizes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_rights" title="Aboriginal rights">aboriginal rights</a> to shared decision-making, and provides a greater economic share of timber rights and $15-million in funding to 26 First Nations in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobeProtect_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-GlobeProtect-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Bear Rainforest (Forest Management) Act was introduced by the government on March 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobeActIntro_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlobeActIntro-26">[26]</a></sup> In September, <a href="/wiki/Prince_William,_Duke_of_Cambridge" title="Prince William, Duke of Cambridge">Prince William, Duke of Cambridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Catherine,_Duchess_of_Cambridge" title="Catherine, Duchess of Cambridge">Catherine, Duchess of Cambridge</a>, visited unveiled a plaque in the forest acknowledging its admission into the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Commonwealth_Canopy" title="Queen's Commonwealth Canopy">Queen's Commonwealth Canopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bear_Rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area is currently in the media due to the <a href="/wiki/Enbridge_Northern_Gateway_Pipelines" title="Enbridge Northern Gateway Pipelines">Northern Gateway Pipelines</a> project, which if approved, would bring crude oil tanker traffic regularly passing through the channels of the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Living_Oceans_Society:_Oil_and_Water_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Living_Oceans_Society:_Oil_and_Water-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
