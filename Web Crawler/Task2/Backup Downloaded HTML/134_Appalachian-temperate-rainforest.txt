<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Appalachian temperate rainforest</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Appalachian temperate rainforest</b> is located in the southern <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> of the eastern U.S. About 351,500 square kilometers (135,000 square miles) of forest land is spread across southwestern <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, western <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REINHARDT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-REINHARDT-2">[2]</a></sup> The annual <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> is more than 60 inches (over 1500mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Appalachian_spruce%E2%80%93fir_forest" title="Southern Appalachian spruce–fir forest">Southern Appalachian spruce–fir forest</a> is a temperate rainforest located in the higher elevations in southwestern Virginia and western North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REINHARDT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-REINHARDT-2">[2]</a></sup> Fir is dominant at higher elevation, spruce at middle elevation, and mixed forests at low elevation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tewksbury_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tewksbury-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creed_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Creed-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Appalachian temperate rainforest has a cool and mild climate. The mean annual low temperature is 4.4 °C (39.9 °F) and high is 15.5 °C (59.9 °F).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> High altitudes of the rainforest receive less than 2,000 millimetres (79 in) of precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REINHARDT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-REINHARDT-2">[2]</a></sup> This temperate rainforest is classified as a perhumid temperate rainforest. It is one of four subtypes of temperate rainforest identified by Alabak,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and it has a cool summer, typical transient snow in winter, mean annual temperature of 7 °C (45 °F), and summer rainfall is above 10% of precipitation memes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup> Precipitation in this area is more than <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>; as a result, the condition is wet whole year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup> Moist air comes from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and western Atlantic Ocean, and when they hit the Appalachian ranges then rain falls by <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">the orographic effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The high elevation is likely to cover clouds, which make the rate of evapotranspiration low and clouds are one of the water sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup> They are an important water source in this area; Interception by clouds supplies 20% to 50% of the annual water supply,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REINHARDT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-REINHARDT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup> which is a relatively high rate. In the eastern Canada Temperate Rainforest, fog has a role of increasing precipitation about 5 to 8%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Appalachian Mountains are very old. They started to form in the middle <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> period by the collision of plates. The collision which caused the uplift of the mountains began around 440 to 480 million years ago and ended around 230 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-8">[8]</a></sup> This range has an important role of forming the temperate rainforest in this area, because of the orographic effect. In <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">the Last Ice Age</a>, ice did not cover the south Appalachian Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Uncovered area was a refuge for animals and plants which lived in northern area. After the ice receded, some species spread back to north, while some of them stayed in this area. This is one of the reasons why there is a high biodiversity in the temperate rainforest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>High mountains make northern species able to survive. At the present, many plants form the temperate rainforest and the forests have a high rate of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup> About 10,000 species, including <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> salamanders and turtles live in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Red spruce and Fraser fir are dominant canopy trees in high mountain areas. In higher elevation (over 1,980 meters or 6,500 feet), Fraser fir is dominant, in middle elevation (1,675 to 1,890 meters or 5,495 to 6,201 feet) red spruce and Fraser fir grow together, and in lower elevation (1,370 to 1,650 meters or 4,490 to 5,410 feet) red spruce is dominant. Yellow birch, mountain ash, and mountain maple grow in the understory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tewksbury_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tewksbury-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creed_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Creed-6">[6]</a></sup> Younger spruce and fir and shrubs like raspberry, blackberry, hobblebush, southern mountain cranberries, red elderberry, minniebush, southern bush honeysuckle are understory vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creed_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Creed-6">[6]</a></sup> Below the spruce-fir forest, at around 1,200 metres (3,900 ft), are forests of American beech, yellow birch, maple, birch, and oak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Skunk cabbage and ground juniper are northern species that remained in this region after the glacier retreated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wet environment supports the high diversity of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>. Over 2000 species live in this area and scientists estimate many unidentified fungi may be there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More than 30 species of salamanders are found in the rainforest and some of them are endemic, such as Black Mountain salamander, southern dusky salamander, Jordan’s salamander, and Cheat Mountain salamander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup> Rotten trees and moist leaves on ground provide a good, wet environment for amphibians, including salamanders. Many species of salamanders in this area do not have lungs, so they breathe through their skin and the wet environment is conducive for their survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two species of turtles are endemic as well: flatted muck turtle and Alabama map turtle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As mammals, the most familiar is likely the American black bear. Other common mammals are white-tailed deer and groundhog. Northern species like <a href="/wiki/Northern_flying_squirrel" title="Northern flying squirrel">northern flying squirrel</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_red_squirrel" title="American red squirrel">red squirrel</a> survive in the area because of the cool climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_use">Human use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> have lived in this area for about 10,000 years. After contact with European settlers, the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation" title="Cherokee Nation">Cherokee Nation</a> was forced to move in 1838 to 1839 from their traditional homeland to Oklahoma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Trail" title="Appalachian Trail">Appalachian Trail</a>, which is a 2,000 miles (3,200 km) or more trail from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> provides one of the main recreational uses. The trail passes through the Appalachian mountain range, including the Appalachian temperate rainforest. The trail plan was published in 1921, and the trail was completed in 1937. According to the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Trail_Conservancy" title="Appalachian Trail Conservancy">Appalachian Trail Conservancy</a> website, 2500 hikers started at the Appalachian Trail southern trailhead in Georgia. Non-profit organizations and <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">the National Park Service</a> maintain the trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Part of the Appalachian temperate rainforest is in <a href="/wiki/Nantahala_National_Forest" title="Nantahala National Forest">the Nantahala National Forest</a> (established in 1920), <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_National_Forest" title="Cherokee National Forest">the Cherokee National Forest</a> (established in 1920 as well), and <a href="/wiki/Great_Smoky_Mountains_National_Park" title="Great Smoky Mountains National Park">the Great Smoky Mountains National Park</a> (established in 1934). The rainforest, protected federal-owned land, is host to recreational visitors. The Cherokee National Forest reported that millions of people visit the national forest each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highlands_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-highlands-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threat">Threat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Threat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic">Anthropogenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a> is caused by anthropogenic factors, such as power plants, factories, and automobiles. It is related to <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>. Vegetation in high elevation is easy to collect pollutants. Acid rain damages the plants and makes streams more acidic. Acidic water affects aquatic species, such as fish, salamanders, and also vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Air pollution affects <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">ground ozone</a>, which experience a biochemical change with sun light. The ground ozone damages plants. In the National Park Service, 30 plants species were damaged by the ground ozone and plants in higher elevation are prone damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">Non-native species</a> are another threat. For example, Balsam Woolly Adelgids, an insect which was accidentally introduced from Europe kills Fraser fir. Many dead fir stands on mountain peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire">Fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appalachian_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_fire" title="Wild fire">Wild fire</a> is created by lightning on average twice per year, usually in May or June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Although fire created by lightning is a natural <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbance</a> and can be a threat, it is an important factor for ensuring biodiversity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DellaSala_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-DellaSala-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Some native species, such as table mountain pines and woodpeckers, benefit from the environmental changes after fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Great Smoky Mountains National Park, the National Park Service performs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlled_burns" title="Controlled burns">controlled burns</a>: "to invigorate a species or ecosystem that benefits from fire" and "to reduce heavy accumulations of dead wood and brush which under drought conditions could produce catastrophic wildfires that threaten human life and valuable property."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup> Some rare plants thrive after a controlled fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSNP_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-GSNP-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
