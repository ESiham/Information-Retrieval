<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Japanese temperate rainforest</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Japanese temperate rainforest</b> is located in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_archipelago" title="Japanese archipelago">Japanese archipelago</a>, in small patches over a wide range of islands, from <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a> in the South to <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> in the North. Due to its geographic features and climate, the Japanese temperate rainforest is very different from other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforests" title="Temperate rainforests">temperate rainforests</a> in the world. The islands in the Japanese archipelago comprise about 1/400 of the world’s land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-s1-1">[1]</a></sup> The islands are located on a latitude that is normally dry; desert can be found elsewhere in the world at this latitude. However, the oceans surrounding Japan provide enough precipitation to maintain a temperate rainforest.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_description">General description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Japanese temperate rainforest can be classified into three types: the warm temperate zone found in the southern islands and lower elevations in the north, the cool temperate zone found in the northern islands and higher elevations in the south, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subalpine_forest" title="Subalpine forest">subalpine forest</a> in the higher elevations of northern <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> and Hokkaido.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-s1-1">[1]</a></sup> The distribution of the Japanese temperate rainforest is also highly dependent on altitude; you may see all of three types of temperate rainforest on the higher mountains such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Fuji" title="Mt. Fuji">Mt. Fuji</a> or Mt. Miyanouradake.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate of this region is warm and wet. The mean annual temperature is 6 – 13 °C in the cool temperate zone and 13 – 23 °C in the warm temperate zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-s2-2">[2]</a></sup> Annual precipitation is 1,200 – 1,800 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-s2-2">[2]</a></sup> Some regions have an annual precipitation of more than 2,800 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-s2-2">[2]</a></sup> The precipitation pattern of cool and warm temperate zones is almost opposite: the southern warm temperate rainforest has higher precipitation in summer and less precipitation in winter, and the northern cool temperate rainforest has lower precipitation in summer and higher in winter with the snowfall. High precipitation is caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" title="Ocean circulation">oceanic circulation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_shadow_effect" title="Rain shadow effect">rain shadow effect</a>. Summer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> bring warm; moist air to the southern islands, especially on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> side. <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerly</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_high" title="Siberian high">Siberian high</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsushima_Current" title="Tsushima Current">Tsushima Current</a> cause heavy snowfall on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a> side of northern Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Japanese temperate rainforest is home to about 5300 plant species, 40 percent of which are unique to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-s1-1">[1]</a></sup> The Japanese archipelago was not influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> extension in the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-s2-2">[2]</a></sup> therefore, it provided <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">refugia</a> for many species. Also, there is no dry, <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> area within the islands; thus, flora moved fluently between north and south after the last ice age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-s1-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, the Japanese islands are isolated, reducing immigration of organisms from the Eurasian continent.</p>
<p>The subalpine (cold) temperate rainforests are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">tsuga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-s1-1">[1]</a></sup> Veitch’s fir (<i><a href="/wiki/Abies_veitchii" title="Abies veitchii">Abies veitchii</a></i>), Maries’ fir (<i><a href="/wiki/Abies_mariesii" title="Abies mariesii">Abies mariesii</a></i>) and northern Japanese hemlock are commonly seen. Also, Japanese cypress (<i><a href="/wiki/Chamaecyparis" title="Chamaecyparis">Chamaecyparis</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Thujopsis" title="Thujopsis">Thujopsis</a></i> (also called hiba) can be found there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s6_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-s6-4">[4]</a></sup> Other than those trees, broad-leaf trees such as Japanese beech (<i><a href="/wiki/Fagus_crenata" title="Fagus crenata">Fagus crenata</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> are co-dominant canopy trees in this area. The understory is dominated by the bamboo <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sasa_veitchii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sasa veitchii (page does not exist)">Sasa veitchii</a></i> in most lower elevation sites in western Hokkaido. <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">Ferns</a>, sedges (<i><a href="/wiki/Carex" title="Carex">Carex</a></i>), and shrubs are co-dominant understory species in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s6_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-s6-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cool temperate rainforest is dominated by Japanese beech (<i><a href="/wiki/Fagus_crenata" title="Fagus crenata">Fagus crenata</a></i>). Also, Marie's fir, (<i>Abies mariesii</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_pumila" title="Pinus pumila">Pinus pumila</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quercus_crispula" title="Quercus crispula">Quercus crispula</a></i>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_cypress" title="Japanese cypress">Japanese cypress</a> are commonly seen in the cool temperate zone. The understory is dominated by another bamboo species called Chisimazasa (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sasa_kurilensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sasa kurilensis (page does not exist)">Sasa kurilensis</a></i>); willow and shrubs such as (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Camellia_rusticana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Camellia rusticana (page does not exist)">Camellia rusticana</a></i>) are also common in this zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (November 2013)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The warm temperate rainforest is dominated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_cedar" title="Japanese cedar">Japanese cedar</a> (<i>Cryptomeria</i>), Japanese stone oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Lithocarpus_edulis" title="Lithocarpus edulis">Lithocarpus edulis</a></i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Castanopsis_sieboldii" title="Castanopsis sieboldii">Castanopsis sieboldii</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Trochodendron" title="Trochodendron">Trochodendron</a></i>, Isu tree (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Distylium_racemosum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Distylium racemosum (page does not exist)">Distylium racemosum</a></i>), oak (<i>Quercus crispula</i>), and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Machilus_thunbergii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Machilus thunbergii (page does not exist)">Machilus thunbergii</a></i> are co-dominant trees in the warm temperate zone. The understory is dominated by another <i>Sasa</i> species called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moso_bamboo" title="Moso bamboo">moso bamboo</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostachys_edulis" title="Phyllostachys edulis">Phyllostachys edulis</a></i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">Rhododendron</a></i>, and <i>Rhododendron subg. Hymenanthes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The warm temperate rainforest is home to a great diversity of lichen and mosses due to the warm temperature and high precipitation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temperate rainforests, especially old-growth forests, provide quality habitat for many species, including <a href="/wiki/Natural_monument" title="Natural monument">natural monument</a> animals or those on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>—species such as the <a href="/wiki/Black_woodpecker" title="Black woodpecker">black woodpecker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_black_bear" title="Asian black bear">Japanese black bear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_dormouse" title="Japanese dormouse">Japanese dormouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_giant_salamander" title="Japanese giant salamander">Japanese giant salamander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_serow" title="Japanese serow">Japanese serow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_macaque" title="Japanese macaque">Japanese macaque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">Japanese golden eagle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sika_deer" title="Sika deer">sika deer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_grass_lizard" title="Japanese grass lizard">Japanese grass lizard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_rat_snake" title="Japanese rat snake">Japanese rat snake</a>. Larger mammals, such as sika deer and Japanese macaque, are commonly seen in all of the temperate zones, but most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a> and small mammals are unique to each zone. Some species, such as the black woodpecker, live in only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old-growth_forests" title="Old-growth forests">old-growth forests</a> and the ongoing loss of their habitat is a serious problem for these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s10_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-s10-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_usage">Historical usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the Japanese temperate rainforest has been logged and used as fuel and building materials over time. Before industrial development, people lived with the forest; they respected the forest and mountains. Mountain worship and mountain asceticism have been very common<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s10_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-s10-8">[8]</a></sup> in Japan through the ages. However, industry and war have forced people to cut the forest. The natural old-growth forest has declined rapidly with economic development and the government's policies. Some areas are turned into <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a> forest (secondary forest) of Japanese cedar, <i>Quercus serrate</i>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sawtooth_oak" title="Sawtooth oak">sawtooth oak</a> (<i>Quercus acutissime</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>These forests are known as <a href="/wiki/Satoyama" title="Satoyama">Satoyama</a> and were well-managed until the government changed its policies again. Takeuchi explains Satoyama as “secondary woodlands and grassland near human settlements that have traditionally used these lands as coppices and meadows for fuel, fertilizer, and fodder.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s14_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-s14-12">[12]</a></sup> Increased importing of fossil fuel and timber changed the value of Satoyama in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s14_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-s14-12">[12]</a></sup> The Japanese forest industry was reduced and people lost interest in forest management and timber harvesting. The population’s aging and loss of timber jobs caused population decrease in rural area, which made it difficult to maintain the Satoyama area. Today, however, society’s attention is being pulled back to the function of Satoyama and people have started to maintain the forest again.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disturbance_and_conservation">Disturbance and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_temperate_rainforest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disturbance and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common disturbances in Japanese temperate rainforests are triggered by <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a> that have a strong influence on both the forests and human populations. Typhoons cause trees to fall as well as floods and landslides. Although some trees falling is a normal part of the forest lifecycle, large numbers of trees falling all at once can alter or damage the ecosystem.</p>
<p>Other recent concerns include damage by animals. Insect infestation and sika deer foraging have become big issues. Infestations by insects have increased rapidly since the 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s15_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-s15-13">[13]</a></sup> especially in the last decade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s15_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-s15-13">[13]</a></sup> and have impacted the forestry industry. The sika deer's foraging has a less direct impact on the forest itself; however, it represents about 60% of all damage from animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s6_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-s6-4">[4]</a></sup> The deer also eat seedlings, which increases the risk of future canopy decline. Occasionally, sika deer attack orchards in search of food; this may suggest overpopulation of sika deer, possibly due to human impact on their habitat. Ongoing losses from land conversion and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> also represent serious threats to the conservation of Japanese temperate rainforests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-s2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
