<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blood rain</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Blood_rain_(disambiguation)" title="Blood rain (disambiguation)">Blood rain (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Blood rain</b> or <b>red rain</b> is a phenomenon in which blood is perceived to fall from the sky in the form of rain. Cases have been recorded since <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, composed approximately 8th century BC, and are widespread. Before the 17th century it was generally believed that the rain was actually blood. Literature mirrors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_(religion)" title="Cult (religion)">cult practice</a>, in which the appearance of blood rain was considered a bad omen. It was used as a tool foreshadowing events, but while some of these may be literary devices, some occurrences are historic. There is now a scientific consensus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> that the blood rain phenomenon is caused by aerial spores of green microalgae <a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)"><i>Trentepohlia annulata</i></a>.</p>
<p>Recorded instances of blood rain usually cover small areas. The duration can vary, sometimes lasting only a short time, others several days. By the 17th century, explanations for the phenomenon had moved away from the supernatural and attempted to provide natural reasons. In the 19th century, blood rains were scientifically examined, and theories that dust gave the water its red colour gained ground. Today, the dominant theories are that the rain is caused by red dust <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspended</a> in the water (<a href="/wiki/Rain_dust" title="Rain dust">rain dust</a>) or by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Red_rain_in_Kerala" title="Red rain in Kerala">micro-organisms</a>. Alternative explanations include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurorae</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_use_in_literature">History and use in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and use in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Occurrences of blood rain throughout history are distributed from the ancient, to the modern day. The earliest literary instance is in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i>Iliad</i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> twice caused a rain of blood, on one occasion to warn of slaughter in a battle. The same portent occurs in the work of the poet <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_442_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_442-2">[2]</a></sup> writing around 700 BC;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the author John Tatlock suggests that Hesiod's story may have been influenced by that recorded in the Iliad. The first-century Greek biographer <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> also recounts a tradition of a rain of blood during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">Romulus</a>, founder of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Roman authors <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> record some later cases of blood rain, with Livy describing it as a bad portent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_442_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_442-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unusual events such as a rain of blood were considered bad omens in Antiquity, and this belief persisted through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and well into the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">Early modern period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPG_100_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TPG_100-5">[5]</a></sup> Throughout northern and western Europe there are many cases of rains of blood which were used by contemporary writers to augur bad events: the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> records that in 685, "there was a bloody rain in Britain. And milk and butter were turned to blood. And <a href="/wiki/Hlothhere_of_Kent" title="Hlothhere of Kent">Lothere</a>, king of Kent, died". Tatlock suggests that although the <i>Chronicle</i> was written long after the events, it may have basis in historical truth. He notes that although the rain may seem to be foreshadowing the death of Lothere, medieval chroniclers often noted unusual occurrences in their works "merely for their general interest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_444_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_444-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">Gregory of Tours</a> records that in 582 "In the territory of Paris there rained real blood from the clouds, falling upon the garments of many men, who were so stained and spotted that they stripped themselves of their own clothing in horror".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarton_97_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarton_97-7">[7]</a></sup> Although the work of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>, a 12th-century writer who popularised the legends of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>, is regarded as "fantastical" rather than reliable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> he too notes the occurrence of blood rain, in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Rivallo" title="Rivallo">Rivallo</a>. This event was further expanded on by <a href="/wiki/Layamon" title="Layamon">Layamon</a> in his poem <i><a href="/wiki/Layamon%27s_Brut" title="Layamon's Brut">Brut</a></i> (written around 1190), who described how blood rain was one of several portents, and which itself led to destruction:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the same time here came a strange token, such as before never came, nor never hitherto since. From heaven here came a marvellous flood; three days it rained blood, three days and three nights. That was exceeding great harm! When the rain was gone, here came another token anon. Here came black flies, and flew in men's eyes; in their mouth, in their nose, their lives went all to destruction; such multitude of flies here was that they ate the corn and the grass. Woe was all the folk that dwelt in the land! Thereafter came such a mortality that few here remained alive. Afterward here came an evil hap, that king Riwald died.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Many works which record occurrences of blood rain, such as that of Layamon, were written significantly after the event was supposed to have taken place. The 14th-century monk <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ralph_Higden" title="Ralph Higden">Ralph Higden</a> in his work, the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Polchronicon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polchronicon (page does not exist)">Polchronicon</a></i>, recounts that in 787 there was a rain of blood, perhaps intended by the author as an indication of the coming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasion. Written in the 12th century, the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leinster" title="Book of Leinster">Book of Leinster</a></i> records many sensational events, including showers of silver; it records a shower of blood in 868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the work of <a href="/wiki/William_of_Newburgh" title="William of Newburgh">William of Newburgh</a>, a rain of blood proves the drive and determination of <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">Richard the Lionheart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/William_of_Newburgh" title="William of Newburgh">William of Newburgh</a>, a contemporary chronicler,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in May 1198 Richard and the labourers working on <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_Gaillard" title="Château Gaillard">the castle</a> were drenched in a "rain of blood". While some of his advisers thought the rain was an evil omen, Richard was undeterred:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the king was not moved by this to slacken one whit the pace of work, in which he took such keen pleasure that, unless I am mistaken, even if an angel had descended from heaven to urge its abandonment he would have been roundly cursed.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— William of Newburgh<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:20%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>[1552] On Wytsone evyne it raynyd in dyvers places in London that it was sene lyynge in dyvers places on the erbbes as redde as wyne.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><cite class="citation"><a class="external text" href="https://archive.org/stream/chronicleofgreyf00londrich#page/74/mode/2up" rel="nofollow"><i>Chronicle of the Grey Friars of London</i></a></cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ABlood+rain&amp;rft.btitle=Chronicle+of+the+Grey+Friars+of+London&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft_id=https%3A%2F%2Farchive.org%2Fstream%2Fchronicleofgreyf00londrich%23page%2F74%2Fmode%2F2up&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></cite></div>
<p>In Germany, a shower of blood was one of several portents for the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> in 1348–1349.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_447_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_447-15">[15]</a></sup> The phenomenon gained exposure to a wide audience in the 16th century, during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, when it was used as an example of the power of God; a form of literature using prodigies such as blood rain as cautions against immorality proliferated across Europe having originated in Italy. In Germany, such works were particularly popular amongst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPG_100_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TPG_100-5">[5]</a></sup> Although unusual events such as rains of blood were still treated with superstition, often as demonstrations of godly power, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Claude_Fabri_de_Peiresc" title="Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc">Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc</a> (1580–1637) was one of the few who proposed natural causes; after hearing of a bloody rain in <a href="/wiki/Aix-en-Provence" title="Aix-en-Provence">Aix-en-Provence</a>, he suggested it was caused by butterflies. Although his theory would later be rejected, he helped the likes of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">Pierre Gassendi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Antoine_Ferchault_de_R%C3%A9aumur" title="René Antoine Ferchault de Réaumur">René Antoine Ferchault de Réaumur</a> to lay the foundations for removing superstition from explanations of the phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarton_97_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sarton_97-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe, there were fewer than 30 recorded cases all together of blood rain in the 13th, 14th, and 15th centuries. There were 190 instances across the 16th and 17th centuries; there was a decline in the 17th century when only 43 were recorded, but this picked up again with 146 in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There is little literature on the subject of blood rain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarton_97_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sarton_97-7">[7]</a></sup> although it has gained the attention of some naturalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The phenomenon received international coverage in 2001, after <a href="/wiki/Red_rain_in_Kerala" title="Red rain in Kerala">red rain fell in Kerala</a>, India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and again in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_rain_Kerala_optical_microscope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="246" data-file-width="300" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Red_rain_Kerala_optical_microscope.jpg/220px-Red_rain_Kerala_optical_microscope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/77/Red_rain_Kerala_optical_microscope.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_rain_Kerala_optical_microscope.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photomicrograph" title="Photomicrograph">Photomicrograph</a> of particles from a sample of red rain from Kerala</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A 2015 study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> has unambiguously established that the cause of blood rain in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> was the aerial spores of green microalgae <a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia</a>. The study used <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">molecular phylogenetics</a> to compare the evolution of DNA sequence of T. annulata isolated from blood rain sample with that of T. annulata from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. Results suggest that the isolate from Kerala is, in fact, a recently introduced species from Austria. The research confirmed the likelihood that the introduction happened through clouds over the ocean, a phenomenon of intercontinental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_dispersal" title="Species dispersal">species dispersal</a> previously reported for bacteria and fungi but not for an alga. Clouds over ocean dispersal is analogous to the intercontinental flights; spores of this alga from Europe are transported to India via clouds that drift across the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spores" title="Spores">Spores</a> might have been carried first to the clouds for its dispersal. How exactly these lower stratospheric clouds containing algal spores got in Kerala remain unknown but it might be related to the monsoon as well, as Kerala is the first state that the southwest monsoon strikes together with Sri Lanka. Again, trade winds (SE and NE) converge at a region called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inter-Tropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone">Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ), which is located close to Kerala and Sri Lanka, which might be another clue for this puzzle. Authors said <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> their steps would be the analysis of intercontinental clouds using High-Efficiency Particulate Air Filters, using the similar DNA sequence based technique called “metagenomics”, which would reveal the entire microbial diversity of these clouds.</p>
<p>While most ancient authors, such as Hesiod and Pliny, tended to ascribe the rain to the acts of gods, <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> rejected the idea and instead suggested that the red rain may be caused by "<i>ex aliqua contagione terrena</i>", "from some earthly contagion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_442_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_442-2">[2]</a></sup> The two cases in the <i>Iliad</i> are explained by <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a> as simply red-coloured rain rather than literally blood; however, a later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholiast" title="Scholiast">scholiast</a> (a critical or explanatory commentator) suggests that it was precipitation of blood that had evaporated earlier:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> after a battle, blood would flow into nearby water courses, evaporate, and then fall as rain. This explanation, demonstrating unfamiliarity with the properties of <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> was echoed by <a href="/wiki/Eustathius_of_Thessalonica" title="Eustathius of Thessalonica">Eustathius of Thessalonica</a>, a 12th-century archbishop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_442_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_442-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tatlock, in a study of some medieval cases of blood rain, notes that the medieval cases of blood rain "agree well" with their classical counterparts. Although there are variables (for example, the rain sometimes lasted only for a short period, while on other occasions, it could last days), they were widely considered to be bad omens and warnings of bad events to come. He also suggests that the phenomenon may be recorded only in small areas because the colour of the rain would not always be noticed and may be obvious against only pale backgrounds. In the classical period, events such as a shower of blood was seen a demonstration of godly power; in the medieval period, Christians were less inclined to attribute the phenomenon to such reasons although followers of <a href="/wiki/Nature_worship" title="Nature worship">nature-religions</a> were happy to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatlock_447_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tatlock_447-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, there was a trend towards examining events such as rains of blood more scientifically; Ehrenberg conducted experiments at the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Berlin Academy</a>, attempting to recreate "blood rain" using dust mixed with water. He concluded that blood rain was caused by water mixing with a reddish dust, mostly composed of animal and vegetable matter. He was unclear on the origin of the dust but stated that it lacked the characteristics of African dust that might have indicated it came from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>. Instead, he suggested that the dust came from dried swamps where it was picked up by violent winds and would later fall as rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This explanation has persisted, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Academic_Press" title="Academic Press">Academic Press</a> Dictionary of Science and Technology</i> (1992) attributes the colour of blood rain to the presence of dust containing <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other reasons for blood rain aside from dust are sometimes given. Schove and Peng-Yoke have suggested that the phenomenon may be connected to <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurorae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Red_rain_in_Kerala" title="Red rain in Kerala">red rain fell in Kerala</a>, dust was the suspected cause. Alternative theories, later dismissed, included dust from a meteorite and extraterrestrial cells in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The particles causing the red colour in Kerala were later determined to be spores of the alga <i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia annulata</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rajkumar_Gangappa_and_Stuart_Hogg_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rajkumar_Gangappa_and_Stuart_Hogg-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
