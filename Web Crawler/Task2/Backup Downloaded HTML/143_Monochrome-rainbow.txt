<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monochrome rainbow</h1>

<p>A <b>Monochrome</b> or <b>Red Rainbow</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> phenomenon and a rare variation of the more commonly seen multicolored <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a>. Its formation process is identical to that of a normal rainbow (namely the reflection/refraction of light in water droplets), the difference being that a <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochrome</a> rainbow requires the sun to be close to the horizon; i.e., near sunrise or sunset. The low angle of the sun results in a longer distance for its light to travel through the atmosphere, causing shorter <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of light, such as blue, green and yellow, to be scattered and leaving primarily red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the lower light environment where the phenomenon most often forms, a monochrome rainbow can leave a highly dramatic effect.</p>
