<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_belief">Religious belief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Religious belief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WhereRainbowRises.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1360" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/WhereRainbowRises.jpg/170px-WhereRainbowRises.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/WhereRainbowRises.jpg/255px-WhereRainbowRises.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/WhereRainbowRises.jpg/340px-WhereRainbowRises.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rainbows_in_mythology" title="Rainbows in mythology">Rainbows in mythology</a></div>
<p>The rainbow has a place in legend owing to its beauty and the historical difficulty in explaining the phenomenon.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_mythology" title="Greco-Roman mythology">Greco-Roman mythology</a>, the rainbow was considered to be a path made by a messenger (<a href="/wiki/Iris_(mythology)" title="Iris (mythology)">Iris</a>) between Earth and Heaven.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>, the rainbow was a slit in the sky sealed by goddess <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCwa" title="Nüwa">Nüwa</a> using stones of five different colours.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_religion" title="Hindu religion">Hindu religion</a>, the rainbow is called <i>Indradhanush</i>, meaning "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_(weapon)" title="Bow (weapon)">bow</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>: <i>dhanush</i> is bow) of <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Indra</a>, the god of <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>". Another Indian mythology says the rainbow is the bow of <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a>, the incarnation of <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>. It is called <i>Ramdhonu</i> in <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>, <i>dhonu</i> (dhanush) meaning bow. Likewise, in mythology of Arabian Peninsula, the rainbow, called <i>Qaus Quzaħ</i> in Arabic, is the war bow of the god Quzaħ.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Armenian_mythology" title="Armenian mythology">Armenian mythology</a> the rainbow is a belt of Tir, a Sun god.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_Mythology" title="Norse Mythology">Norse Mythology</a>, a rainbow called the <a href="/wiki/Bifr%C3%B6st" title="Bifröst">Bifröst</a> Bridge connects the realms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81sgard" title="Ásgard">Ásgard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">Midgard</a>, homes of the gods and humans, respectively.</p>
<p>The Irish <a href="/wiki/Leprechaun" title="Leprechaun">leprechaun</a>'s secret hiding place for his pot of gold is usually said to be at the end of the rainbow. This place is impossible to reach, because the rainbow is an optical effect which depends on the location of the viewer. When walking towards the end of a rainbow, it will appear to "move" further away (two people who simultaneously observe a rainbow at different locations will disagree about where a rainbow is). Also, a rainbow is in fact a full circle, we usually only see the half arc of it due to our positioning in respect to it; if you were to be positioned higher up (such as on a tall building or an airplane) then you could view it's actual full circle. So in reality there is no end to a rainbow, just as there is no end to a circle. Therefore, that 'end of the rainbow' is in other words an impossible/non-existent place.</p>
<p>Another ancient portrayal of the rainbow is given in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a>: the rainbow is the "jewelled necklace of the Great Mother <a href="/wiki/Ishtar" title="Ishtar">Ishtar</a>" that she lifts into the sky as a promise that she "will never forget these days of the great flood" that destroyed her children. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="The Epic of Gilgamesh">The Epic of Gilgamesh</a>, Tablet Eleven)</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Then Ishtar arrived. She lifted up the necklace of great jewels that her father, Anu, had created to please her and said, "Heavenly gods, as surely as this jewelled necklace hangs upon my neck, I will never forget these days of the great flood. Let all of the gods except Enlil come to the offering. Enlil may not come, for without reason he brought forth the flood that destroyed my people."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Genesis, after Noah's flood God put the rainbow in the sky as the sign of His promise that He would never again destroy the earth with flood (Genesis 9:13–17):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>I do set my bow in the cloud, and it shall be for a token of a covenant between me and the earth. And it shall come to pass, when I bring a cloud over the earth, that the bow shall be seen in the cloud: And I will remember my covenant, which is between me and you and every living creature of all flesh; and the waters shall no more become a flood to destroy all flesh. And the bow shall be in the cloud; and I will look upon it, that I may remember the everlasting covenant between God and every living creature of all flesh that is upon the earth. And God said unto Noah, This is the token of the covenant, which I have established between me and all flesh that is upon the earth.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints founder and prophet Joseph Smith stated that the second coming of the Christ would not occur in any year in which a rainbow is seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Dreamtime" title="Dreamtime">Dreamtime</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_mythology" title="Australian Aboriginal mythology">Australian Aboriginal mythology</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow_snake" title="Rainbow snake">rainbow snake</a> is the deity governing <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazonian</a> cultures, rainbows have long been associated with malign spirits that cause harm, such as miscarriages and (especially) skin problems. In the <a href="/wiki/Yanesha%27_language" title="Yanesha' language">Amuesha</a> language of central <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, certain diseases are called <i>ayona’achartan</i>, meaning "the rainbow hurt my skin". A tradition of closing one's mouth at the sight of a rainbow in order to avoid disease appears to pre-date the Incan empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">New Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindu_philosophy" title="Hindu philosophy">Hindu philosophy</a>, the seven colours of the rainbow represent the seven <a href="/wiki/Chakra" title="Chakra">chakras</a>, from the first chakra (red) to the seventh chakra (violet).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainbows are generally described as very colourful and peaceful. The rainbow occurs often in paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Frequently these have a symbolic or programmatic significance (for example, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melancholia_I" title="Melancholia I">Melancholia I</a>). In particular, the rainbow appears regularly in religious art (for example, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Anton_Koch" title="Joseph Anton Koch">Joseph Anton Koch</a>'s <i>Noah's Thank Offering</i>). Romantic landscape painters such as <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">Constable</a> were more concerned with recording fleeting effects of light (for example, Constable's <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral_from_the_Meadows" title="Salisbury Cathedral from the Meadows">Salisbury Cathedral from the Meadows</a>). Other notable examples appear in work by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Memling" title="Hans Memling">Hans Memling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caspar_David_Friedrich" title="Caspar David Friedrich">Caspar David Friedrich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>.</p>

<p>In contemporary visual art, the rainbow often appears as well, notably in Peter Coffin's <i>Untitled (Rainbow)</i>, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and in Ugo Rondinone's <i>Hell, Yes!</i>, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Like many other cultural references to the rainbow, these either emphasize the possible sublimity of the natural world or the cheerfulness, joy, and celebration often culturally associated with a profusion of colors.</p>
<p>In 2012, American artist, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jones_McKean" title="Michael Jones McKean">Michael Jones McKean</a> created a large-scale artwork, <a class="external text" href="http://www.therainbow.org/" rel="nofollow"><i>The Rainbow</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The project created, impart, a fully sustainable prismatic rainbow using thousands of gallons of pressurized harvested rainwater, at times stretching several city blocks in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Judith Bauer Stamper's 1987 Find Your Fate book "Jem and The Holograms #3: The Secret of Rainbow Island ", involves the reader in saving Rainbow Island, a tropical island that frequently has rainbows.</p>
<p>American legend retelling <i>The Rough Face Girl" involves the heroines seeing an invisible Chief, who wears a rainbow as a sash.</i></p>
<p>Stephen King's 1985 book '<a href="/wiki/It_(novel)" title="It (novel)">It</a> <i>features a scene where Ben Hanscom sees a spray bow while fighting with Henry Bowers in The Barrens, and comments on finding gold at the end of it.</i></p>
<p>The 1983 <i><a href="/wiki/Care_Bears" title="Care Bears">Care Bears</a></i> book "A Sister for Ben" involves Cheer Bear, whose tummy symbol is a rainbow, telling Ben he will see a rainbow when his sister says her first word.</p>
<p>Dan Piraro's comic strip <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bizzaro" title="Bizzaro">Bizzaro</a></i> featured a 2008 cartoon about fictional pirate Rainbowbeard in response to Proposition 8.</p>
<p>The rainbow inspires metaphor and simile. <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/To_the_Lighthouse" title="To the Lighthouse">To the Lighthouse</a></i> highlights the transience of life and Man's mortality through Mrs Ramsey's thought,</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"it was all as ephemeral as a rainbow"</i></dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">Wordsworth's</a> 1802 poem "<a href="/wiki/My_Heart_Leaps_Up" title="My Heart Leaps Up">My Heart Leaps Up</a>" begins:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>My heart leaps up when I behold</i>
<dl>
<dd><i>A rainbow in the sky:</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><i>So was it when my life began;</i></dd>
<dd><i>So is it now I am a man;</i></dd>
<dd><i>So be it when I shall grow old,</i>
<dl>
<dd><i>Or let me die!...</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newtonian</a> deconstruction of the rainbow is said to have provoked <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a> to lament in his 1820 poem "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Lamia" title="s:Lamia">Lamia</a>":</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Do not all charms fly</i></dd>
<dd><i>At the mere touch of cold philosophy?</i></dd>
<dd><i>There was an awful rainbow once in heaven:</i></dd>
<dd><i>We know her woof, her texture; she is given</i></dd>
<dd><i>In the dull catalogue of common things.</i></dd>
<dd><i>Philosophy will clip an Angel's wings,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Conquer all mysteries by rule and line,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Empty the haunted air, and gnomed mine –</i></dd>
<dd><i>Unweave a rainbow</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>In contrast to this is <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a>; talking about his book <i><a href="/wiki/Unweaving_the_Rainbow" title="Unweaving the Rainbow">Unweaving the Rainbow</a>: Science, Delusion and the Appetite for Wonder</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"My title is from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keats" title="Keats">Keats</a>, who believed that <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a> had destroyed all the poetry of the rainbow by reducing it to the prismatic colours. Keats could hardly have been more wrong, and my aim is to guide all who are tempted by a similar view, towards the opposite conclusion. Science is, or ought to be, the inspiration for great poetry."</p>
</blockquote>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow" title="Over the Rainbow">Over the Rainbow</a></i> is a song sung by the character Dorothy Gale (<a href="/wiki/Judy_Garland" title="Judy Garland">Judy Garland</a>) in the musical film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i>.</li>
<li>Doo wop bands The Rainbows and Randy and the Rainbows</li>

<li><i>Rainbow Country</i> by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flags">Flags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Flags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_flag" title="Rainbow flag">Rainbow flag</a></div>
<p>Rainbow flags tend to be used as a sign of a new era, of hope, or of social change. Rainbow flags have been used in many places over the centuries: in the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a> in the 16th century, as a symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">Cooperative</a> movement; as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_symbol#The_Peace_Rainbow_Flag" title="Peace symbol">symbol of peace</a>, especially in Italy; to represent the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawantin_Suyu" title="Tawantin Suyu">Tawantin Suyu</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inca" title="Inca">Inca</a> territory, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fotw_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fotw-10">[10]</a></sup> by some <a href="/wiki/Druze" title="Druze">Druze</a> communities in the Middle east; by the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Autonomous_Oblast" title="Jewish Autonomous Oblast">Jewish Autonomous Oblast</a>; to represent the International Order of Rainbow for Girls since the early 1920s, and as a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Gay_pride" title="Gay pride">gay pride</a> and <a href="/wiki/LGBT_social_movements" title="LGBT social movements">LGBT social movements</a> since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainbow_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rainbow-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the 1990s, Archibishop Desmond Tutu and President Nelson Mandela described the newly democratic South Africa as the "rainbow nation".</p>
