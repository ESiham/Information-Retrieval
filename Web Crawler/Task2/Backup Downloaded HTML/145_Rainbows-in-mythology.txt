<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a>, a natural phenomenon noted for its beauty and mystical appearance, has been a favorite component of <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a> throughout history. Rainbows are part of the myths of many cultures around the world. The <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> saw it as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bifrost" title="Bifrost">Bifrost</a>; <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> traditions see it as a covenant with <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> not to destroy the world by means of floodwater. Whether as a bridge to the heavens, messenger, archer’s bow, or <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(symbolism)" title="Serpent (symbolism)">serpent</a>, the rainbow has been pressed into <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a> service for <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennia</a>. There is a <a href="/wiki/Myriad" title="Myriad">myriad</a> of beliefs concerning the rainbow. The complex diversity of rainbow myths are far-reaching, as are their inherent similarities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges">Bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="684" data-file-width="752" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/220px-Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/330px-Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/440px-Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heimdall_an_der_Himmelsbr%C3%BCcke.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Heimdall before the Rainbow Bridge</div>
</div>

<p>In Norse religion, a burning rainbow bridge called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bifrost" title="Bifrost">Bifrost</a> connects <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">Midgard</a> (earth) with Asgard, home of the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-1">[1]</a></sup> Bifrost can be used only by gods and those who are killed in battle. It is eventually shattered under the weight of war - the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ragnarok" title="Ragnarok">Ragnarok</a> (German Götterdammerung). The notion that the rainbow bridge to heaven is attainable by only the good or virtuous, such as warriors and royalty, is a theme repeated often in world myths.</p>
<p>In the ancient beliefs of Japan, rainbows were the bridges that human ancestors took to descend to the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_people" title="Navajo people">Navajo</a> tradition, the rainbow is the path of the holy spirits, and is frequently depicted in sacred sandpaintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Maori tell a tale of Hina, the moon, who caused a rainbow to span the heavens even down to the earth, for her mortal husband to return to earth to end his days, since death may not enter her celestial home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Messenger">Messenger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Messenger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iris_(mythology)" title="Iris (mythology)">Iris</a> is the personification of the rainbow and a messenger linking the gods to humanity. Iris is frequently mentioned as a divine messenger in the <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a>. She carries a caduceus or winged staff. Iris's messages often concerned war and retribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-4">[4]</a></sup> However, like many Greek gods, Iris is continually being redefined. The rainbow eventually became solely a mode of transportation for Iris, who proves to be as elusive and unpredictable as the rainbow itself.</p>
<p>Anuenue, the rainbow maiden, appears in Hawaiian legends as the messenger for her brothers, the gods Tane and Kanaloa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Archer's_bow"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Archer.27s_bow">Archer's bow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archer's bow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rainbow is depicted as an archer's bow in <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">Hindu mythology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Indra</a>, the god of thunder and war, uses the rainbow to shoot arrows of lightning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sumerian_mythology">Sumerian mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sumerian mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A rather ambiguous perception of the rainbow strikes a vein in all world culture, through its entire storied past.</p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="The Epic of Gilgamesh">The Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>, who was an ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> king (ca.3000 BC), is our first detailed written evidence of human civilization. In a Victorian translation of a Gilgamesh variant, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leonidas_Le_Cenci_Hamilton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leonidas Le Cenci Hamilton (page does not exist)">Leonidas Le Cenci Hamilton</a>'s Epic of Ishtar and Izdubar, King Izdubar sees "a mass of colors like the rainbow’s hues" that are "linked to divine sanction for war." Later in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a>, Izdubar sees the "glistening colors of the rainbow rise" in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fountain_of_life" title="Fountain of life">fountain of life</a> next to <a href="/wiki/Elam" title="Elam">Elam</a>’s Tree of Immortality.</p>
<p>The Sumerian farmer god <a href="/wiki/Ninurta" title="Ninurta">Ninurta</a> defends <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> with a bow and arrow, and wore a crown described as a rainbow.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_aboriginal_mythology">Australian aboriginal mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australian aboriginal mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Australian Aboriginal mythology, the rainbow snake is the Creator (Kurreah, Andrenjinyi, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yingarna" title="Yingarna">Yingarna</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ngalyod&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ngalyod (page does not exist)">Ngalyod</a> and others) in the Dreaming, which is the infinite period of time that "began with the world's creation and that has no end. People, animals, and Eternal Beings like the Rainbow Serpent are all part of the Dreaming, and everyday life is affected by the Dreaming's immortals," in almost every Australian <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Aborigine</a> tribe. In these tribes, of which there are over 50, actual rainbows are gigantic, often malevolent, serpents who inhabit the sky or ground. This snake has different names in different tribes, and has both different and similar traits from tribe to tribe.</p>
<p>The theme consistent with most Australian tribes is that the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Serpent" title="Rainbow Serpent">Rainbow Serpent</a> is the creator of the world and all beings. During the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>, the Rainbow Serpent retreats to deep waterholes. Another common theme among all aboriginal tribes is that the Rainbow Serpent has no gender. And while the Rainbow Serpent can give fertility by creating rain, it can also let loose blindness and disease. "The Aboriginal Rainbow is humanity, because it causes the 'energy' and the 'breath' that gives people life."</p>
<p>Ngalyod, first-born son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yingarna" title="Yingarna">Yingarna</a>, who is the Rainbow Serpent creator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kunwinjku" title="Kunwinjku">Kunwinjku</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Arnhem" title="Arnhem">Arnhem</a> Land in Australia—sucks up water during the dry season and spits it out as rain during the wet season. Like Iris, Ngalyod helps to ensure fertility with rains, but he can destroy as well as nurture. Yingarna, the creator of the Kunwinjku people, is "nominally female" and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Androgynous" title="Androgynous">androgynous</a> like her son Ngalyod. She possesses cunningly ambivalent form; as she combines elements of <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">emu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo" title="Kangaroo">kangaroo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> Rainbow Serpent, like the Aborigines' Ngalyod, sucks up water and spits rain, and also has a confused identity – it possesses an <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">ox</a>'s head on its serpent body.</p>
<h2><span id="Judeo–Christian_faith"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Judeo.E2.80.93Christian_faith">Judeo–Christian faith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judeo–Christian faith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> saved the animals from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deluge_(mythology)" title="Deluge (mythology)">the Great Flood</a>, a rainbow appeared. As the flood had killed all other living beings, the rainbow came to symbolise God's promise that he would never send another flood to destroy all of the earth and that never again would all living things be killed in the waters of a flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mythologies">Other mythologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbows_in_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other mythologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For some native people the rainbow around the sun is considered to be a sign from God, marking a time of great change.</p>
<p>“The Whirling Rainbow – Prophecy There will come a day when people of all races, colors, and creeds will put aside their differences. They will come together in love, joining hands in unification, to heal the Earth and all Her children. They will move over the Earth like a great Whirling Rainbow, bringing peace, understanding and healing everywhere they go. Many creatures thought to be extinct or mythical will resurface at this time; the great trees that perished will return almost overnight. All living things will flourish, drawing sustenance from the breast of our Mother, the Earth.</p>
<p>The great spiritual Teachers who walked the Earth and taught the basics of the truths of the Whirling Rainbow Prophecy will return and walk amongst us once more, sharing their power and understanding with all. We will learn how to see and hear in a sacred manner. Men and women will be equals in the way Creator intended them to be; all children will be safe anywhere they want to go. Elders will be respected and valued for their contributions to life. Their wisdom will be sought out. The whole Human race will be called The People and there will be no more war, sickness or hunger forever” ~Ancient American Indian Prophecy</p>
<p><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Armenian_mythology" title="Armenian mythology">Armenian mythology</a> rainbow - is a belt of <a href="/wiki/Tir_(god)" title="Tir (god)">Tir</a>, which was originally a god Sun, and then - god of knowledge. Eating options are apricot's belt, the belt of Our Lady or the Arch of God.</li>
<li>In a Chinese folktale, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hsienpo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hsienpo (page does not exist)">Hsienpo</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yingt%E2%80%99ai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yingt’ai (page does not exist)">Yingt’ai</a> are star-crossed lovers who must wait until the rainbow appears to be alone together. Hsienpo is the red in the rainbow, and Yingt’ai is the blue. <a href="/wiki/Hong_(rainbow-dragon)" title="Hong (rainbow-dragon)">Hong</a> is another name for the rainbow with various associated legends and interpretation.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumu_(people)" title="Sumu (people)">Sumu</a> of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> "may simply refer to the rainbow as <i>walasa aniwe</i>, ‘the devil is vexed’". These people hide their children in their huts to keep them from looking or pointing at the rainbow.</li>
<li>The Fang of <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> (Africa) are initiated into the religion by a "transcendent experience when they arrive at the rainbow's center, for there they can see both the entire circle of the rainbow and of the earth, signaling the success of their vision." The Fang also prohibit their children from looking at the rainbow.</li>
<li>The Ewe of <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> (Africa) who <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=luiBMwEkurk" rel="nofollow">practice</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vodun" title="Vodun">Vodun</a> have a <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dv6EmX7dYLg" rel="nofollow">temple</a> dedicated to the big python <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dagbe_Dre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dagbe Dre (page does not exist)">Dagbe Dre</a> and small python <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dagbe_Kpohoun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dagbe Kpohoun (page does not exist)">Dagbe Kpohoun</a>, related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxumare" title="Oxumare">Oxumare</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_religion" title="Yoruba religion">Yoruban</a> <a href="/wiki/If%C3%A1" title="Ifá">Ifá</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Afro-American_religion" title="Afro-American religion">diasporas</a>. By way of Haiti the <a href="/wiki/Loa" title="Loa">Loa</a> is <a href="/wiki/Ayida-Weddo" title="Ayida-Weddo">Ayida-Weddo</a> as also the <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160126223814/http://www.afrikaworld.net/afrel/zinzindohoue.htm" rel="nofollow">vodun</a> in Benin.</li>
</ul>

A double rainbow features reversed colors in the outer (secondary) bow, not unlike the rainbow Morpheus leaves behind at the end of Chapter 2 of Brief Lives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>For <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhists</a>, the rainbow is "the highest state achievable before attaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nirvana_(concept)" title="Nirvana (concept)">Nirvana</a>, where individual desire and consciousness are extinguished."</li>
<li>For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karens" title="Karens">Karens</a> of Burma, the rainbow is considered as a painted and dangerous demon that eats children.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izanami" title="Izanami">Izanami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Izanagi" title="Izanagi">Izanagi</a>, the male and female creators of the world in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_mythology" title="Japanese mythology">Japanese</a> myth, "descended on the Floating Bridge of Heaven to create land from the ocean of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a>." In many texts this bridge is known as a rainbow.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaman" title="Shaman">Shamans</a> among <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Buryats" title="Buryats">Buryats</a> speak of ascending to the sky-spirit world by way of the rainbow.</li>
<li>In mythology of ancient <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a>, a man touched by the rainbow is drawn to heaven, and becomes a "Planetnik" - half-demonic creature - which is under the power of the thunder and lightning god <a href="/wiki/Perun" title="Perun">Perun</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> legends, it is said that if you walk beneath a rainbow, you will change genders: if a man, you'll begin to think like a woman, and if a woman, you'll begin to think like a man. While most Bulgarians don't believe in the superstition, some of them tease each other and joke around. They might also say "The sun is shining, the rain is falling, a bear is getting married" (roughly translated-in the original verse, the last part rhymes).</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, a common legend asserts that a "pot of gold" is to be found at the end of a rainbow, for the person lucky enough to find it. This treasure is, however, guarded by a <a href="/wiki/Leprechaun" title="Leprechaun">Leprechaun</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_religion" title="Mesoamerican religion">Mesoamerican</a> cultures, <a href="/wiki/Ixchel" title="Ixchel">Ix Chel</a> is a maternal jaguar goddess associated with rain. Chel means rainbow in the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Yucatán">Yucatán</a> <a href="/wiki/Poqomchi%27_people" title="Poqomchi' people">Poqomchi'</a> language. Ix Chel wears a serpent headdress and presides principally over birth and healing.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazonian</a> cultures, rainbows have long been associated with malign spirits that cause harm, such as miscarriages and (especially) skin problems. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amuesha_language" title="Amuesha language">Amuesha language</a> of central <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, certain diseases are called <i>ayona’achartan</i>, meaning "the rainbow hurt my skin". A tradition of closing one's mouth at the sight of a rainbow in order to avoid disease appears to pre-date the Incan empire.</li>
<li>In pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_mythology" title="Arabian mythology">Arabian mythology</a>, the rainbow is the bow of a weather god, <a href="/wiki/Quzah" title="Quzah">Quzaḥ</a>, whose name survives in the Arabic word for rainbow, <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">قوس قزح</span> <i>qaws Quzaḥ</i>, 'the bow of Quzaḥ'.</li>
<li>Rainbows are widely seen in Native American stories and prophecies. The <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> believe the rainbow forms the hem of the sun's coat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the myth of U Lakriah and Niaw wasa(Jaintias of Meghalaya, India) Rainbow is God's image which appeared before U Lakriah,</li>
</ul>
