<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, "precipitation types" can include the character or phase of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> which is falling to ground level. There are three distinct ways that precipitation can occur. <b>Convective</b> precipitation is generally more intense, and of shorter duration, than <b>stratiform</b> precipitation. <b>Orographic</b> precipitation occurs when moist air is forced upwards over rising terrain, such as a mountain.</p>
<p>Precipitation can also fall in either liquid or solid phases, or transition between them. Liquid forms of precipitation include rain and drizzle. Rain or drizzle which freezes on contact within a subfreezing air mass gains the preceding adjective "freezing", becoming known as freezing rain or freezing drizzle. Frozen forms of precipitation include <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">ice needles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">sleet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">graupel</a>. Intensity is classified either by rate of fall, or by visibility restriction.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phases">Phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Precipitation falls in many forms, or phases. They can be subdivided into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Liquid precipitation:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drizzle" title="Drizzle">Drizzle</a> (DZ)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rain</a> (RA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fog</a> <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> on <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foliage" title="Foliage">foliage</a>, dripping on <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPMC_.26_CNRS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-UPMC_.26_CNRS-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Freezing precipitation:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freezing_drizzle" title="Freezing drizzle">Freezing drizzle</a> (FZDZ)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">Freezing rain</a> (FZRA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rain_and_snow_mixed" title="Rain and snow mixed">Rain and snow mixed</a> / "Snain" (RASN)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sunshower" title="Sunshower">Sun Shower</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Frozen precipitation:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">Snow</a> (SN)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snow_grains" title="Snow grains">Snow grains</a> (SG)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">Ice pellets / Sleet</a> (PL)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">Hail</a> (GR)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">Snow pellets / Graupel</a> (GS)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">Ice crystals</a> (IC)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The parenthesized letters are the <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> codes for each phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metar_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metar-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Precipitation occurs when the air becomes saturated with water vapor, and can no longer hold all of it in vapor form. This occurs when moist air cools, usually when an airmass rises through the atmosphere. However, an airmass can also cool (e.g. through <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">radiative cooling</a>, or ground contact with cold terrain) without a change in altitude.</p>
<p><i>Convective</i> precipitation occurs when air rises vertically through the (temporarily) self-sustaining mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. <i>Stratiform</i> precipitation occurs when large air masses rise diagonally as larger-scale atmospheric dynamics force them to move over each other. <i>Orographic</i> precipitation is similar, except the upwards motion is forced when a moving airmass encounters the rising slope of a landform such as a mountain ridge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convectional">Convectional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Convectional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Desert_Electric.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2441" data-file-width="3887" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Desert_Electric.jpg/220px-Desert_Electric.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Desert_Electric.jpg/330px-Desert_Electric.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Desert_Electric.jpg/440px-Desert_Electric.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Desert_Electric.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Violent electrical storm results from convective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a> clouds.</div>

<p>Convection occurs when the Earth's surface, within a conditionally unstable or moist <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, becomes heated more than its surroundings, leading to significant evaporation. Convective rain, or showery precipitation, occurs from convective clouds, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a>. It falls as showers with rapidly changing intensity. Convective precipitation falls over a certain area for a relatively short time, as convective clouds have limited horizontal extent. Most precipitation in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> appears to be convective; however, it has been suggested that stratiform precipitation thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geerts_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geerts-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">Graupel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> indicate convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In mid-latitudes, convective precipitation is associated with <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a> (often behind the front), <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>, and warm fronts in very moist air.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclonic">Cyclonic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cyclonic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">Weather fronts</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Warmfrontai.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="224" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Warmfrontai.svg/220px-Warmfrontai.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Warmfrontai.svg/330px-Warmfrontai.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Warmfrontai.svg/440px-Warmfrontai.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Warmfrontai.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A weather front is the boundary of two airmasses</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Frontal precipitation is caused by frontal systems surrounding <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> or lows, which form when warm and often tropical air meets cooler air. Frontal precipitation typically falls out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nimbostratus" title="Nimbostratus">nimbostratus</a> clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>When masses of air with different density (moisture and temperature characteristics) meet, less dense warmer air overrides colder air. The warmer air is forced to rise and if conditions are right becomes saturated, causing precipitation. In turn, precipitation can enhance the temperature and moisture contrast along a frontal boundary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2015)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Passing weather fronts cause sudden changes in general temperature, and in the <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> in the air at ground level.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">Warm fronts</a> occur where the warm air pushes out a previously lodged cold air mass. The warm air overrides the cooler air and moves upward. Warm fronts are followed by extended periods of light rain and drizzle, because, after the warm air rises above the cooler air (which remains on the ground), it gradually cools due to the air's expansion while being lifted, which forms clouds and leads to precipitation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">Cold fronts</a> occur when a mass of cooler air dislodges a mass of warm air. This type of transition is sharper,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2015)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> since cold air is more dense than warm air. The rain duration is shorter, and generally more intense, than that which occurs ahead of warm fronts.</p>
<p>A wide variety of weather can be found along an <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded front</a>, with thunderstorms possible, but usually their passage is associated with a drying of the air mass.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">Orographic lift</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">Orographic</a> or relief rainfall is caused when masses of air pushed by <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> are forced up the side of elevated land formations, such as large <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>. The lift of the air up the side of the mountain results in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">adiabatic</a> cooling, and ultimately condensation and precipitation. In mountainous parts of the world subjected to relatively consistent winds (for example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a>), a more moist <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> usually prevails on the windward side of a mountain than on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeward" title="Leeward">leeward</a> (downwind) side. Moisture is removed by orographic lift, leaving drier air (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foehn" title="Foehn">Foehn</a>) on the descending (generally warming), leeward side where a <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> is observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Wai%CA%BBale%CA%BBale" title="Mount Waiʻaleʻale">Mount Waiʻaleʻale</a> (<i>Wai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ale<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>ale</i>), on the island of Kauai, is notable for its extreme rainfall. It has the highest average annual rainfall on Earth, with 460 inches (12,000 mm) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Storm systems affect the region with heavy rains between October and March. Local climates vary considerably on each island due to their topography, divisible into windward (<i>Ko<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>olau</i>) and leeward (<i>Kona</i>) regions based upon location relative to the higher mountains. Windward sides face the east-to-northeast <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> and receive much more rainfall; leeward sides are drier and sunnier, with less rain and less cloud cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On the island of Oahu, clouds and often rain can usually be observed around the windward mountain peaks, while the southern parts of the island (including most of Honolulu and Waikiki) receive dramatically less rainfall throughout the year.</p>
<p>In South America, the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> mountain range blocks <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> moisture that arrives on the continent, resulting in a desert-like climate just downwind across western Argentina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range creates the same effect in North America, causing the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> desert,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensity">Intensity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Intensity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Precipitation is measured using a <a href="/wiki/Rain_gauge" title="Rain gauge">rain gauge</a>. When classified according to the rate of precipitation, rain can be divided into categories. Light rain describes rainfall which falls at a rate of between a trace and 2.5 millimetres (0.098 in) per hour. Moderate rain describes rainfall with a precipitation rate of between 2.6 millimetres (0.10 in) and 7.6 millimetres (0.30 in) per hour. Heavy rain describes rainfall with a precipitation rate above 7.6 millimetres (0.30 in) per hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Snowfall intensity is classified in terms of <a href="/wiki/Visibility" title="Visibility">visibility</a>. When the visibility is over 1 kilometre (0.62 mi), snow is determined to be light. Moderate snow describes snowfall with visibility restrictions between .5 kilometres (0.31 mi) and 1 kilometre (0.62 mi). Heavy snowfall describes conditions when visibility is restricted below .5 kilometres (0.31 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
