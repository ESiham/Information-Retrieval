<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yellow rain</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p><b>Yellow rain</b> was the subject of a 1981 political incident in which the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">United States Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Haig" title="Alexander Haig">Alexander Haig</a> accused the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> of supplying <a href="/wiki/T-2_mycotoxin" title="T-2 mycotoxin">T-2 mycotoxin</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Communist_state" title="Communist state">Communist states</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> for use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterinsurgency" title="Counterinsurgency">counterinsurgency</a> warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-1">[1]</a></sup> Refugees described many different forms of attacks, including a sticky yellow liquid falling from planes or helicopters, which was dubbed "yellow rain". The U.S. government alleged that over ten thousand people had been killed in attacks using these <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical weapons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wannemacher_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wannemacher-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Soviets denied these claims and an initial <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> investigation was inconclusive. Some samples of the supposed chemical agent that were supplied to a group of independent scientists turned out to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honeybee" title="Honeybee">honeybee</a> feces, suggesting that the "yellow rain" was due to mass defecation of digested pollen grains from large swarms of bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haig_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-haig-3">[3]</a></sup> Other scientists questioned the accuracy of the refugee accounts and the reliability of the chemical analyses presented by the U.S. government.</p>
<p>The majority of the scientific literature on this topic now regards the hypothesis that yellow rain was a Soviet chemical weapon as disproved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madden_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madden-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopher_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christopher-5">[5]</a></sup> However, the issue remains disputed and the U.S. government has not retracted these allegations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-6">[6]</a></sup> arguing that the issue has not been fully resolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wannemacher_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wannemacher-2">[2]</a></sup> Many of the U.S. documents relating to this incident remain classified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations">Allegations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Allegations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The charges stemmed from events in Laos and North Vietnam beginning in 1975, when the two governments, which were allied with and supported by the Soviet Union, fought against <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a> tribes, peoples who had sided with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and South Vietnam during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. Refugees described events that they believed to be <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a> attacks by low-flying aircraft or helicopters; several of the reports were of a yellow, oily liquid that was dubbed "yellow rain". Those exposed claimed <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurological</a> and physical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symptoms" title="Symptoms">symptoms</a> including seizures, blindness, and bleeding. Similar reports came from the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnamese</a> invasion of <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1997 textbook produced by the U.S. Army Medical Department asserted that over ten thousand people were killed in attacks using chemical weapons in Laos, Cambodia and Afghanistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wannemacher_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wannemacher-2">[2]</a></sup> The descriptions of the attacks were diverse and included air-dropped canisters and sprays, booby traps, artillery shells, rockets and grenades that produced droplets of liquid, dust, powders, smoke or "insect-like" materials of a yellow, red, green, white or brown color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wannemacher_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wannemacher-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Haig" title="Alexander Haig">Alexander Haig</a> announced in September 1981 that:</p>
<dl>
<dd>For some time now, the international community has been alarmed by continuing reports that the Soviet Union and its allies have been using lethal chemical weapons in Laos, Kampuchea, and Afghanistan. ... We have now found physical evidence from Southeast Asia which has been analyzed and found to contain abnormally high levels of three potent mycotoxins—poisonous substances not indigenous to the region and which are highly toxic to man and animals.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> described these accusations as a "big lie" and in turn accused the US government of using chemical weapons during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnam_war" title="Vietnam war">Vietnam war</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The American accusations prompted a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> investigation in Pakistan and Thailand. This involved five doctors and scientists who interviewed alleged witnesses and collected samples that were purported to come from Afghanistan and Cambodia. However, the interviews produced conflicting testimony and the analyses of the samples were inconclusive. The UN experts also examined two refugees who claimed to be suffering from the after-effects of a chemical attack, but the refugees were instead diagnosed as having fungal skin infections. The team reported that they were unable to verify that chemical weapons had been used but noted that circumstantial evidence "suggestive of the possible use of some sort of toxic chemical substance in some instances."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The US mycotoxin analyses were reported in the scientific literature in 1983 and 1984 and reported small amounts of mycotoxins called <a href="/wiki/Trichothecene" title="Trichothecene">trichothecenes</a>, ranging from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> to traces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">parts per billion</a> range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mirocha_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mirocha-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The lowest possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limit_of_detection" title="Limit of detection">limit of detection</a> in these mycotoxin analyses is in the parts per billion range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, several inconsistencies in these reports caused a "prolonged, and at times acrimonious, debate on the validity of the analyses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black-13">[13]</a></sup> A 2003 medical review notes that this debate may have been exacerbated since "Although analytical methods were in their infancy during the controversy, they were still sensitive enough to pick up low levels of environmental trichothecene contamination."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_investigation">Initial investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initial investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>C. J. Mirocha at the University of Minnesota conducted a biochemical investigation, looking for the presence of trichothecene mycotoxins, including T-2 toxin, <a href="/wiki/Diacetoxyscirpenol" title="Diacetoxyscirpenol">diacetoxyscirpenol</a> (DAS), and deoxynivalenol (DON).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mirocha_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mirocha-10">[10]</a></sup> This included chemical analyses of blood, urine, and tissue of alleged victims of chemical attacks in February 1982 in Laos and Kampuchea. "The finding of T-2, HT-2, and DAS toxins in blood, urine, and body tissues of alleged victims of chemical warfare in Southeast Asia provides compelling proof of the use of trichothecenes as nonconventional warfare agents. ... Additional significant findings lie in the trichothecenes found in the leaf samples (T-2, DON, nivalenol) and yellow powder (T-2, DAS). ... The most compelling evidence is the presence of T-2 and DAS in the yellow powder. Both toxins are infrequently found in nature and rarely occur together. In our experience, copious producers of T-2 toxin (<i>F. tricinctum</i>) do not produce DAS, and conversely, good producers of DAS (<i>F. roseum</i> 'Gibbosum') do not produce T-2."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mirocha_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mirocha-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_explanation">Alternative explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternative explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honeybee_hypothesis">Honeybee hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honeybee hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1983, these charges were disputed by Harvard biologist and biological weapons opponent <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Meselson" title="Matthew Meselson">Matthew Meselson</a> and his team, who traveled to Laos and conducted a separate investigation. Meselson's team noted that trichothecene mycotoxins occur naturally in the region and questioned the witness testimony. He suggested an alternate hypothesis that the yellow rain was the harmless fecal matter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honeybee" title="Honeybee">honeybees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haig_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-haig-3">[3]</a></sup> The Meselson team offered the following as evidence: separate "yellow rain drops" which occurred on the same leaf, and which were "accepted as authentic", consisted largely of <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>; each drop contained a different mix of pollen grains, as one would expect if they came from different bees, and the grains showed properties characteristic of pollen digested by bees (the protein inside the pollen grain was gone, while the outer indigestible shell remained).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Further, the pollen mix came from plant species typical of the area where a drop was collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall1986_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall1986-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beedroppings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Beedroppings.jpg/220px-Beedroppings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Beedroppings.jpg/330px-Beedroppings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Beedroppings.jpg/440px-Beedroppings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beedroppings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bee droppings resembling "yellow rain"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The US government responded to these findings by arguing that the pollen was added deliberately, in order to make a substance that could be easily inhaled and "ensure the retention of toxins in the human body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robinson_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-18">[18]</a></sup> Meselson responded to this idea by stating that it was rather far-fetched to imagine that somebody would produce a chemical weapon by "gathering pollen predigested by honeybees."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall1986_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall1986-17">[17]</a></sup> The fact that the pollen originated in Southeast Asia meant that the Soviet Union could not have manufactured the substance domestically, and would have had to import tons of pollen from Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barton2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-barton2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:35</sup> Meselson's work was described in an independent medical review as providing "compelling evidence that yellow rain might have a benign natural explanation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the honeybee hypothesis was made public, a literature search turned up an earlier Chinese paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> on the phenomenon of yellow droppings in Jiangsu Province in September 1976. Strikingly, the Chinese villagers had also used the term "yellow rain" to describe this phenomenon. Many villagers believed that the yellow droppings were portents of imminent earthquake activity. Others believed that the droppings were chemical weapons sprayed by the Soviet Union or Taiwan. However, the Chinese scientists also concluded that the droppings came from bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whiteside2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-whiteside2-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:46</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mycotoxins">Mycotoxins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mycotoxins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Analyses of putative "yellow rain" samples by the British, French and Swedish governments confirmed the presence of pollen and failed to find any trace of mycotoxins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robinson_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Toxicology studies questioned the reliability of reports stating that mycotoxins had been detected in alleged victims up to two months after exposure, since these compounds are unstable in the body and are cleared from the blood in just a few hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Black-13">[13]</a></sup> An autopsy on a <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Rouge" title="Khmer Rouge">Khmer Rouge</a> fighter named Chan Mann, a victim of a putative yellow rain attack in 1982, turned up traces of mycotoxins, but also <a href="/wiki/Aflatoxin" title="Aflatoxin">aflatoxin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_fever" title="Blackwater fever">Blackwater fever</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barton2006_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-barton2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup></p>
<p>Surveys also showed that both mycotoxin-producing fungi and mycotoxin contamination were common in Southeast Asia, casting doubt on the assertion that detecting these compounds was an unusual occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desjardins_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Desjardins-24">[24]</a></sup> For example, a Canadian military laboratory found mycotoxins in the blood of five people from the area who had never been exposed to yellow rain, out of 270 tested, but none in the blood of ten alleged victims,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall1986_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall1986-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barss_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barss-25">[25]</a></sup> and a 1988 paper reported that illnesses from mycotoxin exposure may pose a serious threat to public health in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It is now recognized that mycotoxin contamination of foods such as wheat and maize is a common problem, particularly in temperate regions of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desjardins_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Desjardins-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As noted in a 2003 medical review, "The government research highlighted, if nothing else, that natural mycotoxicoses were an important health hazard in Southeast Asia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability_of_eyewitness_accounts">Reliability of eyewitness accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reliability of eyewitness accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1987 the <i>New York Times</i> reported that Freedom of Information requests showed that field investigations in 1983–85 by US government teams had produced no evidence to substantiate the initial allegations and instead cast doubt on the reliability of the initial reports, but these critical reports were not released to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A 1989 analysis of the initial reports gathered from Hmong refugees that was published in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i> noted "marked inconsistencies that greatly compromised the validity of the testimony" and criticized the methods used in interviews by the US Army medical team that gathered this information. These issues included the US Army team only interviewing those people who claimed to have knowledge of attacks with chemical weapons and the investigators asking leading questions during interviews. The authors noted that individuals' stories changed over time, were inconsistent with other accounts, and that the people who claimed to have been eyewitnesses when first interviewed later stated that they had been relaying the accounts of others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, Meselson had visited a Hmong refugee camp with samples of bee droppings that he had collected in Thailand. Most of the Hmong he interviewed claimed that these were samples of the chemical weapons that they had been attacked with. One man accurately identified them as insect droppings, but switched to the chemical weapons story after discussion with fellow Hmong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whiteside2_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-whiteside2-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:46</sup></p>
<p>Australian military scientist Rod Barton visited Thailand in 1984, and discovered that Thai villagers were blaming yellow rain for a variety of ailments, including scabies. An American doctor in Bangkok explained that the United States had been taking a special interest in yellow rain, and was providing medical care to alleged victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barton2006_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-barton2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:39–40</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_U.S._origin">Possible U.S. origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible U.S. origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A CIA report from the 1960s reported allegations by the Cambodian government that their forces had been attacked with chemical weapons, leaving behind a yellow powder. The Cambodians blamed the United States for these alleged chemical attacks. Some of the samples of "yellow rain" collected from Cambodia in 1983 tested positive for <a href="/wiki/CS_gas" title="CS gas">CS</a>, which the United States had used during the Vietnam War. CS is a form of tear gas and is not toxic, but may account for some of the milder symptoms reported by the Hmong villagers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barton2006_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-barton2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:39</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_conclusions">Disputed conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disputed conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, two main viewpoints exist on the yellow rain controversy. One viewpoint sees these allegations as supported by insufficient evidence, or as having been completely refuted. For instance, a 1992 review published in <i>Politics and the Life Sciences</i> described the idea of yellow rain as a biological agent as conclusively disproved and called for an assessment by the US government of the mistakes made in this episode, stating that "the present approach of sweeping the matter under the rug and hoping people will forget about it could be counterproductive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barss_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barss-25">[25]</a></sup> Similarly, a 1997 review of the history of biological warfare published in the <i>Journal of the American Medical Association</i> stated that the yellow rain allegations are "widely regarded as erroneous",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopher_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christopher-5">[5]</a></sup> a 2001 review in the <i><a href="/wiki/Annual_Reviews_(publisher)" title="Annual Reviews (publisher)">Annual Reviews in Microbiology</a></i> described them as "unsubstantiated for many reasons",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and a 2003 article in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annual_Review_of_Phytopathology" title="Annual Review of Phytopathology">Annual Review of Phytopathology</a></i> described them as "largely discredited".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madden_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Madden-4">[4]</a></sup> A 2003 review of the history of <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> described these allegations as one of many cases where states have produced <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> containing false or unsubstantiated accusations of the use of biological weapons by their enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, as of 1997 the US Army maintains that some experts believe that "trichothecenes were used as biological weapons in Southeast Asia and Afghanistan" although they write that "it has not been possible for the United States to prove unequivocally that trichothecene mycotoxins were used as biological weapons." They argued that presence of pollen in yellow rain samples is best explained by the idea that "during biological warfare attacks, dispersed trichothecenes landed in pollen-containing areas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wannemacher_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wannemacher-2">[2]</a></sup> (Essentially the same position is taken in a subsequent volume in the same series of US Army textbooks published in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>) Similarly, the US <a href="/wiki/Defense_Threat_Reduction_Agency" title="Defense Threat Reduction Agency">Defense Threat Reduction Agency</a> argues that the controversy has not been resolved and states that a CIA report indicated the Soviet Union did possess weapons based on T-2 mycotoxin, although the agency states that "no trace of a trichothecene-containing weapon was ever found in the areas affected by yellow rain" and concludes that the use of such weapons "may never be unequivocally proved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A 2007 review published in <i>Politics and the Life Sciences</i> concluded that the balance of evidence strongly supported the hypothesis that some type of chemical or biological weapon was used in Southeast Asia in the late 1970s and early 1980s, but noted that they found no definitive proof of this hypothesis and that the evidence could not "identify the specific agents used, the intent, or the root source or sources of the attacks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Vietnamese and the Soviets have also reportedly used other chemical weapons in conflict, in Cambodia and Afghanistan, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_events">Later events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An episode of mass pollen release from bees in 2002 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sangrampur,_India" title="Sangrampur, India">Sangrampur, India</a>, prompted unfounded fears of a chemical weapons attack, although this was in fact due to a mass migration of <a href="/wiki/Apis_dorsata" title="Apis dorsata">giant Asian honeybees</a>. This event revived memories of what <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> described as "cold war paranoia", and the article noted that the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> had covered these 1980s yellow rain allegations in particular detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Indeed, the <i>Wall Street Journal</i> continues to assert that the Soviet Union used yellow rain as a chemical weapon in the 1980s and in 2003 accused Matthew Meselson of "excusing away evidence of Soviet violations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the build-up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_war" title="Iraq war">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> the <i>Wall Street Journal</i> alleged that <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a> possessed a chemical weapon called "yellow rain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Iraqis appear to have investigated trichothecene mycotoxins in 1990, but only purified a total of 20 ml of the agent from fungal cultures and did not manage to scale up the purification or produce any weapons containing these compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Although these toxins are not generally regarded as practical tactical weapons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> the T-2 toxin might be a usable weapon since it can be absorbed through the skin, although it would be very difficult to manufacture it in any reasonable quantity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Henry Wilde, a retired US Foreign Service Officer, has drawn parallels between the use of yellow rain allegations by the US government against the Soviet Union and the later exaggerated allegations on the topic of <a href="/wiki/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Iraq and weapons of mass destruction">Iraq and weapons of mass destruction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilde_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilde-44">[44]</a></sup> Wilde considers it likely that states may again "use rumors and false or planted intelligence of such weapons use for propaganda purposes." and calls for the establishment of a more rigorous inspection process to deal with such claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilde_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilde-44">[44]</a></sup> Similar concerns were expressed in a 2006 review published by the <a href="/wiki/World_Organisation_for_Animal_Health" title="World Organisation for Animal Health">World Organisation for Animal Health</a>, which compared the American yellow rain accusations to other cold-war accusations from the Soviet Union and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, as well as to more recent mistaken intelligence on Iraqi weapons capabilities, concluding that such unjustified accusations have encouraged the development of biological weapons and increased the risk that they might be used, as they have discredited arms-control efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furmanski_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Furmanski-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiolab_interview"><i>Radiolab</i> interview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radiolab interview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012 the science-themed show <i><a href="/wiki/Radiolab" title="Radiolab">Radiolab</a></i> aired an interview with Hmong refugee Eng Yang and his niece, author <a href="/wiki/Kao_Kalia_Yang" title="Kao Kalia Yang">Kao Kalia Yang</a>, to discuss Eng Yang's experience with yellow rain. The hosts took the position that yellow rain was unlikely to have been a chemical agent. The episode prompted a backlash among some listeners, who criticized <a href="/wiki/Robert_Krulwich" title="Robert Krulwich">Robert Krulwich</a> for insensitivity, racism, and their disregard for Yang's personal experience with the region in question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The negative response prompted host Krulwich to issue an apology for his handling of the interview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bulgaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 23, 2015, just before the national holiday of May 24 (the day of Bulgarian writing and culture), yellow rain fell in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sofia,_Bulgaria" title="Sofia, Bulgaria">Sofia, Bulgaria</a>. Suspicions were raised because the Bulgarian government was criticizing Russian actions in Ukraine at the time. The Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/National_academy" title="National academy">national academy</a> BAN—closely related to the Russian RAN—explained the event as flower pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
