<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rain shadow</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the Australian television series, see <a href="/wiki/Rain_Shadow_(TV_series)" title="Rain Shadow (TV series)">Rain Shadow (TV series)</a>.</div>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rainshadow_copy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of a rain shadow.</div>

<p>A <b>rain shadow</b> is a dry area on the <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward#Meteorological_significance" title="Windward and leeward">leeward</a> side of a mountainous area (away from the wind). The mountains block the passage of rain-producing weather systems and cast a "shadow" of dryness behind them. Wind and moist air is drawn by the prevailing winds towards the top of the mountains, where it condenses and precipitates before it crosses the top. The air, without much moisture left, advances across the mountains creating a drier side called the "rain shadow".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The condition exists because warm moist air rises by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_lifting" title="Orographic lifting">orographic lifting</a> to the top of a mountain range. As atmospheric pressure decreases with increasing altitude, the air has expanded and <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process#Adiabatic_heating_and_cooling" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatically cooled</a> to the point that the air reaches its adiabatic <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> (which is not the same as its <a href="/wiki/Isobaric_process" title="Isobaric process">constant pressure</a> dew point commonly reported in weather forecasts). At the adiabatic dew point, moisture condenses onto the mountain and it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitates</a> on the top and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windward" title="Windward">windward</a> sides of the mountain. The air descends on the leeward side, but due to the precipitation it has lost much of its moisture. Typically, descending air also gets warmer because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_compression" title="Adiabatic compression">adiabatic compression</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foehn_winds" title="Foehn winds">Foehn winds</a>) down the leeward side of the mountain, which increases the amount of moisture that it can absorb and creates an <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions_of_notable_rain_shadow">Regions of notable rain shadow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regions of notable rain shadow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himalaya_composite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="981" data-file-width="1947" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Himalaya_composite.jpg/350px-Himalaya_composite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Himalaya_composite.jpg/525px-Himalaya_composite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Himalaya_composite.jpg/700px-Himalaya_composite.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himalaya_composite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> (top), perhaps the best example of a rain shadow. Rain does not make it past the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, leading to an arid climate on the leeward side of the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are regular patterns of <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing winds</a> found in bands round the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> region. The zone designated the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> is the zone between about 30° N and 30° S, blowing predominantly from the northeast in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and from the southeast in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> are the prevailing winds in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a> between 30 and 60 degrees <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, blowing predominantly from the southwest in the Northern Hemisphere and from the northwest in the Southern Hemisphere. The strongest westerly winds in the middle latitudes can come in the <a href="/wiki/Roaring_Forties" title="Roaring Forties">Roaring Forties</a> between 30 and 50 degrees latitude.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Examples of notable rain shadowing include:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg/220px-Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg/330px-Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg/440px-Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agasthiyamalai_range_and_Tirunelveli_rainshadow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agasthiyamalai" title="Agasthiyamalai">Agasthiyamalai</a> hills cut off <a href="/wiki/Tirunelveli" title="Tirunelveli">Tirunelveli</a> (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>, creating a rainshadow region</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The peaks of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a> to the west and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindukush" title="Hindukush">Hindukush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamir</a> to the east rain shadow the <a href="/wiki/Karakum_Desert" title="Karakum Desert">Karakum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyzyl_Kum" title="Kyzyl Kum">Kyzyl Kum</a> deserts east of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid</a> <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe" title="Kazakh Steppe">Kazakh Steppe</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dasht-i-Lut" title="Dasht-i-Lut">Dasht-i-Lut</a> in Iran is in the rain shadow of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elburz_Mountains" title="Elburz Mountains">Elburz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a> and is one of the most lifeless areas on Earth.</li>
<li>The Himalaya and connecting ranges also contribute to arid conditions in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> including <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gobi" title="Gobi">Gobi</a> desert, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid</a> steppes of Mongolia and north-central to north western China.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ordos_Desert" title="Ordos Desert">Ordos Desert</a> is rain shadowed by mountain chains including the Kara-naryn-ula, the Sheitenula, and the <a href="/wiki/Yin_Mountains" title="Yin Mountains">Yin Mountains</a>, which link on to the south end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Khingan_Mountains" title="Great Khingan Mountains">Great Khingan Mountains</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thar_desert" title="Thar desert">Thar desert</a> is bounded and rain shadowed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aravalli" title="Aravalli">Aravalli</a> ranges to the south-east, the Himalaya to the northeast, and the Kirthar and Sulaiman ranges to the west.</li>
<li>Eastern Side of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahyadri" title="Sahyadri">Sahyadri</a> ranges on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deccan" title="Deccan">Deccan</a> e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Karnataka" title="Northern Karnataka">Northern Karnataka</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Solapur" title="Solapur">Solapur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beed" title="Beed">Beed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osmanabad" title="Osmanabad">Osmanabad</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidharba" title="Vidharba">Vidharba</a> Plateau and eastern side of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> (in Tamil Nadu) India.</li>
<li>The central region of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> is in the rain shadow of the <a href="/wiki/Arakan_Mountains" title="Arakan Mountains">Arakan Mountains</a> and is almost semi-arid with only 750 millimetres (30 in) of rain as against as much as 5.5 metres (220 in) on the <a href="/wiki/Rakhine_State" title="Rakhine State">Rakhine State</a> coast.</li>
<li>The Tokyo, Japan plain ("Kanto plain") in the winter months experiences significantly less precipitation than the rest of the country by virtue of surrounding mountain ranges, including the "Japan Alps", blocking prevailing northwesterly winds originating in Siberia.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The High Peaks of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Lebanon" title="Mount Lebanon">Mount Lebanon</a> rain-shadow the northern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Beqaa_Valley" title="Beqaa Valley">Beqaa Valley</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Lebanon_mountains" title="Anti-Lebanon mountains">Anti-Lebanon mountains</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Judaean_Desert" title="Judaean Desert">Judaean Desert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> and the western slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Moab" title="Moab">Moab</a> Mountains on the opposite (<a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordanian</a>) side are rain-shadowed by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judaean_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judaean Hills (page does not exist)">Judaean Hills</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> is the driest non-polar desert on Earth because it is blocked from moisture on both sides (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andes_Mountains" title="Andes Mountains">Andes Mountains</a> to the east block moist Amazon basin air and high pressure over the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> and cold ocean currents stop rain from coming in from the west).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cuyo_(Argentina)" title="Cuyo (Argentina)">Cuyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Eastern Patagonia</a> is rain shadowed from the prevailing westerly winds by the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> range and is arid. The aridity of the lands next to eastern piedmont of the Andes decreases to the south due to a decrease in the height of the Andes with the consequence that the <a href="/wiki/Patagonian_Desert" title="Patagonian Desert">Patagonian Desert</a> develop more fully at the Atlantic coast contributing to shaping the climatic pattern known as the <a href="/wiki/Arid_Diagonal" title="Arid Diagonal">Arid Diagonal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruniard_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruniard-2">[2]</a></sup> The Argentinian wine region of Cuyo and Northern Patagonia is almost completely dependent on irrigation, using water drawn from the many rivers that drain glacial ice from the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Guajira_Peninsula" title="Guajira Peninsula">Guajira Peninsula</a> in northern Colombia is in the rain shadow of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_de_Santa_Marta" title="Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta">Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta</a> and despite its tropical latitude is almost arid, receiving almost no rainfall for seven to eight months of the year and being incapable of cultivation without irrigation.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the largest scale, the entirety of the North American <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plains" title="Interior Plains">Interior Plains</a> are shielded from the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a> carrying moist Pacific weather by the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Cordillera" title="North American Cordillera">North American Cordillera</a>. More pronounced effects are observed, however, in particular valley regions within the Cordillera, in the direct lee of specific mountain ranges. Most rainshadows in the western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> are due to the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_Mountains" title="Cascade Mountains">Cascades</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Champlain_Valley" title="Champlain Valley">Champlain Valley</a> in upstate New York. It is protected by the <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Mountains" title="Adirondack Mountains">Adirondack Mountains</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range Province</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, which includes the dry areas east of the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>, which covers almost all of <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> are rain shadowed. The Cascades also cause rain shadowed <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Basin" title="Columbia Basin">Columbia Basin</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Washington" title="Eastern Washington">Eastern Washington</a> and valleys in British Columbia, Canada - most notably the Thompson and Nicola Valleys which can receive less than 10 inches of rain in parts, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okanagan_Valley" title="Okanagan Valley">Okanagan Valley</a> (particularly the south, nearest to the US border) which receives anywhere from 12-17 inches of rain annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> also has rain shadows, with some areas being desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Orographic lifting produces the world's second-highest annual precipitation record, 12.7 meters (500 inches), on the island of <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>; the leeward side is understandably rain-shadowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiteman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteman-1">[1]</a></sup> The entire island of <a href="/wiki/Kahoolawe" title="Kahoolawe">Kahoolawe</a> lies in the rain shadow of Maui's <a href="/wiki/Haleakal%C4%81" title="Haleakalā">East Maui Volcano</a>.</li>
<li>The east slopes of the Coast Ranges in central and southern California also cut off the southern <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> from enough precipitation to ensure desert-like conditions in areas around <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> and adjacent cities are usually drier than the rest of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> because of the rain shadow cast by the highest part of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Mountains" title="Santa Cruz Mountains">Santa Cruz Mountains</a>.</li>
<li>The Dungeness Valley around <a href="/wiki/Sequim,_Washington" title="Sequim, Washington">Sequim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> lies in the rain shadow of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Mountains" title="Olympic Mountains">Olympic Mountains</a>. The area averages 10–15 inches of rain per year, less than half of the amount received in nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Angeles" title="Port Angeles">Port Angeles</a> and approximately 10% of that which falls in <a href="/wiki/Forks,_Washington" title="Forks, Washington">Forks</a> on the western side of the mountains. To a lesser extent, this rain shadow extends to other parts of the eastern Olympic Peninsula, Whidbey Island, and parts of the San Juan Islands and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> around <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">Victoria, British Columbia</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Rock_Desert" title="Black Rock Desert">Black Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert" title="Chihuahuan Desert">Chihuahuan</a> deserts all are in regions which are rain shadowed.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Owens_Valley" title="Owens Valley">Owens Valley</a> in the United States, behind the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range in California.</li>
<li>The aptly named <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a> in the United States, behind both the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Ranges" title="Pacific Coast Ranges">Pacific Coast Ranges</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range, is the driest place in North America and one of the driest places on the planet. This is also due to its location well below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> which tends to cause high pressure and dry conditions to dominate due to the greater weight of the atmosphere above.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_Front_Range" title="Colorado Front Range">Colorado Front Range</a> is limited to precipitation that crosses over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Divide" title="Continental Divide">Continental Divide</a>. While many locations west of the Divide may receive as much as 40 inches (1,000 mm) of precipitation per year, some places on the eastern side, notably the cities of <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pueblo,_Colorado" title="Pueblo, Colorado">Pueblo, Colorado</a>, typically receive only about 12 to 19 inches. Thus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Divide" title="Continental Divide">Continental Divide</a> acts as a barrier for precipitation. This effect applies only to storms traveling west-to-east. When low pressure systems skirt the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and approach from the south, they can generate high precipitation on the eastern side and little or none on the western slope.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a> of Virginia, wedged between the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Mountains" title="Blue Ridge Mountains">Blue Ridge Mountains</a> and partially shielded from moisture from the west and southeast, is much drier than the very humid remainder of Virginia and the American Southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yellowknife" title="Yellowknife">Yellowknife</a>, the capital and most populous city in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, is located in the rain shadow of the mountain ranges to the west of the city.</li>
<li>On the islands of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, the southwestern sides are in the rain shadow of the trade winds and can receive as little as 400 millimetres (16 in) per year as against over 2,000 millimetres (79 in) on the northeastern, windward sides and over 5,000 millimetres (200 in) over some highland areas.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> of Northern England, Welsh mountains, <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highlands_of_Scotland" title="Highlands of Scotland">Highlands of Scotland</a> create a rain shadow that includes most of the eastern United Kingdom, due to the prevailing south-westerly winds. <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, for example, receive around double the rainfall of <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> respectively (although there are no mountains between Edinburgh and Glasgow). The contrast is even stronger further north, where <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> gets around a third of the rainfall of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_William,_Scotland" title="Fort William, Scotland">Fort William</a> or <a href="/wiki/Skye" title="Skye">Skye</a>. The Fens of East Anglia receive similar rainfall amounts to Seville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_Mountains" title="Cantabrian Mountains">Cantabrian Mountains</a> form a sharp divide between "<a href="/wiki/Green_Spain" title="Green Spain">Green Spain</a>" to the north and the dry central plateau. The northern-facing slopes receive heavy rainfall from the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>, but the southern slopes are in rain shadow. The most evident effect on the Iberian Peninsula occurs in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_(province)" title="Almería (province)">Almería</a>, <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">Murcia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alicante_(province)" title="Alicante (province)">Alicante</a> areas, each with an average rainfall of 300 mm, which are the driest spots in Europe (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabo_de_Gata" title="Cabo de Gata">Cabo de Gata</a>) mostly a result of the mountain range running through their western side, which blocks the westerlies.</li>
<li>Some valleys in the inner <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> are also strongly rainshadowed by the high surrounding mountains: the areas of <a href="/wiki/Gap,_Hautes-Alpes" title="Gap, Hautes-Alpes">Gap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian%C3%A7on" title="Briançon">Briançon</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the district of <a href="/wiki/Zernez" title="Zernez">Zernez</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.</li>
<li>The eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenean mountains</a> in the south of France (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerdagne" title="Cerdagne">Cerdagne</a>).</li>
<li>The Plains of <a href="/wiki/Limagne" title="Limagne">Limagne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forez" title="Forez">Forez</a> in the northern <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">Massif Central</a>, France, are also relatively rainshadowed (mostly the plain of Limagne, shadowed by the <a href="/wiki/Cha%C3%AEne_des_Puys" title="Chaîne des Puys">Chaîne des Puys</a> (up to 2000 mm of rain a year on the summits and below 600mm at <a href="/wiki/Clermont-Ferrand" title="Clermont-Ferrand">Clermont-Ferrand</a>, which is one of the driest places in the country).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> wine region of northern Italy is rainshadowed by the mountains that surround it on nearly every side: <a href="/wiki/Asti" title="Asti">Asti</a> receives only 527 mm of precipitation per year, making it one of the driest places in mainland Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The valley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vardar_River" title="Vardar River">Vardar River</a> and south from <a href="/wiki/Skopje" title="Skopje">Skopje</a> to <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> is in the rain shadow of the <a href="/wiki/Prokletije" title="Prokletije">Prokletije</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pindus_Mountains" title="Pindus Mountains">Pindus Mountains</a>. On its windward side the Prokletije has the highest rainfall in Europe at around 5,000 millimetres (200 in) with small glaciers even at mean annual temperatures well above 0 °C (32 °F), but the leeward side receives as little as 400 millimetres (16 in).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Mountains" title="Scandinavian Mountains">Scandinavian Mountains</a> create a rain shadow for lowland areas east of the mountain chain and prevents the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a> from penetrating further east; thus <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>, west of the mountains, receives 2,250 mm precipitation annually while <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> receives only 760 mm, and <a href="/wiki/Skj%C3%A5k" title="Skjåk">Skjåk</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Norway" title="Municipalities of Norway">municipality</a> situated in a deep valley, receives only 280 mm.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The windward side of the island of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, which sees easterly on-shore winds, is wet tropical, while the western and southern sides of the island lie in the rain shadow of the central highlands and are home to thorn forests and deserts. The same is true for the island of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Point,_Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Sandy Point, Tristan da Cunha">Sandy Point</a> on the east coast is warmer and drier than the rainy, windswept settlement of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_of_the_Seven_Seas" title="Edinburgh of the Seven Seas">Edinburgh</a> in the west.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Cape_Province" title="Western Cape Province">Western Cape Province</a>, the Breede River Valley and the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a> lie in the rain shadow of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Mountains" title="Cape Fold Mountains">Cape Fold Mountains</a> and are arid; whereas the wettest parts of the Cape Mountains can receive 1,500 millimetres (59 in), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worcester,_South_Africa" title="Worcester, South Africa">Worcester</a> receives only around 200 millimetres (8 in) and is useful only for grazing.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> is made even drier because of two strong rain shadow effects caused by some major mountains ranges (whose highest points can culminate to more than 4,000 meters high). To the northwest, the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a>, covering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> coast for <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> as well as to the southeast with the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>. On the windward side of the Atlas Mountains, the warm, moist winds blowing from the northwest off the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> which contain a lot of water vapor are forced to rise, lift up and expand over the mountain range. This causes them to cool down, which causes an excess of moisture to condense into high clouds and results in heavy precipitation over the mountain range. This is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_rainfall" title="Orographic rainfall">orographic rainfall</a> and after this process, the air is dry because it has lost most of its moisture over the Atlas Mountains. On the leeward side, the cold, dry air starts to descend and to sink and compress, making the winds warm up. This warming causes the moisture to evaporate, making clouds disappear. This prevents rainfall formation and creates desert conditions in the Sahara. The same phenomenon occurs in the Ethiopian Highlands, but this rain shadow effect is even more pronounced because this mountain range is larger, with the tropical <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">Monsoon of South Asia</a> coming from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and from the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>. These produce clouds and rainfall on the windward side of the mountains, but the leeward side stays rain shadowed and extremely dry. This second extreme rain shadow effect partially explains the extreme aridity of the eastern Sahara Desert, which is the driest and the sunniest place on the planet. Similar levels of aridity and dryness are only seen in the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.</li>
<li>Desert regions in the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a>) such as the <a href="/wiki/Danakil_Desert" title="Danakil Desert">Danakil Desert</a> are all influenced by the air heating and drying produced by rain shadow effect of the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a>, too.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> lies astride the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>, between 19° and 23° south latitude. The climate of the islands is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a>, and rainfall is brought by trade winds from the east. The western side of the Grande Terre lies in the rain shadow of the central mountains, and rainfall averages are significantly lower.</li>
<li>In the South Island of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> is to be found one of the most remarkable rain shadows anywhere on Earth. The Southern Alps intercept moisture coming off the Tasman Sea, precipitating about 6,300 mm (250 in) to 8,900 mm (350 in) liquid water equivalent per year and creating large glaciers. To the east of the Southern Alps, scarcely 50 km (30 mi) from the snowy peaks, yearly rainfall drops to less than 760 mm (30 in) and some areas less than 380 mm (15 in). (see <a href="/wiki/Nor%27west_arch" title="Nor'west arch">Nor'west arch</a> for more on this subject).</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, one of the states of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the central Midlands region is in a strong rain shadow and receives only about a fifth as much rainfall as the highlands to the west.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> (both states of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monaro,_New_South_Wales" title="Monaro, New South Wales">Monaro</a> is shielded by both the Snowy Mountains to the northwest and coastal ranges to the southeast. Consequently, parts of it are as dry as the wheat-growing lands of those states.</li>
<li>Also in Victoria, the western side of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Phillip_Bay" title="Port Phillip Bay">Port Phillip Bay</a> is in the rain shadow of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otway_Ranges" title="Otway Ranges">Otway Ranges</a>. The area between <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Werribee" title="Werribee">Werribee</a> is the driest part of southern Victoria: the crest of the Otway Ranges receives 2,000 millimetres (79 in) of rain per year and has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myrtle_beech" title="Myrtle beech">myrtle beech</a> rainforests much further west than anywhere else, whilst the area around <a href="/wiki/Little_River,_Victoria" title="Little River, Victoria">Little River</a> receives as little as 425 millimetres (16.7 in) annually, which is as little as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nhill,_Victoria" title="Nhill, Victoria">Nhill</a> or <a href="/wiki/Longreach,_Queensland" title="Longreach, Queensland">Longreach</a> and supports only grassland.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wheatbelt_(Western_Australia)" title="Wheatbelt (Western Australia)">Wheatbelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Southern_(Western_Australia)" title="Great Southern (Western Australia)">Great Southern</a> regions are shielded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darling_Range" title="Darling Range">Darling Range</a> to the west: <a href="/wiki/Mandurah" title="Mandurah">Mandurah</a>, near the coast, receives about 700 millimetres (28 in) annually. <a href="/wiki/Dwellingup,_Western_Australia" title="Dwellingup, Western Australia">Dwellingup</a>, 40 km inland and in the heart of the ranges, receives over 1,000 millimetres (39 in) a year while <a href="/wiki/Narrogin,_Western_Australia" title="Narrogin, Western Australia">Narrogin</a>, 130 km further east, receives less than 500 millimetres (20 in) a year.</li>
</ul>
