<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Community Collaborative Rain, Hail and Snow Network</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Community Collaborative Rain, Hail and Snow Network</b>, or <b>CoCoRaHS</b>, is a network of volunteer weather observers in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> that take daily readings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and report them to a central data store over the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Collaborative_Rain,_Hail_and_Snow_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The network was started in 1997, in <a href="/wiki/Larimer_County,_Colorado" title="Larimer County, Colorado">Larimer County, Colorado</a> after a <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flood</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spring_Creek_(Fort_Collins,_Colorado)" title="Spring Creek (Fort Collins, Colorado)">Spring Creek</a> killed five people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning2006-1">[1]</a></sup> and damaged structures in the city of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Collins,_Colorado" title="Fort Collins, Colorado">Fort Collins, Colorado</a>, including hundreds of millions of dollars in damage to the Colorado State University campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FC1997_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FC1997-2">[2]</a></sup> The severity of the flood and its widespread spatial variability surprised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a>, and former assistant state <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climatologist" title="Climatologist">climatologist</a> for the state of Colorado, Nolan Doesken, asked for precipitation measurements from private citizens in the area. About 300 responded to his emergency request for data. Said Doesken later, "The results of the data showed that more than 14 in. (36 cm) of rain fell over southwest Fort Collins, the area where the flood waters originated, while less than 2 in. (5 cm) of rain fell only 3-4 mi (5-6 km) east. The enthusiastic interest shown by volunteers and the great value of the data verified the need for such a service, and CoCoRaHS was born."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since March 2015">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> The program was originally confined to Colorado (the first "Co" in "CoCoRaHS" stood for "Colorado" instead of "Community"), but began expanding to other states during the 2000s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Users">Users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Collaborative_Rain,_Hail_and_Snow_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CoCoRaHS is used by a wide variety of organizations and individuals. The <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS), other meteorologists, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrologist" title="Hydrologist">hydrologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">emergency managers</a>, city utilities (water supply, water conservation, storm water), insurance adjusters, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA</a>, engineers, mosquito control, ranchers and farmers, outdoor &amp; recreation interests, teachers, students, and neighbors in the community are examples of people who use CoCoRaHS data.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_programs">Other programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Collaborative_Rain,_Hail_and_Snow_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A similar program, the <a href="/wiki/Significant_Weather_Observing_Program" title="Significant Weather Observing Program">Significant Weather Observing Program</a> (SWOP), was begun independently in around the year 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service_Lincoln,_Illinois" title="National Weather Service Lincoln, Illinois">National Weather Service Lincoln, Illinois</a>. CoCoRaHS data supplements the more rigorous data from the national program with increased spatial and temporal resolution. Real-time data is also provided by the <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Weather_Observer_Program" title="Citizen Weather Observer Program">Citizen Weather Observer Program</a> (CWOP), whose users operate <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> that automatically report over the internet, and which supplements the more rigorous data reported by formal <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observation</a> stations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsors">Sponsors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Collaborative_Rain,_Hail_and_Snow_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sponsors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF) are major sponsors of CoCoRaHS. Other organizations have contributed either financially, and/or with supplies and equipment. Many other organizations and individuals have also pitched in time and resources to help keep the network up and running.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Collaborative_Rain,_Hail_and_Snow_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2015 all fifty <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>, and the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico participate in CoCoRaHS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoCoRaHS-2015-03_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoCoRaHS-2015-03-4">[4]</a></sup> The CoCoRaHS Canada network began in Manitoba in December 2011 following a massive flood in that province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoCoRaHS-Canada-2014-07_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoCoRaHS-Canada-2014-07-5">[5]</a></sup> As of 2014, the network had expanded to the Canadian provinces of Alberta, British Columbia, Manitoba, New Brunswick, Newfoundland and Labrador, Nova Scotia, Ontario, Prince Edward Island, Quebec, and Saskatchewan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoCoRaHS-Canada-2014-07_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CoCoRaHS-Canada-2014-07-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
