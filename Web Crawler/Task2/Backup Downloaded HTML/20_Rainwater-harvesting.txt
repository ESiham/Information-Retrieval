<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rainwater harvesting</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Rainwater harvesting</b> is the accumulation and deposition of rainwater for reuse on-site, rather than allowing it to run off. <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="v:Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater can be collected</a> from <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Surface_water" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Surface water">rivers</a> or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rooftop_rainwater_harvesting" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rooftop rainwater harvesting">roofs</a>, and in many places, the water collected is redirected to a deep pit (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Groundwater_recharge/Wells,_shafts,_and_boreholes" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Groundwater recharge/Wells, shafts, and boreholes">well, shaft, or borehole</a>), a reservoir with percolation, or collected from <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Fog_and_dew_collection" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Fog and dew collection">dew or fog</a> with nets or other tools. Its uses include water for gardens, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, domestic use with proper treatment, indoor heating for houses, etc. The harvested water can also be used as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, longer-term storage, and for other purposes such as <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a>.</p>
<p>Rainwater harvesting is one of the simplest and oldest methods of <a href="/wiki/Self-supply_of_water_and_sanitation" title="Self-supply of water and sanitation">self-supply of water</a> for households usually financed by the user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainwater harvesting provides an independent water supply during regional water restrictions, and in developed countries, is often used to supplement the main supply. It provides water when a drought occurs, can help mitigate flooding of low-lying areas, and reduces demand on wells which may enable groundwater levels to be sustained. It also helps in the availability of potable water, as rainwater is substantially free of salinity and other salts. Application of rainwater harvesting in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_urban_water_management" title="Integrated urban water management">urban water system</a> provides a substantial benefit for both <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> subsystems by reducing the need for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_water" title="Clean water">clean water</a> in water distribution system, less generated <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewer_system" title="Sewer system">sewer system</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and a reduction in stormwater runoff polluting freshwater bodies.</p>
<p>A large body of work has focused on the development of lifecycle assessment and lifecycle costing methodologies to assess the level of environmental impacts and money that can be saved by implementing rainwater harvesting systems.</p>
<p>More development and knowledge is required to understand the benefits rainwater harvesting can provide to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Many countries, especially those with arid environments, use rainwater harvesting as a cheap and reliable source of clean water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To enhance <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> in arid environments, ridges of soil are constructed to trap and prevent rainwater from running down hills and slopes. Even in periods of low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>, enough water is collected for crops to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> Water can be collected from roofs, and dams and ponds can be constructed to hold large quantities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainwater" title="Rainwater">rainwater</a> so that even on days when little to no rainfall occurs, enough is available to irrigate crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality">Quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concentration of contaminants is reduced significantly by diverting the <a href="/wiki/First_flush" title="First flush">initial flow</a> of run-off water to waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Improved water quality can also be obtained by using a floating draw-off mechanism (rather than from the base of the tank) and by using a series of tanks, withdraw from the last in series. Prefiltration is a common practice used in the industry to ensure that the water entering the tank is free of large sediment. Prefiltration is important to keep the system healthy.</p>
<p>Conceptually, a water supply system should match the quality of water with the end use. However, in most of the developed world, high-quality potable water is used for all end uses. This approach wastes money and energy and imposes unnecessary impacts to the environment. Supplying rainwater that has gone through preliminary filtration measures for nonpotable water uses, such as toilet flushing, irrigation, and laundry, may be a significant part of a sustainable water management strategy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="System_setup">System setup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: System setup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainwater harvesting systems can range in complexity, from systems that can be installed with minimal skills, to automated systems that require advanced setup and installation. The basic rainwater harvesting system is more of a plumbing job than a technical job, as all the outlets from the building terrace are connected through a pipe to an underground tank that stores water.</p>
<p>Systems are ideally sized to meet the water demand throughout the dry season, since it must be big enough to support daily water consumption. Specifically, the rainfall capturing area such as a building roof must be large enough to maintain adequate flow of water. The water storage tank size should be large enough to contain the captured water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Needs elaboration and citation to avoid the use of 'should' and 'must'. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>For low-tech systems, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Portal:Water" title="v:Portal:Water">many low-tech methods</a> are used to capture rainwater: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rooftop_rainwater_harvesting" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rooftop rainwater harvesting">rooftop systems</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Surface_water" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Surface water">surface water capture</a>, and pumping the rainwater that has already soaked into the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Groundwater_recharge" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Groundwater recharge">ground or captured in reservoirs</a> and storing it in tanks (cisterns).</p>
<p>Before a rainwater harvesting system is built, use of digital tools is useful. For instance, to detect if a region has a high rainwater harvesting potential, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rainwater_Harvesting_GIS_Map" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rainwater Harvesting GIS Map">rainwater-harvesting GIS maps</a> can be made using an <a class="external text" href="http://www.rainfoundation.org/tools/map-your-rain.html" rel="nofollow">online interactive tool</a>. Or, to estimate how much water is needed to fulfill a community's water needs, the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rain_is_Gain_Tool" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rain is Gain Tool">Rain is Gain tool</a> helps. Tools like these can save time and money before a commitment to build a system is undertaken, in addition to making the project sustainable and last a long time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle_assessment:_design_for_environment">Lifecycle assessment: design for environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lifecycle assessment: design for environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eeast_figure.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="720" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Eeast_figure.jpg/220px-Eeast_figure.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Eeast_figure.jpg/330px-Eeast_figure.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Eeast_figure.jpg/440px-Eeast_figure.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eeast_figure.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
EEAST model for LCAs of rainwater harvesting systems</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Contemporary system designs require an analysis of not only the economic and technical performance of a system, but also the environmental performance. <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Lifecycle assessment</a> is a methodology used to evaluate the environmental impacts of a precut or systems, from cradle-to-grave of its lifetime. Devkota et al.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Devkota_2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Devkota_2015-7">[7]</a></sup> developed such a methodology for rainwater harvesting, and found that the building design (e.g., dimensions) and function (e.g., educational, residential, etc.) play critical roles in the environmental performance of the system. The Economic and Environmental Analysis of Sanitations Technologies, <a class="external text" href="https://defneapul.wikispaces.com/Water+sustainability" rel="nofollow">EEAST</a> model evaluates the greenhouse gas emissions and cost of such systems over the lifetime of a variety of building types.</p>
<p>To address the functional parameters of rainwater harvesting systems, a new metric was developed - the demand to supply ratio (D/S) - identifying the ideal building design (supply) and function (demand) in regard to the environmental performance of rainwater harvesting for toilet flushing. With the idea that supply of rainwater not only saves the potable water, but also saves the stormwater entering the combined sewer network (thereby requiring treatment), the savings in environmental emissions were higher if the buildings are connected to a combined sewer network compared to separate one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Devkota_2015_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Devkota_2015-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Rainwater harvesting is possible by growing <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_swamp_forest" title="Freshwater swamp forest">freshwater-flooded forests</a> without losing the income from the used, submerged land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The main purpose of the rainwater harvesting is to use the locally available rainwater to meet water requirements throughout the year without the need of huge capital expenditure. This would facilitate the availability of uncontaminated water for domestic, industrial, and irrigation needs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_approaches">New approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Instead of using the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rooftop_rainwater_harvesting" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rooftop rainwater harvesting">roof for catchment</a>, the RainSaucer, which looks like an upside-down umbrella, collects rain straight from the sky. This decreases the potential for contamination and makes potable water for developing countries a potential application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other applications of this free-standing rainwater collection approach are sustainable gardening and small-plot farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Dutch invention called the <a href="/wiki/Groasis_Waterboxx" title="Groasis Waterboxx">Groasis Waterboxx</a> is also useful for growing trees with harvested and stored dew and rainwater.</p>

<p>Traditionally, stormwater management using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detention_basins" title="Detention basins">detention basins</a> served a single purpose. However, <a href="/wiki/OptiRTC" title="OptiRTC">optimized real-time control</a> lets this infrastructure double as a source of rainwater harvesting without compromising the existing detention capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This has been used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a> headquarters to evacuate stored water prior to storm events, thus reducing wet weather flow while ensuring water availability for later reuse. This has the benefit of increasing water quality released and decreasing the volume of water released during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_sewer_overflow" title="Combined sewer overflow">combined sewer overflow</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Generally, <a href="/wiki/Check_dam" title="Check dam">check dams</a> are constructed across the streams to enhance the percolation of surface water into the <a href="/wiki/Subsoil" title="Subsoil">subsoil</a> strata. The water percolation in the water-impounded area of the check dams can be enhanced artificially manyfold by loosening the subsoil strata and <a href="/wiki/Overburden" title="Overburden">overburden</a> using <a href="/wiki/ANFO" title="ANFO">ANFO</a> explosives as used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_cast_mining" title="Open cast mining">open cast mining</a>. Thus, local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquifers" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a> can be recharged quickly using the available surface water fully for use in the dry season.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Around the third century BCE, the farming communities in <a href="/wiki/Balochistan" title="Balochistan">Balochistan</a> (now located in Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Iran), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kutch" title="Kutch">Kutch</a>, India, used rainwater harvesting for agriculture and many other uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In ancient <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> , rainwater harvesting was done by <a href="/wiki/Chola_dynasty" title="Chola dynasty">Chola kings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Rainwater from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brihadeeswarar_temple" title="Brihadeeswarar temple">Brihadeeswarar temple</a> (located in Balaganpathy Nagar, <a href="/wiki/Thanjavur" title="Thanjavur">Thanjavur</a>, India) was collected in Shivaganga tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> During the later Chola period, the Vīrānam tank was built (1011 to 1037 CE) in Cuddalore district of <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> state to store water for drinking and irrigation purposes. Vīrānam is a 16-km-long tank with a storage capacity of 1,465,000,000 cu ft (41,500,000 m<sup>3</sup>).</p>
<p>Though little-known, for centuries, the town of Venice depended on rainwater harvesting. The lagoon which surrounds Venice is brackish water, which is not suitable for drinking. The ancient inhabitants of Venice established a system of rainwater collection which was based on man-made insulated collection wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Water percolated down the specially designed stone flooring, and was filtered by a layer of sand, then collected at the bottom of the well. Later, as Venice acquired territories on the mainland, it started to import water by boat from local rivers, but the wells remained in use, and were especially important in time of war when access to the mainland water could be blocked by an enemy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_use">Current use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In China, Argentina, and Brazil, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiversity.org/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting/Rooftop_rainwater_harvesting" title="v:Rainwater harvesting/Rooftop rainwater harvesting">rooftop rainwater harvesting</a> is being practised for providing drinking water, domestic water, water for livestock, water for small irrigation, and a way to replenish groundwater levels. <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> province in China and semiarid <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">northeast Brazil</a> have the largest rooftop rainwater harvesting projects going on.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> has the largest fraction of the population in the rural area relying on rainwater harvesting (currently around 40%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Rainwater harvesting was promoted heavily by the government in the 1980s. In the 1990s, after government funding for the collection tanks ran out, the private sector stepped in and provided several million tanks to private households, many of which continue to be used. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saladin2016_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-saladin2016-19">[19]</a></sup> This is one of the largest examples of <a href="/wiki/Self-supply_of_water_and_sanitation" title="Self-supply of water and sanitation">self-supply of water</a> worldwide.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, the law requires all new construction to include rainwater harvesting adequate for the residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a> has a similar law.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">Guinea-Bissau</a>, the houses of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diola" title="Diola">Diola</a>-people are frequently equipped with homebrew rainwater harvesters made from local, organic materials.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_Delta" title="Irrawaddy Delta">Irrawaddy Delta</a> of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, the groundwater is saline and communities rely on mud-lined rainwater ponds to meet their drinking water needs throughout the dry season. Some of these ponds are centuries old and are treated with great reverence and respect.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, until 2009 in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, water rights laws almost completely restricted rainwater harvesting; a property owner who captured rainwater was deemed to be stealing it from those who have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prior_appropriation_water_rights" title="Prior appropriation water rights">rights to take water</a> from the watershed. Now, residential well owners who meet certain criteria may obtain a permit to install a rooftop precipitation collection system (SB 09-080).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Up to 10 large scale pilot studies may also be permitted (HB 09-1129).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The main factor in persuading the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_Legislature" title="Colorado Legislature">Colorado Legislature</a> to change the law was a 2007 study that found that in an average year, 97% of the precipitation that fell in <a href="/wiki/Douglas_County,_Colorado" title="Douglas County, Colorado">Douglas County</a>, in the southern suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, never reached a stream—it was used by plants or evaporated on the ground. In Colorado, one cannot even drill a water well on properties less than 35 acres (14 ha). Rainwater catchment is mandatory for new dwellings in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe, New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-23">[23]</a></sup> Texas offers a sales tax exemption on the purchase of rainwater harvesting equipment. Both Texas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and Ohio allow the practice even for potable purposes. Oklahoma passed the Water for 2060 Act in 2012, to promote pilot projects for rainwater and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graywater" title="Graywater">graywater</a> use among other water-saving techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSL_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSL-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, some housing societies are now adding rainwater in their main water sources after proper treatment.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, Professor Micheal Mcginley established a project to design a rainwater harvesting prototype in the biosystems design challenge module at <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin" title="University College Dublin">University College Dublin</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting_in_Canada" title="Rainwater harvesting in Canada">Rainwater harvesting in Canada</a></div>
<p>A number of Canadians have started implementing rainwater harvesting systems for use in stormwater reduction, irrigation, laundry, and lavatory plumbing. Substantial reform to Canadian law since the mid-2000s has increased the use of this technology in agricultural, industrial, and residential use, but ambiguity remains amongst legislation in many provinces. Bylaws and local municipal codes often regulate rainwater harvesting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Pan India Potential: No authenticated potential of rainwater harvesting has been assessed in India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> was the first state to make rainwater harvesting compulsory for every building to avoid groundwater depletion. The scheme was launched in 2001 and has been implemented in all rural areas of Tamil Nadu. Posters all over Tamil Nadu including rural areas create awareness about harvesting rainwater <a class="external text" href="http://www.tn.gov.in/dtp/rainwater.htm" rel="nofollow">TN Govt site</a>. It gave excellent results within five years, and slowly every state took it as a role model. Since its implementation, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> had a 50% rise in water level in five years and the water quality significantly improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>: In <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>, adoption of rainwater harvesting is mandatory for every owner or the occupier of a building having the site area measuring 60 ft (18.3 m) X 40 ft (12.2 m) and above and for newly constructed building measuring 30 ft (9.1 m) X 40 ft (12.2 m) and above dimensions. In this regard, <a href="/wiki/Bangalore_Water_Supply_and_Sewerage_Board" title="Bangalore Water Supply and Sewerage Board">Bangalore Water Supply and Sewerage Board</a> has initiated and constructed “Rain Water Harvesting Theme Park” in the name of Sir <a href="/wiki/M._Visvesvaraya" title="M. Visvesvaraya">M. Visvesvaraya</a> in 1.2 acres (4,900 m<sup>2</sup>) of land situated at <a href="/wiki/Jayanagar,_Bangalore" title="Jayanagar, Bangalore">Jayanagar, Bangalore</a>. In this park, 26 different type of rainwater harvesting models are demonstrated along with the water conservation tips. The auditorium on the first floor is set up with a "green" air conditioning system and will be used to arrange the meeting and showing of a video clip about the rainwater harvesting to students and general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, rainwater harvesting has traditionally been practised by the people of the <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">Thar Desert</a>. Many ancient water harvesting systems in Rajasthan have now been revived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Water harvesting systems are widely used in other areas of Rajasthan, as well, for example the <i><a href="/wiki/Chauka" title="Chauka">chauka</a></i> system from the <a href="/wiki/Jaipur_district" title="Jaipur district">Jaipur district</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>:
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting_in_Kerala" title="Rainwater harvesting in Kerala">Rainwater harvesting in Kerala</a></div>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>: At present, in <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>, rainwater harvesting is compulsory for any new housing society to be registered.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, rainwater harvesting is being considered as a good solution to solve the water crisis.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Mumbai City council is planning to make rainwater harvesting mandatory for large societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> An attempt has been made at the Department of Chemical Engineering, IISc, <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a> to harvest rainwater using upper surface of a solar still, which was used for water distillation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Southwest Center for the Study of Hospital and Healthcare Systems in cooperation with Rotary International is sponsoring a rainwater harvesting model program across the country. The first rainwater catchment system was installed at an elementary school in Lod, Israel. The project is looking to expand to Haifa in its third phase. The Southwest Center has also partnered with the Water Resources Action Project of Washington, DC, which currently has rainwater harvesting projects in the West Bank. Rainwater harvesting systems are being installed in local schools for the purpose of educating schoolchildren about water conservation principles and bridging divides between people of different religious and ethnic backgrounds, all while addressing the <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> issue that the Middle East faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although New Zealand has plentiful rainfall in the West and South, for much of the country, rainwater harvesting is the normal practice for most rural housing and is encouraged by most councils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sri_Lanka">Sri Lanka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sri Lanka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rainwater harvesting has been a popular method of obtaining water for agriculture and for drinking purposes in rural homes. The legislation to promote rainwater harvesting was enacted through the Urban Development Authority (Amendment) Act, No. 36 of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Lanka rainwater harvesting forum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> is leading the Sri Lanka's initiative.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The South African Water Research Commission has supported research into rainwater harvesting. Reports on this research are available on their 'Knowledge Hub'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Studies in arid, semiarid, and humid regions have confirmed that techniques such as mulching, pitting, ridging, and modified run-on plots are effective for small-scale crop production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Rainwater harvesting in the United Kingdom">Rainwater harvesting in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_tank" title="Rainwater tank">water butts</a> are often found in domestic gardens and on <a href="/wiki/Allotment_(gardening)" title="Allotment (gardening)">allotments</a> to collect rainwater, which is then used to water the garden. However, the British government's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_For_Sustainable_Homes" title="Code For Sustainable Homes">Code For Sustainable Homes</a> encouraged fitting large underground tanks to newly built homes to collect rainwater for flushing toilets, watering, and washing. Ideal designs had the potential to reduce demand on mains water supply by half. The code was revoked in 2015.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nontraditional">Nontraditional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_harvesting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Nontraditional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In 1992, American artist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jones_McKean" title="Michael Jones McKean">Michael Jones McKean</a> created an artwork in Omaha, Nebraska, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bemis_Center_for_Contemporary_Art" title="Bemis Center for Contemporary Art">Bemis Center for Contemporary Art</a> that created a fully sustainable rainbow in the Omaha skyline. The project collected thousands of gallons of rainwater, storing the water in six daisy-chained 12,000 gallons tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The massive logistical undertaking, during its five-month span, was one of the largest urban rainwater harvesting sites in the American Midwest.</li>
</ul>
