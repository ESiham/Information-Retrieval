<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Raincoat</h1>

<p>A <b>raincoat</b> or <b>slicker</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Waterproofing" title="Waterproofing">waterproof or water-resistant</a> <a href="/wiki/Coat_(clothing)" title="Coat (clothing)">coat</a> worn to protect the body from <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>. The term <b>rain jacket</b> is sometimes used to refer to raincoats that are waist length. A rain jacket may be combined with a pair of <b>rain pants</b> to make a <b>rain suit</b>.</p>
<p>Modern raincoats are often constructed of breathable, waterproof fabrics such as <a href="/wiki/Gore-Tex" title="Gore-Tex">Gore-Tex</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tyvek" title="Tyvek">Tyvek</a> and coated <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylons</a>. These fabrics allow some air to pass through, allowing the garment to 'breathe' so that sweat vapour can escape. The amount of pouring rain a raincoat can handle is sometimes measured in the unit <a href="/wiki/Millimeters,_water_gauge" title="Millimeters, water gauge">millimeters, water gauge</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raincoat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first waterproof raincoat was created following the patent by Scottish chemist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Macintosh" title="Charles Macintosh">Charles Macintosh</a> in 1824 of new <a href="/wiki/Tarpaulin" title="Tarpaulin">tarpaulin</a> fabric, described by him as "India rubber cloth," and made by sandwiching a core of rubber softened by naptha in two pieces of fabric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
