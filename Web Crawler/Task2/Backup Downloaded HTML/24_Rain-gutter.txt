<p>A <b>rain gutter</b> or <b>surface water collection channel</b> is a component of water discharge system for a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Water from a pitched roof flows down into a valley gutter, a parapet gutter or an eaves gutter. An <i>eaves gutter</i> is also known as an <i>eavestrough</i> (especially in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>), <i>eaves channel,</i> <i>dripster,</i> <i>guttering</i> or simply as a <i>gutter</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The word <i>gutter</i> derives from Latin <i>gutta</i>(noun), meaning "a drop, spot or mark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guttering in its earliest form consisted of lined wooden or stone troughs. <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Lead</a> was a popular liner and is still used in pitched valley gutters. Many materials have been used to make guttering: <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asbestos_cement" title="Asbestos cement">asbestos cement</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UPVC" title="UPVC">UPVC</a> (PVCu), cast and extruded <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gutters prevent water ingress into the fabric of the building by channelling the rainwater away from the exterior of the walls and their foundations. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012461_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012461-4">[4]</a></sup> Water running down the walls causes dampness in the affected rooms and provides a favourable environment for growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mould" title="Mould">mould</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wet_rot" title="Wet rot">wet rot</a> in timber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A rain gutter may be a:</p>
<ul>
<li>Roof integral trough along the lower edge of the roof slope which is fashioned from the roof covering and <a href="/wiki/Flashing_(weatherproofing)" title="Flashing (weatherproofing)">flashing</a> materials.</li>
<li>Discrete trough of metal, or other material that is suspended beyond the roof edge and below the projected slope of the roof.</li>
<li>Wall integral structure beneath the roof edge, traditionally constructed of masonry, fashioned as the crowning element of a wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A roof must be designed with a suitable fall to allow the rainwater to discharge. The water drains into a gutter that is fed into a downpipe. A flat roof will have a watertight surface with a fall of 1 in 60, or 1 in the case of lead. They can drain internally or to an eaves gutter, which has a minimum 1 in 360 fall towards the downpipe. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChudley1988479_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudley1988479-7">[7]</a></sup> The pitch of a pitched roof is determined by the construction material of the covering. For slate this will be at 25%, for machine made tiles it will be 35%. Water falls towards a <i>parapet gutter</i>, a <i>valley gutter</i> or an <i>eaves gutter</i>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476-7_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476-7-8">[8]</a></sup> When two pitched roofs meet at an angle, they also form a <i>pitched valley gutter</i>: the join is sealed with valley flashing. Parapet gutters and valley gutters discharge into internal rainwater pipes or directly into external down pipes at the end of the run. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476-7_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudley1988476-7-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The capacity of the gutter is a significant design consideration. The area of the roof is calculated (metres) and this is multiplied by <a href="/wiki/Rain#intensity" title="Rain">rainfall</a> (litres/sec/metres²) which is assumed to be 0.0208. This gives a required discharge outfall capacity. (litres/sec) .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012465_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012465-9">[9]</a></sup> Rainfall intensity, the amount of water likely to generated in a two-minute rainstorm is more important that average rainfall, the British Standards Institute<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> notes that an indicative storm in Essex, (annual rainfall 500mm per annum) delivers 0.022 l/s/m²- while one in Cumbria (annual rainfall 1800mm per annum) delivers 0.014 l/s/m².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012462_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012462-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eaves gutters can be made from a variety of materials such as <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galvanised" title="Galvanised">galvanised</a> steel, painted <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, painted <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> (and other plastics) and occasionally from <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Water collected by a rain gutter is fed, usually via a <a href="/wiki/Downspout" title="Downspout">downpipe</a> (also called a leader or conductor),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> from the roof edge to the base of the building where it is either discharged or collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ching_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ching-14">[14]</a></sup> The down pipe can terminate in a shoe and discharge directly onto the surface, but using modern construction techniques would be connected through an inspection chamber to a drain that led to a surface water drain or soakaway. Alternatively it would connect via a <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">gulley</a> <a href="/wiki/Trap_(plumbing)" title="Trap (plumbing)">(u-bend)</a> with 50mm water seal to a combined drain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChudley1988480_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudley1988480-15">[15]</a></sup> Water from rain gutters may be <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">harvested</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_barrel" title="Rain barrel">rain barrel</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Cistern" title="Cistern">cistern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rain_Water_Harvesting_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rain_Water_Harvesting-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rain gutters can be equipped with gutter screens, micro mesh screens, louvers or solid hoods to allow water from the roof to flow through, while reducing passage of roof debris into the gutter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clogged gutters can also cause water ingress into the building as the water backs up. Clogged gutters can also lead to stagnant water build up which in some climates allows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquitoes" title="Mosquitoes">mosquitoes</a> to breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Romans brought rainwater systems to Britain. The technology was subsequently lost, but was re-introduced by the Normans. The <a href="/wiki/White_Tower_(Tower_of_London)" title="White Tower (Tower of London)">White Tower</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> had external gutters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stafford_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-stafford-19">[19]</a></sup> In March 1240 the Keeper of the Works at the Tower of London was ordered by King Henry "to have the Great Tower whitened both inside and out". This was according to the fashion at the time. Later that year the king wrote to the Keeper, commanding that the White Tower's lead guttering should be extended with the effect that "the wall of the tower ... newly whitened, may be in no danger of perishing or falling outwards through the trickling of the rain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I.26P25-27_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-I.26P25-27-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> times, the <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thanes</a> erected buildings with large overhanging roofs to throw the water clear of the walls in the same way that occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thatched_cottage" title="Thatched cottage">thatched cottages</a>. The cathedral builder used lead parapet gutters, with elaborate <a href="/wiki/Gargoyle" title="Gargoyle">gargoyles</a> for the same purpose. With the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_monasteries" title="Dissolution of the monasteries">dissolution of the monasteries</a>- those buildings were recycled and there was plenty of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_(metal)" title="Lead (metal)">lead</a> that could be used for secular building. The yeoman would use wooden gutters or lead lined wooden gutters. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stafford_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-stafford-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>When <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace" title="The Crystal Palace">The Crystal Palace</a> was designed in 1851 by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paxton" title="Joseph Paxton">Joseph Paxton</a> with its innovative ridge-and-furrow roof, the rafters that spanned the space between the roof girders of the glass roof also served as the gutters. The wooden <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace#40" title="s:The Crystal Palace">Paxton gutters</a> had a deep semi-circular channel to remove the rainwater and grooves at the side to handle the condensation. They were under trussed with an iron plate and had preformed notches for the glazing bars: they drained into a wooden box gutter that drained into and through structural cast iron columns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> introduced new methods of <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">casting-iron</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railways</a> brought a method of distributing the heavy cast-iron item to building sites. The relocation into the cities created a demand for housing that needed to be compact. Dryer houses controlled <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>. In 1849 <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bazalgette" title="Joseph Bazalgette">Joseph Bazalgette</a> proposed a sewerage system for London, that prevented run-off being channelled into the Thames. By the 1870s all houses were constructed with cast iron gutters and down pipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stafford_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-stafford-19">[19]</a></sup> The Victorian gutter was an ogee, 115mm in width, that was fitted directly to the fascia boards eliminating the need for brackets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-period_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-period-22">[22]</a></sup> Square and half-round profiles were also available. For a brief period after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">first world war</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asbestos-cement" title="Asbestos-cement">asbestos-cement</a> guttering became popular due to it being maintenance free: the disadvantages however ensured this was a short period: it was more bulky and fractured on impact <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1982422_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1982422-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_iron">Cast iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cast iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cast iron gutters were introduced in the late 18th century as an alternative to lead. Cast iron enabled eaves gutters to be mass-produced: they were rigid and non-porous while lead could only be used as a liner within timber gutters. Installation was a single process and didn't require heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> They could be attached directly to the fascia board. Cast iron gutters are still specified for restoration work in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_area" title="Conservation area">conservation areas</a>, but are usually replaced with cast aluminium made to the same profile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-period_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-period-22">[22]</a></sup> Extruded aluminium gutters can be made to a variety of profiles from a roll of aluminium sheet on site in lengths of up to 30m. They feature internal brackets at 400mm spacing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012427_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012427-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UPVC">UPVC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: UPVC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In UK domestic architecture, guttering is made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UPVC" title="UPVC">UPVC</a> sections. The first PVC pipes were introduced in the 1930s for use in sanitary drainage systems. Polyethylene was developed in 1933. The first pressurised plastic drinking water pipes were installed in the Netherlands in the 1950s. During the 1960s rain water pipes, guttering and down pipes using plastic materials were introduced followed by PVC soil systems which became viable with the introduction of ring seals. A British Standard was launched for soil systems, local authorities started to specify PVC systems. By 1970 plastic rainwater systems accounted for over 60% of new installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A European Standard EN607 has existed since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>It is easy to install, economical, lightweight requires minimum maintenance and has a life expectancy of 50 years. The material has a disadvantageous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a> 0.06 mm/m°C, so design allowances have to be made. A 4-metre gutter, enduring a −5 °C to 25 °C temperature range will need space to expand, 30 × 4 × 0.06 = 7.2 mm within its end stops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012467_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaskrey2012467-28">[28]</a></sup> As a rule of thumb a 4-inch (100 mm) gutter with a single 68-millimetre (2.7 in) downpipe will drain a 600-square-foot (56 m<sup>2</sup>) roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1982423_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1982423-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zinc">Zinc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zinc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In UK commercial and European commercial and domestic architecture, guttering is made from <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>. Metal gutters with bead stiffened fronts is governed in the UK by BS EN612:2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finlock_gutters">Finlock gutters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Finlock gutters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Finlock gutters, a proprietary name<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> for concrete gutters, can be employed on a large range of buildings. There were used on domestic properties in the 1950s and 1960s, as a replacement for cast iron gutters when there was a shortage of steel and surplus of concrete.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They were discredited after differential movement was found to open joints and allow damp to penetrate, but can be fitted with an aluminium and bitumastic liner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-santo_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-santo-32">[32]</a></sup> Finlock concrete gutter units are made up of two troughs – one is the visible gutter and the other sits across the cavity wall. The blocks which can range from 8 to 12 inches (200 to 300 mm) can be joined using <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforcing rods and concrete</a>, to form <a href="/wiki/Lintel" title="Lintel">lintels</a> for doors and windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-santo_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-santo-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vernacular_buildings">Vernacular buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vernacular buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Guttering can be made from any locally available material such as stone or wood. Porous materials may be lined with <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gutter_guards">Gutter guards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_gutter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gutter guards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Gutter guards</b> (also called <b>gutter covers</b>, <b>gutter protection</b> or <b>leaf guards</b>) are primarily aimed at preventing damage caused from clogged gutters and reducing the need for regular gutter cleaning. They are a common add-on or included as an option for <a href="/wiki/Custom_home" title="Custom home">custom-built homes</a>. Forms include wire mesh across the tops of guttering, lengths of spiked plastic, or reverse curves in the gutter design to catch water but not falling objects.</p>
