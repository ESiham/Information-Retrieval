<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Earth rainfall climatology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Earth rainfall climatology</b> Is the study of rainfall, a sub-field of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorology</a>. Formally, a wider study includes water falling as ice crystals, i.e. hail, sleet, snow (parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>). The aim of rainfall climatology is to measure, understand and predict rain distribution across different regions of planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, a factor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pressure" title="Air pressure">air pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloud_type" title="Cloud type">cloud type</a> and raindrop size, via direct measurement and <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> data acquisition. Current technologies accurately predict rainfall 3–4 days in advance using <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">Geostationary</a> orbiting satellites gather IR and visual wavelength data to measure realtime localised rainfall by estimating <a href="/wiki/Cloud_albedo" title="Cloud albedo">cloud albedo</a>, water content, and the corresponding probability of rain. Geographic distribution of rain is largely governed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_type&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Climate type (page does not exist)">climate type</a>, topography and habitat humidity. In mountainous areas, heavy precipitation is possible where upslope flow is maximized within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windward" title="Windward">windward</a> sides of the terrain at elevation. On the leeward side of mountains, desert climates can exist due to the dry air caused by compressional heating. The movement of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a>, brings <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy seasons</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">savannah</a> <a href="/wiki/Clime" title="Clime">climes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect leads to increased rainfall, both in amounts and intensity, downwind of cities. <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> may also cause changes in the precipitation pattern globally, including wetter conditions at high latitudes and in some wet tropical areas, and drier conditions in parts of the subtropics and middle latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-1">[1]</a></sup> Precipitation is a major component of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, and is responsible for depositing most of the <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>. Approximately 505,000 cubic kilometres (121,000 cu mi) of water falls as precipitation each year; 398,000 cubic kilometres (95,000 cu mi) of it over the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chow_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-chow-2">[2]</a></sup> Given the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface area, that means the globally averaged annual precipitation is 990 millimetres (39 in). <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">Climate</a> classification systems such as the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> system use average annual rainfall to help differentiate between differing climate regimes.</p>
<p>Most of Australia is semi-arid or desert, making it the world's driest <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a>. Australia's rainfall is mainly regulated by the movement of the monsoon trough during the summer rainy season, with lesser amounts falling during the winter and spring in its southernmost sections. Almost whole North Africa is semi-arid, arid or hyper-arid, containing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> which is the largest hot desert in the world, while central Africa (known as <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>) sees an annual rainy season regulated by the movement of the intertropical convergence zone or monsoon trough, though the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel Belt</a> located at the south of the Sahara Desert knows an extremely intense and a nearly permanent dry season and only receives minimum summer rainfall. Across Asia, a large annual rainfall minimum, composed primarily of deserts, stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a> in Mongolia west-southwest through Pakistan and Iran into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Desert" title="Arabian Desert">Arabian Desert</a> in Saudi Arabia. In Asia, rainfall is favored across its southern portion from India east and northeast across the Philippines and southern China into Japan due to the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> advecting moisture primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> into the region. Similar, but weaker, monsoon circulations are present over <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the wettest regions are in the Alps and downwind of bodies of water, particularly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> west coasts. Within North America, the drier areas of the United States are the Desert Southwest, Great Basin, valleys of northeast Arizona, eastern Utah, central Wyoming, and the Columbia Basin. Other dry regions within the continent are far northern Canada and the Sonoran Desert of northwest Mexico. The Pacific Northwest United States, the Rockies of British Columbia, and the coastal ranges of Alaska are the wettest locations in North America. The equatorial region near the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ), or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, is the wettest portion of the world's continents. Annually, the rain belt within the tropics marches northward by August, then moves back southward into the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> by February and March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Todd_Mitchell_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Todd_Mitchell-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a></div>
<p>The Köppen classification depends on average monthly values of temperature and precipitation. The most commonly used form of the Köppen classification has five primary types labeled A through E. Specifically, the primary types are A, tropical; B, dry; C, mild mid-latitude; D, cold mid-latitude; and E, polar. The five primary classifications can be further divided into secondary classifications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna" title="Tropical savanna">tropical savanna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical" title="Humid subtropical">humid subtropical</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humid_continental" title="Humid continental">humid continental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subarctic climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polar_ice_cap" title="Polar ice cap">polar ice cap</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>.</p>
<p>Rain forests are characterized by high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>, with definitions setting minimum normal annual rainfall between 1,750 millimetres (69 in) and 2,000 millimetres (79 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A tropical savanna is a <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a> located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">semi-arid</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humid" title="Humid">semi-humid</a> climate regions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>, with rainfall between 750 millimetres (30 in) and 1,270 millimetres (50 in) a year. They are widespread on <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and are also found in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the northern parts of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAVWOOD_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAVWOOD-6">[6]</a></sup> The humid subtropical climate zone where winter rainfall (and sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowfall" title="Snowfall">snowfall</a>) is associated with large <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> steer from west to east. Most summer rainfall occurs during <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> and from occasional <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Humid subtropical climates lie on the east side continents, roughly between <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> 20° and 40° degrees away from the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>An oceanic (or maritime) climate is typically found along the west coasts at the middle latitudes of all the world's continents, bordering cool oceans, as well as southeastern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and is accompanied by plentiful precipitation year-round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Mediterranean climate regime resembles the climate of the lands in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>, parts of western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, in southwestern <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and in parts of central <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. The climate is characterized by hot, dry summers and cool, wet winters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A steppe is a dry <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Subarctic climates are cold with continuous <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> and little precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subritter_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-subritter-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">Wet season</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1824" data-file-width="1624" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg/200px-Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg/300px-Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg/400px-Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Africa_satellite_orthographic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map of Africa showing the ecological break around the Sahara</div>

<p>Its northern half of the continent is primarily desert, containing the vast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, while its southern areas contain both <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plains</a>, and its central portion contains very dense <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>) regions. The equatorial region near the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> is the wettest portion of the continent. Annually, the rain belt across the continent marches northward into <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> by August, then moves back southward into south-central Africa by March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Todd_Mitchell_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Todd_Mitchell-3">[3]</a></sup> Mesoscale convective systems which form in tandem with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> that move along the Intertropical Convergence Zone during the summer months become the seedlings for tropical cyclones which form in the northern Atlantic and northeast Pacific oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A4-13">[13]</a></sup> Areas with a savannah climate in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Darfur" title="Darfur">Darfur</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> have a distinct rainy season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> bring moisture up the eastern slopes of the island, which is deposited as rainfall, and brings drier downsloped winds to areas south and west leaving the western sections of the island in a rain shadow. This leads to significantly more rainfall over northeast sections of the island than the southwestern portions of Madagascar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Southern Africa receives most of its rainfall from summer convective storms and with <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> moving through the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a>. Once a decade, tropical cyclones lead to excessive rainfall across the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A large annual rainfall minimum, composed primarily of deserts, stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a> in Mongolia west-southwest through Pakistan and Iran into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Desert" title="Arabian Desert">Arabian Desert</a> in Saudi Arabia. Rainfall around the continent is favored across its southern portion from India east and northeast across the Philippines and southern China into Japan due to the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> advecting moisture primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> into the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The monsoon trough can reach as far north as the <a href="/wiki/40th_parallel_north" title="40th parallel north">40th parallel</a> in East Asia during August before moving southward thereafter. Its poleward progression is accelerated by the onset of the summer monsoon which is characterized by the development of lower air pressure (a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a>) over the warmest part of Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFMRF_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCFMRF-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cherrapunji" title="Cherrapunji">Cherrapunji</a>, situated on the southern slopes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Eastern Himalaya</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shillong" title="Shillong">Shillong</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> is one of the wettest places on Earth, with an average annual rainfall of 11,430 mm (450 in). The highest recorded rainfall in a single year was 22,987 mm (904.9 in) in 1861. The 38-year average at <a href="/wiki/Mawsynram" title="Mawsynram">Mawsynram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> is 11,873 mm (467.4 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Lower rainfall maxima are found on the Mediterranean and Black Sea coasts of Turkey and the mountains of Tajikistan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Most of Australia is semi-arid or desert,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> making it the world's driest <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a>. The movement of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> is linked to the peak of the rainy season within the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Northern portions of the continent see the most rainfall, which is concentrated in the summer months. During winter and spring southern Australia sees its maximum rainfall. The interior desert sees its greatest rainfall during spring and summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The wettest spot is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Bellenden_Ker" title="Mount Bellenden Ker">Mount Bellenden Ker</a> in the north-east of the country records an average of 8,000 millimetres (310 in) per year, with over 12,000 millimetres (470 in) of rain recorded in the year 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> While Melbourne is thought of as being significantly wetter than <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, Sydney receives an average of 1212 mm (47.8 in) of rain per year compared to Melbourne's 650 mm (25.5 in), although Sydney is significantly sunnier and receives less days of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sydney_.28Observatory_Hill.29_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sydney_.28Observatory_Hill.29-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melbourne_Regional_Office_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Melbourne_Regional_Office-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Europe" title="Climate of Europe">Climate of Europe</a></div>
<p>On an annual basis, rainfall across the continent is favored within the Alps, and from Slovenia southward to the western coast of Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satmed_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-satmed-33">[33]</a></sup> Other maxima exist in western Georgia, northwest Spain, western Great Britain, and western Norway. The maxima along the eastern coasts of water bodies is due to the westerly wind flow which dominates across the continent. A bulk of the precipitation across the Alps falls between March and November. The <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a> in lands bordering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_sea" title="Mediterranean sea">Mediterranean sea</a> lasts from October through March, with November and December typically the wettest months. Summer rainfall across the continent evaporates completely into the warm atmosphere, leaving winter precipitation to be the source of groundwater for Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">Mesoscale</a> rain systems during the rainy season track south and eastward over the Mediterranean, with western portions of the sea experiencing 20 percent more rainfall than eastern sections of the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satmed_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-satmed-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>European Monsoon</b> (more commonly known as the <b>Return of the Westerlies</b>) is the result of a resurgence of westerly winds from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>, where they become loaded with wind and rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> These Westerly winds are a common phenomenon during the European winter, but they ease as Spring approaches in late March and through April and May. The winds pick up again in June, which is why this phenomenon is also referred to as "the return of the westerlies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The rain usually arrives in two waves, at the beginning of June and again in mid to late June. The European monsoon is not a monsoon in the traditional sense in that it doesn't meet all the requirements to be classified as such. Instead the Return of the Westerlies is more regarded as a conveyor belt that delivers a series of low pressure centres to <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> where they create unseasonable weather. These storms generally feature significantly lower than average temperatures, fierce rain or hail, thunder and strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The Return of the Westerlies affects Europe's <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Atlantic</a> coastline, more precisely <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux countries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Germany" title="Western Germany">Western Germany</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_France" title="Northern France">Northern France</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.</p>
<p>There are cycles seen within the rainfall data from Northern Europe between Great Britain and Germany, which are seen at a 16-year interval. Southern Europe experiences a 22-year cycle in rainfall variation. Other smaller term cycles are seen at 10-12 year and 6-7 year periods within the rainfall record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Places with significant impact by <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> across the continent include most of eastern Europe from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> northward into <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Precipitation across Canada is highest in the mountain ranges in the western portions due to onshore flow bringing Pacific moisture into the mountains, which is subsequently forced to lift up their slopes and deposit significant precipitation, primarily between August and May. Mesoscale convective systems are common mid-summer near the central border with the United States from the Prairie provinces eastward towards the Great Lakes. Southeastern sections of the country are also wet, due to the development of extratropical cyclones along the east coast of the continent which move northward into Atlantic Canada. During the summer and fall months, tropical cyclones from the Atlantic basin are also possible across Atlantic Canada. Amounts decrease as one works farther inland from the Pacific and Atlantic coasts, and from south to north towards the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rainfall varies widely both by location and season. Arid or semiarid conditions are encountered in the <a href="/wiki/Baja_California_Peninsula" title="Baja California Peninsula">Baja California Peninsula</a>, the northwestern state of <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, the northern altiplano, and also significant portions of the southern Altiplano. Rainfall in these regions averages between 300 and 600 millimeters (11.8 and 23.6 in) per year, with lower amounts across Baja California Norte. Average rainfall totals are between 600 and 1,000 millimeters (23.6 and 39.4 in) in most of the major populated areas of the southern altiplano, including <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guadalajara,_Jalisco" title="Guadalajara, Jalisco">Guadalajara</a>. Low-lying areas along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> receive in excess of 1,000 millimeters (39.4 in) of rainfall in an average year, with the wettest region being the southeastern state of Tabasco, which typically receives approximately 2,000 millimeters (78.7 in) of rainfall on an annual basis. Parts of the northern altiplano, highlands and high peaks in the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre Occidental</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Oriental" title="Sierra Madre Oriental">Sierra Madre Oriental</a> occasionally receive significant snowfalls.</p>
<p>Mexico has pronounced wet and dry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons" title="Seasons">seasons</a>. Most of the country experiences a rainy season from June to mid-October and significantly less rain during the remainder of the year. February and July generally are the driest and wettest months, respectively. <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, for example, receives an average of only 5 millimeters (0.2 in) of rain during February but more than 160 millimeters (6.3 in) in July. Coastal areas, especially those along the Gulf of Mexico, experience the largest amounts of rain in September. <a href="/wiki/Tabasco" title="Tabasco">Tabasco</a> typically records more than 300 millimeters (11.8 in) of rain during that month. A small coastal area of northwestern coastal Mexico around <a href="/wiki/Tijuana" title="Tijuana">Tijuana</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> with considerable coastal fog and a rainy season that occurs in winter.</p>
<p>Tropical cyclones track near and along the western Mexican coastline primarily between the months of July and September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> These storms enhance the monsoon circulation over northwest Mexico and the southwest <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On an average basis, eastern Pacific tropical cyclones contribute about one-third of the annual rainfall along the Mexican Riviera, and up to one-half of the rainfall seen annually across Baja California Sur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Mexico is twice as likely (18% of the basin total) to be impacted by a Pacific tropical cyclone on its west coast than an Atlantic tropical cyclone on its east coast (9% of the basin total). The three most struck states in Mexico in the 50 years at the end of the 20th century were <a href="/wiki/Baja_California_Sur" title="Baja California Sur">Baja California Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa" title="Sinaloa">Sinaloa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_rainfall_climatology" title="United States rainfall climatology">United States rainfall climatology</a></div>
<p>Late <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall" title="Fall">fall</a> <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> bring a majority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> which falls across western, southern, and southeast <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> annually. During the fall, winter, and <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a>, Pacific storm systems bring most of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and the western United States much of their precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHorel_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-JHorel-45">[45]</a></sup> Nor'easters moving up the East coast bring cold season precipitation to the Mid-Atlantic and New England states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> During the summer, the Southwest <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> combined with <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> moisture moving around the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> in the Atlantic Ocean bring the promise of afternoon and evening thunderstorms to the southern tier of the country as well as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHorel_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-JHorel-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> enhance precipitation across southern sections of the country,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a>. Over the top of the ridge, the jet stream brings a summer precipitation maximum to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. Large thunderstorm areas known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_Complex" title="Mesoscale Convective Complex">mesoscale convective complexes</a> move through the Plains, Midwest, and Great Lakes during the warm season, contributing up to 10% of the annual precipitation to the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a> affects the precipitation distribution, by altering rainfall patterns across the West,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Midwest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> the Southeast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and throughout the tropics. There is also evidence that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is leading to increased precipitation to the eastern portions of North America, while droughts are becoming more frequent in the tropics and subtropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The eastern half of the contiguous United States east of the <a href="/wiki/98th_meridian_west" title="98th meridian west">98th meridian</a>, the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range are the wetter portions of the nation, with average rainfall exceeding 30 inches (760 mm) per year. The drier areas are the Desert Southwest, Great Basin, valleys of northeast Arizona, eastern Utah, central Wyoming, eastern Oregon and Washington and the northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Peninsula" title="Olympic Peninsula">Olympic Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Waialeale" title="Mount Waialeale">Mount Waialeale</a> on the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaua%CA%BBi" title="Kauaʻi">Kauaʻi</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a> averages more than 11,680 millimetres (460 in) of rain over the last 32 years, with a record 17,340 millimetres (683 in) in 1982. Its summit is considered one of the rainiest spots on earth. It has been promoted in tourist literature for many years as the wettest spot in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The annual average rainfall maxima across the continent lie across the northwest from northwest Brazil into northern Peru, Colombia, and Ecuador, then along the Atlantic coast of the Guyanas and far northern Brazil, as well as within the southern half of Chile. <a href="/wiki/Llor%C3%B3" title="Lloró">Lloró</a>, a town situated in <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Chocó Department">Chocó</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, is probably the place with the largest measured rainfall in the world, averaging 13,300 mm per year (523.6 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDCxrain_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCDCxrain-60">[60]</a></sup> In fact, the whole Department of Chocó is extraordinarily humid. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tutunendo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tutunendo (page does not exist)">Tutunendo</a>, a small town situated in the same department, is one of the wettest places on earth, averaging 11,394 mm per year (448 in); in 1974 the town received 26,303 mm (86 ft 3½ in), the largest annual rainfall measured in Colombia. Unlike Cherrapunji, which receives most of its rainfall between April and September, Tutunendo receives rain almost uniformly distributed throughout the year. The months of January and February have somewhat less frequent storms. On average, Tutunendo has 280 days with rainfall per year. Over ⅔ of the rain (68%) falls during the night. The average relative humidity is 90% and the average temperature is 26.4 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quibd%C3%B3" title="Quibdó">Quibdó</a>, the capital of Chocó, receives the most rain in the world among cities with over 100,000 inhabitants: 9,000 millimetres (350 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDCxrain_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCDCxrain-60">[60]</a></sup> Storms in Chocó can drop 500 mm (19.7 in) of rainfall in a day. This amount is more than falls in many cities in a year's time. The <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> mountain range blocks <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> moisture that arrives in that continent, resulting in a desertlike climate just downwind across western Argentina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andes_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andes-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_heat_island_impacts">Urban heat island impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Urban heat island impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">Urban heat island</a></div>
<p>Aside from the effect on temperature, urban heat islands (UHIs) can produce secondary effects on local meteorology, including the altering of local wind patterns, the development of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, and the rates of precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The extra heat provided by the UHI leads to greater upward motion, which can induce additional shower and thunderstorm activity. Rainfall rates downwind of cities are increased between 48% and 116%. Partly as a result of this warming, monthly rainfall is about 28% greater between 20 miles (32 km) to 40 miles (64 km) downwind of cities, compared with upwind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Some cities show a total precipitation increase of 51%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Using satellite images, researchers discovered that city climates have a noticeable influence on plant growing seasons up to 10 kilometers (6 mi) away from a city's edges. Growing seasons in 70 cities in eastern North America were about 15 days longer in urban areas compared to rural areas outside of a city's influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
