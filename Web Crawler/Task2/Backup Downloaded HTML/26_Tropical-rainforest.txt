<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical rainforest</h1>

<p><b>Tropical rainforests</b> occur in areas of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate" title="Tropical rainforest climate">tropical rainforest climate</a> in which there is no <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> – all months have an average <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> of at least 60 mm – and may also be referred to as <i>lowland equatorial evergreen rainforest</i>. True rainforests are typically found between 10 degrees north and south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> (see map); they are a sub-set of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forest</a> biome that occurs roughly within the 28 degree latitudes (in the equatorial zone between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>). Within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>'s biome classification, tropical rainforests are a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_moist_broadleaf_forest" title="Tropical moist broadleaf forest">tropical moist broadleaf forest</a> (or tropical wet forest) that also includes the more extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_seasonal_forest" title="Tropical seasonal forest">tropical seasonal forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rio_Madre_de_Dios,_Peru.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG/300px-Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG/450px-Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG/600px-Rio_Madre_de_Dios%2C_Peru.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rio_Madre_de_Dios,_Peru.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> rain forest in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical rainforests can be characterized in two words: hot and wet. Mean monthly temperatures exceed 18 °C (64 °F) during all months of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Average annual rainfall is no less than 1,680 mm (66 in) and can exceed 10 m (390 in) although it typically lies between 1,750 mm (69 in) and 3,000 mm (120 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup> This high level of precipitation often results in poor soils due to leaching of soluble nutrients in the ground.</p>
<p>Tropical rainforests exhibit high levels of biodiversity. Around 40% to 75% of all biotic <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_(ecology)" title="Indigenous (ecology)">indigenous</a> to the rainforests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variables_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variables-6">[6]</a></sup> Rainforests are home to half of all the living animal and plant species on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MI-7">[7]</a></sup> Two-thirds of all flowering plants can be found in rainforests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup> A single hectare of rainforest may contain 42,000 different species of insect, up to 807 trees of 313 species and 1,500 species of higher plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup> Tropical rainforests have been called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_largest_pharmacy_(idiom)" title="World's largest pharmacy (idiom)">world's largest pharmacy</a>", because over one quarter of natural <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicines</a> have been discovered within them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is likely that there may be many millions of species of plants, insects and <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> still undiscovered in tropical rainforests.</p>
<p>Tropical rainforests are among the most threatened ecosystems globally due to large-scale fragmentation as a result of human activity. <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">Habitat fragmentation</a> caused by geological processes such as volcanism and climate change occurred in the past, and have been identified as important drivers of speciation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-10">[10]</a></sup> However, fast human driven habitat destruction is suspected to be one of the major causes of species extinction. Tropical rain forests have been subjected to heavy <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">agricultural clearance</a> throughout the 20th century, and the area covered by rainforests around the world is rapidly shrinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h3>
<p>Tropical rainforests have existed on earth for hundreds of millions of years. Most tropical rainforests today are on fragments of the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic era</a> supercontinent of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorlettandPrimack2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorlettandPrimack2006-13">[13]</a></sup> The separation of the landmass resulted in a great loss of amphibian diversity while at the same time the drier climate spurred the diversification of reptiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-10">[10]</a></sup> The division left tropical rainforests located in five major regions of the world: tropical America, Africa, Southeast Asia, Madagascar, and New Guinea, with smaller outliers in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorlettandPrimack2006_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-CorlettandPrimack2006-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the specifics of the origin of rainforests remain uncertain due to an incomplete fossil record.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_tropical_forest">Types of tropical forest</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">Tropical forest</a></div>
<p>Several biomes may appear similar-to, or merge via <a href="/wiki/Ecotone" title="Ecotone">ecotones</a> with, tropical rainforest:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Moist tropical seasonal forest</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg/220px-Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg/330px-Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg/440px-Rain_Forest_Daintree_Australia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daintree_rainforest" title="Daintree rainforest">Daintree</a> "rainforest" in Queensland is actually a <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_tropical_forest" title="Seasonal tropical forest">seasonal tropical forest</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Moist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_seasonal_forest" title="Tropical seasonal forest">tropical seasonal forests</a> receive high overall rainfall with a warm summer wet season and a cooler winter dry season. Some trees in these forests drop some or all of their leaves during the winter dry season, thus they are sometimes called "tropical mixed forest". They are found in parts of South America, in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and around the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, in coastal <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, parts of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, and across much of <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Montane rainforests</dt>
</dl>
<p>These are found in cooler-climate mountainous areas, becoming are known as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forests</a> at higher elevations. Depending on latitude, the lower limit of montane rainforests on large mountains is generally between 1500 and 2500 m while the upper limit is usually from 2400 to 3300 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruijnzeel_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruijnzeel-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Flooded rainforests</dt>
</dl>
<p>Tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater_swamp_forests" title="Freshwater swamp forests">freshwater swamp forests</a>, or "flooded forests", are found in Amazon basin (the <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/V%C3%A1rzea" title="Várzea">Várzea</a>) and elsewhere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_structure">Forest structure</span></h2>
<p>Rainforests are divided into different strata, or layers, with vegetation organized into a vertical pattern from the top of the soil to the canopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golley_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golley-15">[15]</a></sup> Each layer is a unique biotic community containing different plants and animals adapted for life in that particular strata. Only the emergent layer is unique to tropical rainforests, while the others are also found in temperate rainforests.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_floor">Forest floor</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="763" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg/220px-Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg/330px-Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg/440px-Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gorilla_gorilla04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Western lowland gorilla</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The forest floor, the bottom-most layer, receives only 2% of the sunlight. Only plants <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to low light can grow in this region. Away from riverbanks, swamps and clearings, where dense undergrowth is found, the forest floor is relatively clear of vegetation because of the low sunlight penetration. This more open quality permits the easy movement of larger animals such as: ungulates like the <a href="/wiki/Okapi" title="Okapi">okapi</a> (<i>Okapia johnstoni</i>), <a href="/wiki/Tapir" title="Tapir">tapir</a> (<i>Tapirus</i> sp.), <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_rhinoceros" title="Sumatran rhinoceros">Sumatran rhinoceros</a> (<i>Dicerorhinus sumatrensis</i>), and apes like the <a href="/wiki/Western_lowland_gorilla" title="Western lowland gorilla">western lowland gorilla</a> (<i>Gorilla gorilla</i>), as well as many species of reptiles, amphibians, and insects. The forest floor also contains <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decaying</a> plant and animal matter, which disappears quickly, because the warm, humid conditions promote rapid decay. Many forms of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> growing here help decay the animal and plant waste.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Understory_layer">Understory layer</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">Understory</a></div>
<p>The understory layer lies between the canopy and the forest floor. The understory is home to a number of birds, small mammals, insects, reptiles, and predators. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a> (<i>Panthera pardus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Poison_dart_frog" title="Poison dart frog">poison dart frogs</a> (<i>Dendrobates</i> sp.), <a href="/wiki/South_American_coati" title="South American coati">ring-tailed coati</a> (<i>Nasua nasua</i>), <a href="/wiki/Boa_constrictor" title="Boa constrictor">boa constrictor</a> (<i>Boa constrictor</i>), and many species of <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">Coleoptera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup> The vegetation at this layer generally consists of shade-tolerant shrubs, herbs, small trees, and large woody vines which climb into the trees to capture sunlight. Only about 5% of sunlight breaches the canopy to arrive at the understory causing true understory plants to seldom grow to 3 m (10 feet). As an adaptation to these low light levels, understory plants have often evolved much larger leaves. Many seedlings that will grow to the canopy level are in the understory.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FRIM_canopy.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/FRIM_canopy.JPG/220px-FRIM_canopy.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/FRIM_canopy.JPG/330px-FRIM_canopy.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/FRIM_canopy.JPG/440px-FRIM_canopy.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

The canopy at the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Research_Institute_Malaysia" title="Forest Research Institute Malaysia">Forest Research Institute Malaysia</a></div>
</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canopy_layer">Canopy layer</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(ecology)" title="Canopy (ecology)">Canopy (ecology)</a></div>
<p>The canopy is the primary layer of the forest forming a roof over the two remaining layers. It contains the majority of the largest trees, typically 30–45 m in height. Tall, broad-leaved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evergreen_trees" title="Evergreen trees">evergreen trees</a> are the dominant plants. The densest areas of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> are found in the forest canopy, as it often supports a rich flora of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphytes" title="Epiphytes">epiphytes</a>, including orchids, bromeliads, mosses and lichens. These epiphytic plants attach to trunks and branches and obtain water and minerals from rain and debris that collects on the supporting plants. The fauna is similar to that found in the emergent layer, but more diverse. It is suggested that the total arthropod species richness of the tropical canopy might be as high as 20 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erwin_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erwin-16">[16]</a></sup> Other species habituating this layer include many avian species such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow-casqued_wattled_hornbill" title="Yellow-casqued wattled hornbill">yellow-casqued wattled hornbill</a> (<i>Ceratogymna elata</i>), <a href="/wiki/Collared_sunbird" title="Collared sunbird">collared sunbird</a> (<i>Anthreptes collaris</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_gray_parrot" title="African gray parrot">African gray parrot</a> (<i>Psitacus erithacus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Keel-billed_toucan" title="Keel-billed toucan">keel-billed toucan</a> (<i>Ramphastos sulfuratus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_macaw" title="Scarlet macaw">scarlet macaw</a> (<i>Ara macao</i>) as well as other animals like the <a href="/wiki/Spider_monkey" title="Spider monkey">spider monkey</a> (<i>Ateles</i> sp.), African giant swallowtail (<i><a href="/wiki/Papilio_antimachus" title="Papilio antimachus">Papilio antimachus</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Three-toed_sloth" title="Three-toed sloth">three-toed sloth</a> (<i>Bradypus tridactylus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Kinkajou" title="Kinkajou">kinkajou</a> (<i>Potos flavus</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Tamandua" title="Tamandua">tamandua</a> (<i>Tamandua tetradactyla</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergent_layer">Emergent layer</span></h3>
<p>The emergent layer contains a small number of very large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trees" title="Trees">trees</a>, called <i>emergents</i>, which grow above the general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a>, reaching heights of 45–55 m, although on occasion a few species will grow to 70–80 m tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golley_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Golley-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Some examples of emergents include: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Balizia_elegans&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Balizia elegans (page does not exist)">Balizia elegans</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dipteryx_panamensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dipteryx panamensis (page does not exist)">Dipteryx panamensis</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hieronyma_alchorneoides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hieronyma alchorneoides (page does not exist)">Hieronyma alchorneoides</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hymenolobium_mesoamericanum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hymenolobium mesoamericanum (page does not exist)">Hymenolobium mesoamericanum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lecythis_ampla" title="Lecythis ampla">Lecythis ampla</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terminalia_oblonga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terminalia oblonga (page does not exist)">Terminalia oblonga</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-King-18">[18]</a></sup> These trees need to be able to withstand the hot temperatures and strong winds that occur above the canopy in some areas. Several unique faunal species inhabit this layer such as the <a href="/wiki/Crowned_eagle" title="Crowned eagle">crowned eagle</a> (<i>Stephanoaetus coronatus</i>), the <a href="/wiki/King_colobus" title="King colobus">king colobus</a> (<i>Colobus polykomos</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Large_flying_fox" title="Large flying fox">large flying fox</a> (<i>Pteropus vampyrus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Newman.2C_Arnold_2002-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(vegetation)" title="Stratification (vegetation)">stratification</a> is not always clear. Rainforests are dynamic and many changes affect the structure of the forest. Emergent or canopy trees collapse, for example, causing gaps to form. Openings in the forest canopy are widely recognized as important for the establishment and growth of rainforest trees. It’s estimated that perhaps 75% of the tree species at La Selva Biological Station, Costa Rica are dependent on canopy opening for seed germination or for growth beyond sapling size, for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denslow_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Denslow-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_(2).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2376" data-file-width="4224" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG/220px-El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG/330px-El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG/440px-El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_%282%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:El_bosque_inundado_amazonico-cosmocaixa-2009_(2).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Artificial tropical rainforest in Barcelona</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical rainforests are located around and near the equator, therefore having what is called an equatorial climate characterized by three major climatic parameters: temperature, rainfall, and dry season intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MalhiandWright2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-MalhiandWright2004-20">[20]</a></sup> Other parameters that affect tropical rainforests are carbon dioxide concentrations, solar radiation, and nitrogen availability. In general, climatic patterns consist of warm temperatures and high annual rainfall. However, the abundance of rainfall changes throughout the year creating distinct moist and dry seasons. Tropical forests are <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests">classified</a> by the amount of rainfall received each year, which has allowed ecologists to define differences in these forests that look so similar in structure. According to <a href="/wiki/Holdridge_life_zones" title="Holdridge life zones">Holdridge’s classification</a> of tropical ecosystems, true tropical rainforests have an annual rainfall greater than 2 m and annual temperature greater than 24 degrees Celsius, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_evapotranspiration" title="Potential evapotranspiration">potential evapotranspiration</a> ratio (PET) value of &lt;0.25. However, most lowland <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a> can be classified as tropical moist or wet forests, which differ in regards to rainfall. Tropical forest ecology- dynamics, composition, and function- are sensitive to changes in climate especially changes in rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MalhiandWright2004_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-MalhiandWright2004-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soils">Soils</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_types">Soil types</span></h4>
<p>Soil types are highly variable in the tropics and are the result of a combination of several variables such as climate, vegetation, topographic position, parent material, and soil age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aragao2009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aragao2009-21">[21]</a></sup> Most tropical soils are characterized by significant <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> and poor nutrients, however there are some areas that contain fertile soils. Soils throughout the tropical rainforests fall into two classifications which include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultisols" title="Ultisols">ultisols</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxisols" title="Oxisols">oxisols</a>. Ultisols are known as well weathered, acidic red clay soils, deficient in major nutrients such as calcium and potassium. Similarly, oxisols are acidic, old, typically reddish, highly weathered and leached, however are well drained compared to ultisols. The clay content of ultisols is high, making it difficult for water to penetrate and flow through. The reddish color of both soils is the result of heavy heat and moisture forming oxides of iron and aluminium, which are insoluble in water and not taken up readily by plants.</p>
<p>Soil chemical and physical characteristics are strongly related to above ground productivity and forest structure and dynamics. The physical properties of soil control the tree turnover rates whereas chemical properties such as available nitrogen and phosphorus control forest growth rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moreira_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moreira-22">[22]</a></sup> The soils of the eastern and central Amazon as well as the Southeast Asian Rainforest are old and mineral poor whereas the soils of the western Amazon (Ecuador and Peru) and volcanic areas of Costa Rica are young and mineral rich. Primary productivity or wood production is highest in western Amazon and lowest in eastern Amazon which contains heavily weathered soils classified as oxisols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aragao2009_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aragao2009-21">[21]</a></sup> Additionally, Amazonian soils are greatly weathered, making them devoid of minerals like phosphorus, potassium, calcium, and magnesium, which come from rock sources. However, not all tropical rainforests occur on nutrient poor soils, but on nutrient rich floodplains and volcanic soils located in the Andean foothills, and volcanic areas of Southeast Asia, Africa, and Central America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oxisols, infertile, deeply weathered and severely leached, have developed on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwanan</a> <a href="/wiki/Shield_(geology)" title="Shield (geology)">shields</a>. Rapid bacterial decay prevents the accumulation of humus. The concentration of iron and aluminium oxides by the <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterization</a> process gives the oxisols a bright red color and sometimes produces minable deposits (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>). On younger substrates, especially of volcanic origin, tropical soils may be quite fertile.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_recycling">Nutrient recycling</span></h4>
<p>This high rate of decomposition is the result of phosphorus levels in the soils, precipitation, high temperatures and the extensive microorganism communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland2006_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland2006-24">[24]</a></sup> In addition to the bacteria and other microorganisms, there are an abundance of other <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposers</a> such as fungi and termites that aid in the process as well. Nutrient recycling is important because below ground resource availability controls the above ground biomass and community structure of tropical rainforests. These soils are typically phosphorus limited, which inhibits net primary productivity or the uptake of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aragao2009_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Aragao2009-21">[21]</a></sup> The soil contains microbial organisms such as bacteria, which break down leaf litter and other organic matter into inorganic forms of carbon usable by plants through a process called decomposition. During the decomposition process the microbial community is respiring, taking up oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide. The decomposition rate can be evaluated by measuring the uptake of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland2006_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland2006-24">[24]</a></sup> High temperatures and precipitation increase decomposition rate, which allows plant litter to rapidly decay in tropical regions, releasing nutrients that are immediately taken up by plants through surface or ground waters. The seasonal patterns in respiration are controlled by leaf litter fall and precipitation, the driving force moving the decomposable carbon from the litter to the soil. Respiration rates are highest early in the wet season because the recent dry season results in a large percentage of leaf litter and thus a higher percentage of organic matter being leached into the soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland2006_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland2006-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buttress_roots">Buttress roots</span></h4>
<p>A common feature of many tropical rainforests is the distinct <a href="/wiki/Buttress_root" title="Buttress root">buttress roots</a> of trees. Instead of penetrating to deeper soil layers, buttress roots create a widespread root network at the surface for more efficient uptake of nutrients in a very nutrient poor and competitive environment. Most of the nutrients within the soil of a tropical rainforest occur near the surface because of the rapid turnover time and decomposition of organisms and leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2010_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young2010-25">[25]</a></sup> Because of this, the buttress roots occur at the surface so the trees can maximize uptake and actively compete with the rapid uptake of other trees. These roots also aid in water uptake and storage, increase surface area for gas exchange, and collect leaf litter for added nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2010_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young2010-25">[25]</a></sup> Additionally, these roots reduce soil erosion and maximize nutrient acquisition during heavy rains by diverting nutrient rich water flowing down the trunk into several smaller flows while also acting as a barrier to ground flow. Also, the large surface areas these roots create provide support and stability to rainforests trees, which commonly grow to significant heights. This added stability allows these trees to withstand the impacts of severe storms, thus reducing the occurrence of fallen trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2010_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Young2010-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_succession">Forest succession</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">Succession</a> is an ecological process that changes the biotic community structure over time towards a more stable, diverse community structure after an initial disturbance to the community. The initial disturbance is often a natural phenomenon or human caused event. Natural disturbances include hurricanes, volcanic eruptions, river movements or an event as small as a fallen tree that creates gaps in the forest. In tropical rainforests, these same natural disturbances have been well documented in the fossil record, and are credited with encouraging speciation and endemism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical rainforests exhibit a vast diversity in plant and animal species. The root for this remarkable speciation has been a query of scientists and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologists" title="Ecologists">ecologists</a> for years. A number of theories have been developed for why and how the tropics can be so diverse.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interspecific_competition">Interspecific competition</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Interspecific_competition" title="Interspecific competition">Interspecific competition</a> results from a high density of species with similar niches in the tropics and limited resources available. Species which "lose" the competition may either become extinct or find a new niche. Direct competition will often lead to one species dominating another by some advantage, ultimately driving it to extinction. Niche partitioning is the other option for a species. This is the separation and rationing of necessary resources by utilizing different habitats, food sources, cover or general behavioral differences. A species with similar food items but different feeding times is an example of niche partitioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleistocene_refugia">Pleistocene refugia</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">theory of Pleistocene refugia</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Haffer" title="Jürgen Haffer">Jürgen Haffer</a> in 1969 with his article <i>Speciation of Amazonian Forest Birds</i>. Haffer proposed the explanation for speciation was the product of rainforest patches being separated by stretches of non forest vegetation during the last glacial period. He called these patches of rainforest areas refuges and within these patches allopatric speciation occurred. With the end of the glacial period and increase in atmospheric humidity, rainforest began to expand and the refuges reconnected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haffer_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haffer-27">[27]</a></sup> This theory has been the subject of debate. Scientists are still skeptical of whether or not this theory is legitimate. Genetic evidence suggests speciation had occurred in certain taxa 1–2 million years ago, preceding the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moritz_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moritz-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_dimensions">Human dimensions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitation">Habitation</span></h3>
<p>Tropical rainforests have harboured human life for many millennia, with many Indian tribes in South- and Central America, who belong to the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous peoples of the Americas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Congo_Pygmies" title="Congo Pygmies">Congo Pygmies</a> in Central Africa, and several tribes in South-East Asia, like the <a href="/wiki/Dayak_people" title="Dayak people">Dayak people</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Penan_people" title="Penan people">Penan people</a> in <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012-29">[29]</a></sup> Food resources within the forest are extremely dispersed due to the high biological diversity and what food does exist is largely restricted to the canopy and requires considerable energy to obtain. Some groups of hunter-gatherers have exploited rainforest on a seasonal basis but dwelt primarily in adjacent savanna and open <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environments</a> where food is much more abundant. Other people described as rainforest dwellers are hunter-gatherers who subsist in large part by trading high value forest products such as hides, feathers, and honey with agricultural people living outside the forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_peoples">Indigenous peoples</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous peoples</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="699" data-file-width="1000" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg/220px-%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg/330px-%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg/440px-%C3%8Dndios_isolados_no_Acre_7.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>A variety of indigenous people live within the rainforest as hunter-gatherers, or subsist as part-time small scale farmers supplemented in large part by trading high-value forest products such as hides, feathers, and honey with agricultural people living outside the forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-30">[30]</a></sup> Peoples have inhabited the rainforests for tens of thousands of years and have remained so elusive that only recently have some tribes been discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012-29">[29]</a></sup> These indigenous peoples are greatly threatened by loggers in search for old-growth tropical hardwoods like Ipe, Cumaru and Wenge, and by farmers who are looking to expand their land, for cattle(meat), and soybeans, which are used to feed cattle in Europe and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-human-occupation-of-tropical-rainforests-intro-2012-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> On 18 January 2007, <a href="/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Nacional_do_%C3%8Dndio" title="Fundação Nacional do Índio">FUNAI</a> reported also that it had confirmed the presence of 67 different <a href="/wiki/Uncontacted_peoples" title="Uncontacted peoples">uncontacted tribes</a> in Brazil, up from 40 in 2005. With this addition, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> has now overtaken the island of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> as the country having the largest number of uncontacted tribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irian_Jaya" title="Irian Jaya">Irian Jaya</a> or West Papua in the island of New Guinea is home to an estimated 44 uncontacted tribal groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pygmy peoples are hunter-gatherer groups living in equatorial rainforests characterized by their short height (below one and a half meters, or 59 inches, on average). Amongst this group are the Efe, Aka, <a href="/wiki/Twa" title="Twa">Twa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baka_people_(Cameroon_and_Gabon)" title="Baka people (Cameroon and Gabon)">Baka</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbuti" title="Mbuti">Mbuti</a> people of Central Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> However, the term pygmy is considered pejorative so many tribes prefer not to be labeled as such.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-focus_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-focus-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some notable <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a>, or Amerindians, include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huaorani" title="Huaorani">Huaorani</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ya%CC%A7nomam%C3%B6" title="Ya̧nomamö">Ya̧nomamö</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kayapo" title="Kayapo">Kayapo</a> people of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon</a>. The traditional agricultural system practiced by tribes in the Amazon is based on swidden cultivation (also known as <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> or shifting cultivation) and is considered a relatively benign disturbance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dufour_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dufour-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herrera_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera-39">[39]</a></sup> In fact, when looking at the level of individual swidden plots a number of traditional farming practices are considered beneficial. For example, the use of shade trees and fallowing all help preserve soil organic matter, which is a critical factor in the maintenance of soil fertility in the deeply weathered and leached soils common in the Amazon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewel_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ewel-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a diversity of forest people in Asia, including the <a href="/wiki/Lumad" title="Lumad">Lumad</a> peoples of the Philippines and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penan" title="Penan">Penan</a> and Dayak people of Borneo. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dayaks" title="Dayaks">Dayaks</a> are a particularly interesting group as they are noted for their traditional headhunting culture. Fresh human heads were required to perform certain rituals such as the Iban "kenyalang" and the Kenyah "mamat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jessup_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jessup-41">[41]</a></sup> Pygmies who live in Southeast Asia are, amongst others, referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Negrito" title="Negrito">Negrito</a>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources">Resources</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivated_foods_and_spices">Cultivated foods and spices</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yam_(vegetable)" title="Yam (vegetable)">Yam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mango" title="Mango">mango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papaya" title="Papaya">papaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macadamia" title="Macadamia">macadamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> all originally came from tropical rainforest and are still mostly grown on plantations in regions that were formerly primary forest. In the mid-1980s and 1990s, 40 million tons of bananas were consumed worldwide each year, along with 13 million tons of mango. Central American coffee exports were worth US$3 billion in 1970. Much of the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic variation</a> used in evading the damage caused by new <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> is still derived from resistant wild stock. Tropical forests have supplied 250 cultivated kinds of <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>, compared to only 20 for <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate forests</a>. Forests in <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> alone contain 251 tree species with edible fruits, of which only 43 had been established as cultivated crops by 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_services">Ecosystem services</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a></div>
<p>In addition to extractive human uses, rain forests also have non-extractive uses that are frequently summarized as <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>. Rain forests play an important role in maintaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_diversity" title="Biological diversity">biological diversity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestering and storing carbon</a>, global climate regulation, <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> control, and <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monteverde_puente.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Monteverde_puente.jpg/220px-Monteverde_puente.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Monteverde_puente.jpg/330px-Monteverde_puente.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Monteverde_puente.jpg/440px-Monteverde_puente.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monteverde_puente.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Canopy_walkway" title="Canopy walkway">Canopy walkway</a> for seeing the diverse tropical forest in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite the negative effects of tourism in the tropical rainforests, there are also several important positive effects.</p>
<ul>
<li>In recent years <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a> in the tropics has increased. While rainforests are becoming increasingly rare, people are travelling to nations that still have this diverse habitat. Locals are benefiting from the additional income brought in by visitors, as well areas deemed interesting for visitors are often conserved. <i>Ecotourism can be an incentive for conservation, especially when it triggers positive economic change</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stronza_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stronza-44">[44]</a></sup> Ecotourism can include a variety of activities including animal viewing, scenic jungle tours and even viewing cultural sights and native villages. If these practices are performed appropriately this can be beneficial for both locals and the present flora and fauna.</li>
<li>An increase in tourism has increased economic support, allowing more revenue to go into the protection of the habitat. Tourism can contribute directly to the conservation of sensitive areas and habitat. Revenue from park-entrance fees and similar sources can be utilised specifically to pay for the protection and management of environmentally sensitive areas. Revenue from taxation and tourism provides an additional incentive for governments to contribute revenue to the protection of the forest.</li>
<li>Tourism also has the potential to increase public appreciation of the environment and to spread awareness of environmental problems when it brings people into closer contact with the environment. Such increased awareness can induce more environmentally conscious behavior. Tourism has had a positive effect on wildlife preservation and protection efforts, notably in Africa but also in South America, Asia, Australia, and the South Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Rainforest#Deforestation" title="Rainforest">Rainforest § Deforestation</a></div>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining_and_drilling">Mining and drilling</span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OkTediMine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/OkTediMine.jpg/220px-OkTediMine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/OkTediMine.jpg/330px-OkTediMine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/OkTediMine.jpg/440px-OkTediMine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OkTediMine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Ok_Tedi_Mine" title="Ok Tedi Mine">Ok Tedi Mine</a> in southwestern Papua New Guinea</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Deposits of precious metals (<a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">coltan</a>) and fossil fuels (<a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) occur underneath rainforests globally. These resources are important to developing nations and their extraction is often given priority to encourage economic growth. <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_and_gas_drilling" title="Oil and gas drilling">drilling</a> can require large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land development</a>, directly causing <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, a West African nation, deforestation from decades of mining activity left about 12% of the country's original rainforest intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_agricultural_land">Conversion to agricultural land</span></h5>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">invention of agriculture</a>, humans were able to clear sections of rainforest to produce crops, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn#Historical_background" title="Slash and burn">converting it to open farmland</a>. Such people, however, obtain their food primarily from farm plots cleared from the forest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_L._Walker_1998_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_L._Walker_1998-47">[47]</a></sup> and hunt and forage within the forest to supplement this. The issue arising is between the independent farmer providing for his family and the needs and wants of the globe as a whole. This issue has seen little improvement because no plan has been established for all parties to be aided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomich_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomich-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculture on formerly forested land is not without difficulties. Rainforest soils are often thin and leached of many minerals, and the heavy rainfall can quickly leach nutrients from area cleared for cultivation. People such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanomamo" title="Yanomamo">Yanomamo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon</a>, utilize <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> agriculture to overcome these limitations and enable them to push deep into what were previously rainforest environments. However, these are not rainforest dwellers, rather they are dwellers in cleared farmland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_L._Walker_1998_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_L._Walker_1998-47">[47]</a></sup> that make forays into the rainforest. Up to 90% of the typical Yanamomo diet comes from farmed plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_L._Walker_1998_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_L._Walker_1998-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some action has been taken by suggesting fallow periods of the land allowing secondary forest to grow and replenish the soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jong_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jong-49">[49]</a></sup> Beneficial practices like soil restoration and conservation can benefit the small farmer and allow better production on smaller parcels of land.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a></div>
<p>The tropics take a major role in reducing atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. The tropics (most notably the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>) are called <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As major carbon reducers and carbon and soil methane storages, their destruction contributes to increasing <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">global energy trapping</a>, atmospheric gases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Climate change has been significantly contributed to by the destruction of the rainforests. A simulation was performed in which all rainforest in Africa were removed. The simulation showed an increase in atmospheric temperature by 2.5 to 5 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semazzi_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Semazzi-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span></h3>
<p>Efforts to protect and conserve tropical rainforest habitats are diverse and widespread. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_conservation" title="Tropical rainforest conservation">Tropical rainforest conservation</a> ranges from strict preservation of habitat to finding sustainable management techniques for people living in tropical rainforests. International policy has also introduced a market incentive program called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reducing_Emissions_from_Deforestation_and_Forest_Degradation" title="Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation">Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation</a> (REDD) for companies and governments to outset their carbon emissions through financial investments into rainforest conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
