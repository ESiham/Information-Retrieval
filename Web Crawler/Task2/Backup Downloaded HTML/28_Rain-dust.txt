<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rain dust</h1>

<p><b>Rain dust</b> or <b>snow dust</b>, traditionally known as <b>muddy rain</b>, <b>red rain</b>, or <b>coloured rain</b>, is a variety of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> (or any other form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>) which contains enough <a href="/wiki/Mineral_dust" title="Mineral dust">desert dust</a> for the dust to be visible without using a microscope.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rain dust is common in the Western and Southern Mediterranean, where the dust supply comes from the atmospheric depressions going through the northern part of North Africa. The main sources of desert dust reach the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> in the form of dust transported by wind or rain from the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and Central <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-JGR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mud rains are relatively frequent and had been increasing in early 1990s in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also occurs in arid <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> such as west <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. It occasionally happens in the grasslands as it did in <a href="/wiki/Bexar_County,_Texas" title="Bexar County, Texas">Bexar County, Texas</a> on March 18, 2008.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_composition">Dust composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dust composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rain dust is very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-JGR-1">[1]</a></sup> Some of the large particles contain mixtures of chemicals such as sulfate and sea salt (chiefly with sodium, chlorine and magnesium). Major minerals in order of decreasing abundance are: <a href="/wiki/Illite" title="Illite">illite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smectite" title="Smectite">smectite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palygorskite" title="Palygorskite">palygorskite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-JGR-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">Majorca</a> a study finds that the size, by volume, 89% of the particles from rain dust fraction corresponded to <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> (between 0.002 mm and 0.063 mm) and that there was virtually no <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> sized particles (less than 0.29%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The particulates that rain dust carries are important for the formation of long-term <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, counteracting, in large part the effects of <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>. The amount of solids in rain dust have been estimated at 5.3 grams per m<sup>2</sup> (in a study made in <a href="/wiki/Montseny" title="Montseny">Montseny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in this location the dust provides 34% of the calcium needed by the <a href="/wiki/Quercus_ilex" title="Quercus ilex">holm oak</a>. The amount of the deposition of dust particles is highly variable depending on the year.</li>
<li>Saharan dust significantly increases the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of rain water. This may counteract the effects of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">Radioactivity</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chernobyl_Disaster" title="Chernobyl Disaster">Chernobyl Disaster</a> was carried by rain dust to Greece in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood.2Fred_rain">Blood/red rain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blood/red rain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rain dust is the most common cause of <a href="/wiki/Blood_rain" title="Blood rain">blood rain</a>.</p>
<p>Red rain is however not always a rain dust, see for example the <a href="/wiki/Red_rain_in_Kerala" title="Red rain in Kerala">Red rain in Kerala</a>.</p>
