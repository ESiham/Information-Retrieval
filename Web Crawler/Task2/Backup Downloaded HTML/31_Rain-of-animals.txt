<p><b>Raining animals</b> is a rare <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> phenomenon in which flightless animals fall from the sky. Such occurrences have been reported in many countries throughout history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_of_Congress_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Library_of_Congress-1">[1]</a></sup> One hypothesis is that <a href="/wiki/Waterspout#Tornadic" title="Waterspout">tornadic waterspouts</a> sometimes pick up creatures such as fish or frogs, and carry them for up to several miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_of_Congress_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Library_of_Congress-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, this aspect of the phenomenon has never been witnessed by scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amp.C3.A8re_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amp.C3.A8re-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_of_animals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rain of flightless animals and things has been reported throughout history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_of_Congress_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Library_of_Congress-1">[1]</a></sup> In the first century AD, Roman naturalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pliny_The_Elder" title="Pliny The Elder">Pliny The Elder</a> documented storms of frogs and fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1794, French soldiers witnessed toads fall from the sky during heavy rain at Lalain, near the French city of <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rural inhabitants in <a href="/wiki/Yoro" title="Yoro">Yoro</a>, Honduras, claim 'fish rain' happens there every summer, a phenomenon they call <a href="/wiki/Lluvia_de_Peces" title="Lluvia de Peces">Lluvia de Peces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations">Explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_of_animals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tornade003.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1780" data-file-width="1200" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Tornade003.jpg/150px-Tornade003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Tornade003.jpg/225px-Tornade003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Tornade003.jpg/300px-Tornade003.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tornade003.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tornadoes may lift up animals into the air and deposit them miles away.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>French physicist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="André-Marie Ampère">André-Marie Ampère</a> (1775 – 1836) was among the first scientists to take seriously accounts of raining animals. Addressing the Society of Natural Sciences,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> Ampère suggested that at times frogs and toads roam the countryside in large numbers, and that violent winds could pick them up and carry them great distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amp.C3.A8re_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amp.C3.A8re-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometimes the animals survive the fall, suggesting the animals are dropped shortly after extraction. Several witnesses of raining frogs describe the animals as startled but healthy, and exhibiting relatively normal behavior shortly after the event. In some incidents, the animals are frozen to death or even completely encased in ice. There are examples where the product of the rain is not intact animals, but shredded body parts. Some cases occur just after storms having strong winds, especially during tornadoes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, there have been many unconfirmed cases in which rainfalls of animals have occurred in fair weather and in the absence of strong winds or waterspouts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Given that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterspouts" title="Waterspouts">waterspouts</a> do not actually lift anything (the water droplets visible in the column are merely condensation), it is implausible to suggest they are capable of lifting fish from below the surface of the water and high into the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A better accepted scientific explanation involves <a href="/wiki/Waterspout#Tornadic" title="Waterspout">tornadic waterspouts</a>: a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> that forms over land and travels over the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_of_Congress_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Library_of_Congress-1">[1]</a></sup> Under this hypothesis, a tornadic waterspout transports animals to relatively high altitudes, carrying them over large distances. This hypothesis appears supported by the type of animals in these rains: small and light, usually aquatic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and by the suggestion that the rain of animals is often preceded by a storm. However, the theory does not account for how all the animals involved in each individual incident would be from only one <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, and not a group of similarly-sized animals from a single area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="603" data-file-width="1080" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png/300px-Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png/450px-Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png/600px-Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bat-supercell_encounter_NWS.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Doppler Image from Texas showing the collision of a thunderstorm with a group of bats in flight. The color red indicates the animals flying into the storm.</div>
</div>

<p>In the case of birds, storms may overcome a flock in flight, especially in times of migration. The image to the right shows an example wherein a group of bats is overtaken by a thunderstorm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the image, the bats are in the red zone, which corresponds to winds moving away from the radar station, and enter into a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> associated with a tornado (in green). These events may occur easily with birds, which can get killed in flight, or stunned and then fall (unlike flightless creatures, which first have to be lifted into the air by an outside force). Sometimes this happens in large groups, for instance, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-winged_Blackbird" title="Red-winged Blackbird">blackbirds</a> falling from the sky in <a href="/wiki/Beebe,_Arkansas" title="Beebe, Arkansas">Beebe, Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on December 31, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is common for birds to become disoriented (for example, because of bad weather or fireworks) and collide with objects such as trees or buildings, killing them or stunning them into falling to their death. The number of blackbirds killed in Beebe is not spectacular considering the size of their congregations, which can be in the millions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The event in Beebe, however, captured the imagination and led to more reports in the media of birds falling from the sky across the globe, such as in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> though many scientists claim such mass deaths are common occurrences but usually go unnoticed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In contrast, it is harder to find a plausible explanation for rains of terrestrial animals.</p>
<p>After a reported rain of fish in Singapore in 1861, French naturalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_de_la_Porte,_comte_de_Castelnau" title="François Louis de la Porte, comte de Castelnau">Francis de Laporte de Castelnau</a> established that a migration of <a href="/wiki/Walking_catfish" title="Walking catfish">walking catfish</a> had taken place. As these fish are capable of dragging themselves over land from one puddle to another,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> this accounted for their presence on the ground following the rain.</p>

<h2><span id='"Raining_cats_and_dogs"'></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Raining_cats_and_dogs.22">"Raining cats and dogs"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_of_animals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Raining cats and dogs"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="522" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg/250px-478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg/375px-478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg/500px-478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:478px-Pluie_de_chats.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 19th-century English cartoon illustrating the phrase "it is raining cats and dogs" (and "pitchforks" too)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The English idiom "it is raining cats and dogs", used to describe an especially <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Downpour" title="Downpour">heavy rain</a>, is of unknown etymology, and is not necessarily related to the "raining animals" phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-liberman-40">[40]</a></sup> The phrase (with "<a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">polecats</a>" instead of "cats") was used at least since the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laur_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-laur-42">[42]</a></sup> A number of improbable <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymologies</a> have been put forward to explain the phrase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>An "explanation" widely circulated by email claimed that in 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> when <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasant</a> homes were commonly <a href="/wiki/Thatching" title="Thatching">thatched</a>, animals could crawl into the thatch to find shelter from the elements, and would fall out during heavy rain. However, there seems to be no evidence in support of either assertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Drainage systems on buildings in 17th-century Europe were poor, and may have disgorged their contents during heavy showers, including the corpses of any animals that had accumulated in them. This occurrence is documented in <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>'s 1710 poem 'Description of a City Shower', in which he describes <i>"Drowned puppies, stinking sprats, all drenched in mud,/Dead cats and turnip-tops come tumbling down the flood."</i></li>
<li>"Cats and dogs" may be a corruption of the Greek word <i>Katadoupoi</i>, referring to the waterfalls on the Nile,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-liberman-40">[40]</a></sup> possibly through the old French word <i>catadupe</i> ("waterfall").</li>
<li>The Greek phrase "kata doksa", which means "contrary to expectation" is often applied to heavy rain, but there is no evidence to support the theory that it was borrowed by English speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-liberman-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There may not be a logical explanation; the phrase may have been used just for its nonsensical humor value, like other equivalent English expressions ("it is raining <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork" title="Pitchfork">pitchforks</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a> handles", etc.).</p>

<li>Bosnian: <i>padaju ćuskije</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Crowbar_(tool)" title="Crowbar (tool)">crowbars</a>")</li>
