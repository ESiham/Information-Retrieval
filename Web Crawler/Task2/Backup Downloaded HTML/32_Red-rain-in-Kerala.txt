<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Red rain in Kerala</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Red_rain_(disambiguation)" title="Red rain (disambiguation)">Red Rain</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WaterSample.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="176" data-file-width="310" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/WaterSample.jpg/220px-WaterSample.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/WaterSample.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WaterSample.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rain water sample (left) and after the particles settled (right). Dried sediment (centre).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Kerala red rain phenomenon</b> was a <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_rain" title="Blood rain">blood rain</a></i> event that occurred from 25 July to 23 September 2001, when heavy downpours of red-coloured rain fell sporadically on the southern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, staining clothes pink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gentleman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gentleman-1">[1]</a></sup> Yellow, green, and black rain was also reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hinduonet_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hinduonet-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup> Coloured rain was also reported in Kerala in 1896 and several times since,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> most recently in June 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epoch_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epoch-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kannur_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kannur-7">[7]</a></sup> and from 15 November 2012 to 27 December 2012 in eastern and north-central provinces of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a light microscopy examination in 2001, it was initially thought that the rains were coloured by fallout from a hypothetical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteor</a> <a href="/wiki/Air_burst" title="Air burst">burst</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> but a study commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a> concluded that the rains had been coloured by airborne <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a> from a locally prolific terrestrial green alga from the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> An international team later identified the exact species as <i>T. annulata</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-researchgate.net_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-researchgate.net-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehindu.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu.com-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="251" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png/120px-Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png/180px-Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png/240px-Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_Kottayam_Kerala.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Kottayam_district" title="Kottayam district">Kottayam district</a> in Kerala, which experienced the most red rainfall</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The coloured rain of Kerala began falling on 25 July 2001, in the districts of Kottayam and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idukki" title="Idukki">Idukki</a> in the southern part of the state. Yellow, green, and black rain was also reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hinduonet_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-hinduonet-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup> Many more occurrences of the red rain were reported over the following ten days, and then with diminishing frequency until late September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup> According to locals, the first coloured rain was preceded by a loud <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunderclap</a> and flash of light, and followed by groves of trees shedding shrivelled grey "burnt" leaves. Shriveled leaves and the disappearance and sudden formation of <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a> were also reported around the same time in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiatoday_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-indiatoday-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesofindia_20010806_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesofindia_20010806-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesofindia_20010805_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesofindia_20010805-16">[16]</a></sup> It typically fell over small areas, no more than a few square kilometres in size, and was sometimes so localised that normal rain could be falling just a few meters away from the red rain. Red rainfalls typically lasted less than 20 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup> Each millilitre of rain water contained about 9 million red particles. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrapolating" title="Extrapolating">Extrapolating</a> these figures to the total amount of red rain estimated to have fallen, it was estimated that 50,000 kilograms (110,000 lb) of red particles had fallen on Kerala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_the_particles">Description of the particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description of the particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brownish-red solid separated from the red rain consisted of about 90% round red particles and the balance consisted of debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> The particles in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> in the rain water were responsible for the colour of the rain, which at times was strongly coloured red. A small percentage of particles were white or had light yellow, bluish grey and green tints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup> The particles were typically 4 to 10 <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">µm</a> across and spherical or oval. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscope" title="Transmission electron microscope">Electron microscope</a> images showed the particles as having a depressed centre. At still higher magnification some particles showed internal structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some water samples were taken to the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Earth_Science_Studies" title="Centre for Earth Science Studies">Centre for Earth Science Studies</a> (CESS) in India, where they separated the suspended particles by filtration. The <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of the water was found to be around 7 (neutral). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> of the rainwater showed the absence of any dissolved <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>. Sediment (red particles plus debris) was collected and analysed by the CESS using a combination of ion-coupled plasma <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a>, atomic absorption spectrometry and wet chemical methods. The major elements found are listed below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> The CESS analysis also showed significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, including nickel (43 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>), <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> (59 ppm), <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> (321 ppm), <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> (67ppm) and copper (55 ppm).</p>
<p>Physicists <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Louis" title="Godfrey Louis">Godfrey Louis</a> and Santhosh Kumar of the <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi_University" title="Mahatma Gandhi University">Mahatma Gandhi University</a>, Kerala, used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_dispersive_X-ray_spectroscopy" title="Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy">energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy</a> analysis of the red solid and showed that the particles were composed of mostly carbon and oxygen, with trace amounts of silicon and iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup> A <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CHN_analyser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CHN analyser (page does not exist)">CHN analyser</a> showed content of 43.03% carbon, 4.43% hydrogen, and 1.84% nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>J. Thomas Brenna in the Division of Nutritional Sciences at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> conducted carbon and nitrogen isotope analyses using a scanning electron microscope with X-ray micro-analysis, an elemental analyser, and an isotope ratio (IR) mass spectrometer. The red particles collapsed when dried, which suggested that they were filled with fluid. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> in the particles were analysed and seven were identified (in order of concentration): <a href="/wiki/Phenylalanine" title="Phenylalanine">phenylalanine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a>/<a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serine" title="Serine">serine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aspartic_acid" title="Aspartic acid">aspartic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a>. The results were consistent with a marine origin or a terrestrial plant that uses a <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4</a> photosynthetic pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isotope_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-isotope-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_report">Government report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_rain_Kerala_TEM.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="284" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Red_rain_Kerala_TEM.png/220px-Red_rain_Kerala_TEM.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/64/Red_rain_Kerala_TEM.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
</div>
<p>Initially, the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Earth_Science_Studies" title="Centre for Earth Science Studies">Centre for Earth Science Studies</a> (CESS) stated that the likely cause of the red rain was an exploding meteor, which had dispersed about 1,000 kg (one ton) of material. A few days later, following a basic <a href="/wiki/Optical_microscope" title="Optical microscope">light microscopy</a> evaluation, the CESS retracted this as they noticed the particles resembled spores,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rense_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rense-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fungus_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-fungus-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and because debris from a meteor would not have continued to fall from the stratosphere onto the same area while unaffected by wind. A sample was, therefore, handed over to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Botanical_Garden_and_Research_Institute" title="Tropical Botanical Garden and Research Institute">Tropical Botanical Garden and Research Institute</a> (TBGRI) for <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiological</a> studies, where the spores were allowed to grow in a medium suitable for growth of algae and fungi. The inoculated Petri dishes and conical flasks were incubated for three to seven days and the cultures were observed under a microscope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2069" data-file-width="1480" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG/220px-Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG/330px-Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG/440px-Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trentepohlia_sp._on_Cryptomeria_japonica_bark.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia</a></i> on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptomeria_japonica" title="Cryptomeria japonica">Cryptomeria japonica</a></i> bark</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In November 2001, commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a>'s Department of Science &amp; Technology, the Centre for Earth Science Studies (CESS) and the Tropical Botanical Garden and Research Institute (TBGRI) issued a joint report, which concluded:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fungus_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-fungus-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The colour was found to be due to the presence of a large amount of spores of a <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>-forming <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">alga</a> belonging to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia</a></i>. Field verification showed that the region had plenty of such lichens. Samples of lichen taken from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changanacherry" title="Changanacherry">Changanacherry</a> area, when cultured in an algal growth medium, also showed the presence of the same species of algae. Both samples (from rainwater and from trees) produced the same kind of algae, indicating that the spores seen in the rainwater most probably came from local sources</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The site was again visited on 16 August 2001 and it was found that almost all the trees, rocks and even lamp posts in the region were covered with <i>Trentepohlia</i> estimated to be in sufficient amounts to generate the quantity of spores seen in the rainwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> Although red or orange, <i>Trentepohlia</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyta" title="Chlorophyta">chlorophyte</a> green alga which can grow abundantly on tree bark or damp soil and rocks, but is also the photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiont</a> or photobiont of many lichens, including some of those abundant on the trees in Changanassery area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> The strong orange colour of the algae, which masks the green of the <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>, is caused by the presence of large quantities of orange <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> pigments. A <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> is not a single organism, but the result of a partnership (<a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a>) between a fungus and an alga or cyanobacterium.</p>
<p>The report also stated that there was no meteoric, volcanic or desert dust origin present in the rainwater and that its colour was not due to any dissolved gases or pollutants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> The report concluded that heavy rains in Kerala – in the weeks preceding the red rains – could have caused the widespread growth of lichens, which had given rise to a large quantity of spores into the atmosphere. However, for these lichen to release their spores simultaneously, it is necessary for them to enter their reproductive phase at about the same time. The CESS report noted that while this may be a possibility, it is quite improbable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup> Also, they could find no satisfactory explanation for the apparently extraordinary dispersal, nor for the apparent uptake of the spores into clouds. CESS scientists noted that "While the cause of the colour in the rainfall has been identified, finding the answers to these questions is a challenge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fungus_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-fungus-19">[19]</a></sup> Attempting to explain the unusual spore proliferation and dispersal, researcher Ian Goddard proposed several local atmospheric models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parts of the CESS/TBGRI report were supported by <a href="/wiki/Milton_Wainwright" title="Milton Wainwright">Dr. Milton Wainwright</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheffield_University" title="Sheffield University">Sheffield University</a>, who, together with <a href="/wiki/Chandra_Wickramasinghe" title="Chandra Wickramasinghe">Chandra Wickramasinghe</a>, has studied <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gentleman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gentleman-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmcnet_2006_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-tmcnet_2006-23">[23]</a></sup> In March 2006 Wainwright said the particles were similar in appearance to spores of a <a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">rust fungus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yorkshire_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-yorkshire-24">[24]</a></sup> later saying that he had confirmed the presence of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Darling.27s_Newsletter_.2339_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_Darling.27s_Newsletter_.2339-25">[25]</a></sup> and reported their similarity to algal spores, and found no evidence to suggest that the rain contained dust, sand, fat globules, or blood. In November 2012, Dr. Rajkumar Gangappa and Dr. Stuart Hogg from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glamorgan" title="University of Glamorgan">University of Glamorgan</a>, UK, confirmed that the red rain cells from Kerala contain DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rajkumar_Gangappa_and_Stuart_Hogg_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rajkumar_Gangappa_and_Stuart_Hogg-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2015, a team of scientists from India and Austria, also supported the identification of the algal spores as <i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia annulata</a></i>, however, they speculate that the spores from the 2011 incident were carried by winds from Europe to the Indian subcontinent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-researchgate.net_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-researchgate.net-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_alga_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-European_alga-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phylogenetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study published in February 2015 established that the exact species of green microalgae is <i><a href="/wiki/Trentepohlia_(alga)" title="Trentepohlia (alga)">Trentepohlia annulata</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-researchgate.net_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-researchgate.net-12">[12]</a></sup> The study used <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">molecular phylogenetics</a> to compare the evolution of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> sequences of <i>T. annulata</i> isolated from colored rain samples with that of <i>T. annulata</i> from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. The results suggest that the isolate from Kerala is a recently introduced species from Austria. The researchers suggest that the introduction happened through clouds over the ocean – a phenomenon of intercontinental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_dispersal" title="Species dispersal">species dispersal</a> previously reported for bacteria and fungi, but first time for the alga. How exactly these lower stratospheric clouds containing algal spores got to Kerala remains unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehindu.com_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu.com-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_hypotheses">Alternative hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alternative hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>History records many instances of unusual objects falling with the rain – in 2000, in an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raining_animals" title="Raining animals">raining animals</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> sucked up a school of fish a mile off shore, depositing them shortly afterwards on <a href="/wiki/Great_Yarmouth" title="Great Yarmouth">Great Yarmouth</a> in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Coloured rain is by no means rare, and can often be explained by the airborne transport of <a href="/wiki/Rain_dust" title="Rain dust">rain dust</a> from <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> or other dry regions which is washed down by rain. "Red Rains" have been frequently described in southern Europe, with increasing reports in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B.C3.BCcher_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-B.C3.BCcher-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> One such case occurred in England in 1903, when dust was carried from the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and fell with rain in February of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-english_rain_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-english_rain-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>At first, the red rain in Kerala was attributed to the same effect, with dust from the deserts of <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> initially the suspect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiatoday_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-indiatoday-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIDAR" title="LIDAR">LIDAR</a> observations had detected a cloud of dust in the atmosphere near Kerala in the days preceding the outbreak of the red rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Satyanarayana_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Satyanarayana-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, laboratory tests from all involved teams ruled out the particles were desert sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Satyanarayana_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Satyanarayana-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>K.K. Sasidharan Pillai, a senior scientific assistant in the Indian Meteorological Department, proposed dust and acidic material from an eruption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayon_Volcano" title="Mayon Volcano">Mayon Volcano</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> as an explanation for the coloured rain and the "burnt" leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The volcano was erupting in June and July 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and Pillai calculated that the Eastern or Equatorial <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> could have transported volcanic material to Kerala in 25–36 hours. The Equatorial jet stream is unusual in that it sometimes flows from east to west at about 10° N,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> approximately the same latitude as Kerala (8° N) and Mayon Volcano (13° N). This hypothesis was also ruled out as the particles were neither acidic nor of volcanic origin, but were spores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESS_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-CESS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study has been published showing a correlation between historic reports of coloured rains and of meteors;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmcaf_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmcaf-37">[37]</a></sup> the author of the paper, Patrick McCafferty, stated that sixty of these events (coloured rain), or 36%, were linked to meteoritic or cometary activity. But not always strongly. Sometimes the fall of red rain seems to have occurred after an air-burst, as from a meteor exploding in air; other times the odd rainfall is merely recorded in the same year as the appearance of a comet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Panspermia_hypothesis">Panspermia hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Panspermia hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003 <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Louis" title="Godfrey Louis">Godfrey Louis</a> and Santhosh Kumar, physicists at the <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi_University" title="Mahatma Gandhi University">Mahatma Gandhi University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kottayam" title="Kottayam">Kottayam</a>, Kerala, posted an article entitled "Cometary <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a> explains the red rain of Kerala"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K2003a_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K2003a-39">[39]</a></sup> in the non-<a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a> web site. While the CESS report said there was no apparent relationship between the loud sound (possibly a <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a>) and flash of light which preceded the red rain, to Louis and Kumar it was a key piece of evidence. They proposed that a meteor (from a comet containing the red particles) caused the sound and flash and when it disintegrated over Kerala it released the red particles which slowly fell to the ground. However, they omitted an explanation on how debris from a meteor continued to fall in the same area over a period of two months while unaffected by winds.</p>
<p>Their work indicated that the particles were of biological origin (consistent with the CESS report), however, they invoked the <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a> <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> to explain the presence of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> in a supposed fall of meteoric material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chandra_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-chandra-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Additionally, using <a href="/wiki/Ethidium_bromide" title="Ethidium bromide">ethidium bromide</a> they were unable to detect <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> or <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> in the particles. Two months later they posted another paper on the same web site entitled "New biology of red rain extremophiles prove cometary panspermia"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K2003b_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K2003b-43">[43]</a></sup> in which they reported that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The microorganism isolated from the red rain of Kerala shows very extraordinary characteristics, like the ability to grow optimally at 300 °C (572 °F) and the capacity to metabolise a wide range of organic and inorganic materials.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>These claims and data have yet to be verified and reported in any peer reviewed publication. In 2006 Louis and Kumar published a paper in <i><a href="/wiki/Astrophysics_and_Space_Science" title="Astrophysics and Space Science">Astrophysics and Space Science</a></i> entitled "The red rain phenomenon of Kerala and its possible extraterrestrial origin"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup> which reiterated their arguments that the red rain was biological matter from an extraterrestrial source but made no mention of their previous claims to having induced the cells to grow. The team also observed the cells using <a href="/wiki/Microscopy#Phase_contrast" title="Microscopy">phase contrast</a> fluorescence microscopy, and they concluded that: "The fluorescence behaviour of the red cells is shown to be in remarkable correspondence with the extended red emission observed in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Rectangle_Nebula" title="Red Rectangle Nebula">Red Rectangle Nebula</a> and other galactic and extragalactic <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust clouds</a>, suggesting, though not proving an extraterrestrial origin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121oC_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-121oC-44">[44]</a></sup> One of their conclusions was that if the red rain particles are biological cells and are of cometary origin, then this phenomenon can be a case of cometary <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K_2006_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K_2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2008 Louis and Kumar again presented their case in an astrobiology conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The abstract for their paper states that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The red cells found in the red rain in Kerala, India are now considered as a possible case of extraterrestrial life form. These cells can undergo rapid replication even at an extreme high temperature of 300 °C. They can also be cultured in diverse unconventional chemical substrates. The molecular composition of these cells is yet to be identified.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In September 2010 a similar paper was presented at a conference in California, USA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_ancestry">Cosmic ancestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cosmic ancestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ancestry" title="Cosmic ancestry">Cosmic ancestry</a></div>
<p>Researcher <a href="/wiki/Chandra_Wickramasinghe" title="Chandra Wickramasinghe">Chandra Wickramasinghe</a> used Louis and Kumar's "extraterrestrial origin" claim to further support his <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a> hypothesis called cosmic ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> This hypothesis postulates that life is neither the product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligent_Design" title="Intelligent Design">supernatural creation</a>, nor is it spontaneously generated through <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>, but that it has always existed in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. Cosmic ancestry speculates that higher life forms, including intelligent life, descend ultimately from pre-existing life which was at least as advanced as the descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_rain_in_Kerala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Louis and Kumar made their first publication of their finding on a web site in 2003, and have presented papers at conferences and in astrophysics magazines a number of times since. The controversial conclusion of Louis et al. is the only hypothesis suggesting that these organisms are of extraterrestrial origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Such reports have been popular in the media, with major news agencies like CNN repeating the panspermia theory without critique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hypothesis' authors – G. Louis and Kumar – did not explain how debris from a meteor could have continued to fall on the same area over a period of two months, despite the changes in climatic conditions and wind pattern spanning over two months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K2003a_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K2003a-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Samples of the red particles were also sent for analysis to his collaborators <a href="/wiki/Milton_Wainwright" title="Milton Wainwright">Milton Wainwright</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheffield_University" title="Sheffield University">Sheffield University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chandra_Wickramasinghe" title="Chandra Wickramasinghe">Chandra Wickramasinghe</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_University" title="Cardiff University">Cardiff University</a>. Louis then incorrectly reported on 29 August 2010 in the non-peer reviewed online physics archive "arxiv.org" that they were able to have these cells "reproduce" when incubated at high pressure saturated steam at 121 °C (<a href="/wiki/Autoclave" title="Autoclave">autoclaved</a>) for up to two hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121oC_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-121oC-44">[44]</a></sup> Their conclusion is that these cells reproduced, without DNA, at temperatures higher than any known life form on earth is able to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121oC_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-121oC-44">[44]</a></sup> They claimed that the cells, however, were unable to reproduce at temperatures similar to known organisms.</p>
<p>Regarding the "absence" of DNA, Louis admits he has no training in biology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and has not reported the use of any standard microbiology <a href="/wiki/Growth_medium" title="Growth medium">growth medium</a> to culture and induce <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a> and growth of the spores, basing his claim of "biological growth" on light absorption measurements following aggregation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluids#Nano_and_micro_particle_formation" title="Supercritical fluids">supercritical fluids</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K2003b_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K2003b-43">[43]</a></sup> an inert physical observation. Both his collaborators, Wickramasinghe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astro_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-astro-57">[57]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Milton_Wainwright" title="Milton Wainwright">Milton Wainwright</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Darling.27s_Newsletter_.2339_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-David_Darling.27s_Newsletter_.2339-25">[25]</a></sup> independently extracted and confirmed the presence of DNA from the spores. The absence of DNA was key to Louis and Kumar's hypothesis that the cells were of extraterrestrial origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.26K2003a_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-L.26K2003a-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis' only reported attempt to stain the spores' DNA was by the use of <a href="/wiki/Malachite_green" title="Malachite green">malachite green</a>, which is generally used to stain bacterial endospores, not algal spores,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> whose primary function of their cell wall and their impermeability is to ensure its own survival through periods of environmental stress. They are therefore resistant to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desiccation" title="Desiccation">desiccation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysozyme" title="Lysozyme">lysozyme</a>, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> and chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfectants" title="Disinfectants">disinfectants</a>. Visualizing algal spore DNA under a light microscope can be difficult due to the impermeability of the highly resistant spore wall to dyes and stains used in normal staining procedures. The spores' DNA is tightly packed, encapsulated and desiccated, therefore, the spores must first be cultured in suitable <a href="/wiki/Growth_medium" title="Growth medium">growth medium</a> and temperature to first induce <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Lichen#Growth_form" title="Lichen">cell growth</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Lichen#Reproduction_and_dispersal" title="Lichen">reproduction</a> before staining the DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other researchers have noted recurring instances of red rainfalls in 1818, 1846, 1872, 1880, 1896, and 1950, including one described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup> and several times since then. Most recently, coloured rainfall occurred over Kerala during the summers of 2001, 2006, 2007, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and 2012; since 2001, the botanists have found the same <i>Trentepohlia</i> spores every time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup> This supports the notion that the red rain is a seasonal local environmental feature caused by <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algal</a> spores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_54-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>The red rain phenomenon in Kerala has been studied from a social scientific perspective too. The book, <a class="external text" href="http://www.oxfordscholarship.com/view/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199469123.001.0001/acprof-9780199469123" rel="nofollow"><i>Contested Knowledge: Science, Media, and Democracy in Kerala</i></a>, argues that 'Public deliberation about the scientific causes of the coloured rain ...revealed the interpretative latitude surrounding scientific explanations and the strategies that were formulated by diverse actors to challenge dominant scientific claims. This has continuously forced the established boundaries between disciplines, subcultures, and networks of science open for negotiation. The [red rain] controversy witnessed the emergence of a small group of researchers [Godfrey Louis and Santhosh Kumar] skilfully highlighting their quasi-scientific theory in public and forming coalitions with researchers in Europe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
