<p><b>Rainmaking</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Weather_modification" title="Weather modification">weather modification</a> <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> that attempts to invoke <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>.</p>
<p>Among the best known examples of weather modification rituals are North American <b>rain dances</b>, historically performed by many <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> tribes, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a>. Some of these weather modification rituals are still implemented today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Julia M. Buttree (the wife of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Thompson_Seton" title="Ernest Thompson Seton">Ernest Thompson Seton</a>) describes the rain dance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuni_people" title="Zuni people">Zuni</a>, along with other Native American dances, in her book <i>The Rhythm of the Redman</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Feathers and <a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a>, or other blue items, are worn during the ceremony to symbolize wind and rain respectively. Details on how best to perform the Rain Dance have been passed down by oral tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In an early sort of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, Native Americans in the midwestern parts of the modern United States often tracked and followed known weather patterns while offering to perform a rain dance for settlers in return for trade items. This is best documented among the <a href="/wiki/Osage_Nation" title="Osage Nation">Osage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quapaw" title="Quapaw">Quapaw</a> Indian tribes of Missouri and Arkansas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rain is a central concern of African societies which depend on it for their sustenance and that of their animals. The power to make rain is a usual attribute of African kings. In a number of African societies kings who failed to produce the expected rain ran the risk of being blamed as a scapegoat and killed by their people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tribal rain dances are done to ensure rain comes. Notable people known to have done rain dances are tribes on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wu_(shaman)#Wu-shamans_as_rainmakers" title="Wu (shaman)">Wu Shamans</a> in ancient China performed sacrificial rain dance ceremonies in times of drought. Wu anciently served as intermediaries with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_spirits" title="Nature spirits">nature spirits</a> believed to control rainfall and flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> "Shamans had to carry out an exhausting dance within a ring of fire until, sweating profusely, the falling drops of perspirations produced the desired rain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rain is important for agriculturists, since they want water for growing plants. When the rain doesn’t fall in a season, people use traditional means of making rain to solve the problem of the lack of rainfall. In Thailand, there is a tradition called the “Cat Parade”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The “Cat Parade” is a ritual of Thai farmers in both the central and eastern parts of the country. It is performed when close to the rainy season, if the rain hasn’t come. Farmers will often join the "Cat Parade". The ancients believed that the cat is an animal which is afraid of rain and water. If it rained, the cat would cry. The ancients considered that if the cat cries it means that rain is going to fall. Some people believe that the cat represents drought. If the cat is wet, the drought will be driven away. Others believed that the cat has the power to make rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>To perform a “Cat Parade”, you must bring a female cat that has grey or black fur. The cats are then to be placed in baskets; only one cat should be put in a basket. You must then walk around in the village. When the “Cat Parade” goes through someone's house, that person needs to splash water on the cats. It is believed that rain would then fall after three to seven days.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Caloian" title="Caloian">Caloian</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dodola" title="Dodola">Dodola</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perperuna" title="Perperuna">Perperuna</a></i> / <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paparuda" title="Paparuda">Paparuda</a></i>, among other terms, refer to a family of <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a> rainmaking rituals, some of which survived into the 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Popular_Culture">In Popular Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainmaking_(ritual)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In Popular Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the TV show, <a href="/wiki/Impractical_Jokers" title="Impractical Jokers">Impractical Jokers</a>, the Jokers, while in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, had to give presentations on Hawaiian culture. Two of the guys, Sal and Q had to do an Hawaiian Rain Dance where they were forced to incorporate middle fingers into the dance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
