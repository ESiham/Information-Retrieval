<p>The fictitious mathematician <b>John Rainwater</b> was created as a student prank but has become known as the author of important results in <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a> in 1952, John Rainwater was invented and enrolled in a mathematics course by graduate students who were in possession of a duplicate student-registration form. Later, mathematicians published under the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> of John Rainwater.</p>
<p>Papers were published under the name Rainwater mainly in <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>, particularly in the geometric theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banach_spaces" title="Banach spaces">Banach spaces</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Convex_function" title="Convex function">convex functions</a>. Rainwater's theorem is an important result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summability_theory" title="Summability theory">summability theory</a> and functional analysis. The University of Washington's seminar in functional analysis is called the Rainwater seminar, and the associated Rainwater notes have influenced <a href="/wiki/Banach_space" title="Banach space">Banach-space theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convex_analysis" title="Convex analysis">convex analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhelpsRainwater_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-PhelpsRainwater-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of a fictional pseudonym used by multiple people creating valuable mathematics is not unique. Most notably, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki" title="Nicolas Bourbaki">Nicolas Bourbaki</a> has been the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_pseudonym" title="Collective pseudonym">collective pseudonym</a> for a number of leading mathematicians writing in French for many decades.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Rainwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>John Rainwater was invented by graduate students at the University of Washington in 1952, when students used an extra registration form to enroll Rainwater in a course on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_function" title="Real function">real functions</a>. Students submitted homework for Rainwater throughout the semester. The professor caught on to the prank around the middle of the term. Other students in the class were made aware of the situation from the professor's enigmatic remarks after he became the victim of a novelty "exploding" <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain pen</a> bearing Rainwater's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Rainwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early on, Rainwater distinguished himself by solving problems in the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Monthly" title="American Mathematical Monthly">American Mathematical Monthly</a></i>, whose sponsoring society, the Mathematical Association of America, invited him to join. <a href="/wiki/John_R._Isbell" title="John R. Isbell">John R. Isbell</a> published the first paper in Rainwater's name. Other mathematicians have published papers using the name "Rainwater", and acknowledged "Rainwater's assistance" in articles. The seminar on <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a> at the University of Washington has been called the "Rainwater seminar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhelpsRainwater_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-PhelpsRainwater-1">[1]</a></sup> Rainwater's theorem is an important result in summability theory and <a href="/wiki/Banach_space" title="Banach space">Banach-space</a> theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Rainwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002 Robert Phelps summarized the impact of Rainwater's research. The first Rainwater paper (by Isbell) was in topology and had had 19 citations. While only one page, Rainwater's note in the 1963 <i><a href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_American_Mathematical_Society" title="Proceedings of the American Mathematical Society">Proceedings of the American Mathematical Society</a></i> had had eight citations in papers; its main result has been called "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rainwater's theorem (page does not exist)">Rainwater's theorem</a>" in books on <a href="/wiki/Convex_function" title="Convex function">convex functions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>. "There is even one citation to number 13, his unpublished 1967 <i>Rainwater Seminar</i> note on Lindenstrauss spaces," which are named after a construction by <a href="/wiki/Joram_Lindenstrauss" title="Joram Lindenstrauss">Joram Lindenstrauss</a>. "In summary, it appears that most of John Rainwater's published work has been reasonably well received."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhelpsRainwater_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-PhelpsRainwater-1">[1]</a></sup> While Rainwater is lesser known and younger than Nicolas Bourbaki, the collective pseudonym for a number of leading mathematicians writing in French, he is more senior and has more publications than the combination of research by three other pseudonymous mathematicians—<a href="/wiki/Peter_Orno" title="Peter Orno">Peter Orno</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._G._Stanley" title="M. G. Stanley">M. G. Stanley</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._C._Enos" title="H. C. Enos">H. C. Enos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematicians_publishing_as_Rainwater">Mathematicians publishing as Rainwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Rainwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mathematicians publishing as Rainwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many internationally renowned mathematicians have published under the name of John Rainwater. John Isbell wrote Rainwater's first, second, and tenth papers; by 2002, Isbell had also written or coauthored six other pseudonymous papers under two other names. Functional analyst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_R._Phelps" title="Robert R. Phelps">Robert R. Phelps</a> wrote the third, ninth, eleventh (an unpublished note for the Rainwater seminar), twelfth, and thirteenth (with Peter D. Morris), fifteenth (with <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Namioka" title="Isaac Namioka">Isaac Namioka</a>), and sixteenth (with <a href="/wiki/David_Preiss" title="David Preiss">David Preiss</a>) papers. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Glicksberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Irving Glicksberg (page does not exist)">Irving Glicksberg</a> wrote the fourth and eighth papers. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edgar_Asplund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edgar Asplund (page does not exist)">Edgar Asplund</a> wrote the seventh. "Paper 14 is a departure for John Rainwater. Not only is it in algebra, but he doesn't thank anyone for helpful conversations. He notes, however, that his work was supported by four different grants. (Culprits this time were Ken Brown, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Goodearl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ken Goodearl (page does not exist)">Ken Goodearl</a>, Toby Stafford and Bob Warfield.)" John Rainwater's c.v. lists an incomplete collection of problems or solutions that he contributed to the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Monthly" title="American Mathematical Monthly">American Mathematical Monthly</a></i>, the earliest in 1959 (by John Isbell).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhelpsRainwater_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-PhelpsRainwater-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p><span class="reference-text">John Rainwater came into existence at the University of Washington in 1952 when Nick Massey, a mathematics graduate student in Prof. Maynard Arsove's beginning real variables class, erroneously received a blank registration card. (In those years, each student filled out a card for every class, which first circulated among various tabulating clerks in the registrar's office before being sent to the professor.) He and a fellow graduate student, Sam Saunders, decided to use the card to enroll a fictional student, and since it was raining at the time, decided to call him "John Rainwater". They handed in John Rainwater's homework regularly, so it wasn't until after the first midterm exam that Prof. Arsove became aware of the deception. He took it well, even when he later opened an "exploding" fountain pen with John Rainwater's name engraved on it which had been left on the classroom table. After remarks by Arsove, such as "I guess I'll never see Rainwater except in a barrel," virtually all the students learned of the Rainwater prank. (<a href="#CITEREFPhelps2002">Phelps 2002</a>)</span></p>
