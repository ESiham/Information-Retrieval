<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">United States tropical cyclone rainfall climatology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <b>United States tropical cyclone rainfall climatology</b> concerns the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, primarily in the form of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, which occurs during <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> remnants across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Typically, five tropical cyclones and their remnants impact the country each year, contributing between a tenth and a quarter of the annual rainfall across the southern tier of the country. The highest rainfall amounts appear close to the coast, with lesser amounts falling farther inland. Obstructions to the precipitation pattern, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachian_mountains" title="Appalachian mountains">Appalachian mountains</a>, focus higher amounts from northern Georgia through New England. While most impacts occur with systems moving in from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic ocean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, some emanate from the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_ocean" title="Pacific ocean">Pacific ocean</a>, with a few crossing <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> before impacting the Southwest. Those making landfall within the Southeast portion of the country tend to have the greatest potential for heavy rains.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_averages">Long-term averages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Long-term averages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On average, five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricanes</a> or their remnants lead to rainfall across the contiguous United States each year, contributing between a tenth and a quarter of the annual rainfall to the southern United States. While many of these storms form in the Atlantic Basin, some systems or their remnants move through Mexico from the Eastern Pacific Basin. Tropical cyclones from the eastern Pacific bring nearly 20 percent of the average annual rainfall to southern California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The average storm total rainfall for a tropical cyclone impacting the lower 48 from the Atlantic Basin is about 16 inches/406 mm, with 70-75 percent of the storm total falling within a 24‑hour period.</p>

<p>State maxima relating to tropical cyclones and their remnants are shown on the left, color-coded by amount. Tropical cyclones from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> Basin have the most sway along the Gulf coast and <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Eastern Seaboard</a>. The impact of tropical cyclones and their remnants originally from the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> stretches as far east as <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>. Rainfall related to the low pressure area once associated with a tropical cyclone, or its remnants aloft, are included in this sample. No additional rainfall from pre-existing upper lows as seen before cyclones such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fran" title="Hurricane Fran">Hurricane Fran</a> of 1996 or from upper cyclones that closed off behind former <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juan" title="Hurricane Juan">Hurricane Juan</a> of 1985 was included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statemax_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-statemax-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> has the highest amounts, followed by <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. Out West, the same can be said for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kathleen_(1976)" title="Hurricane Kathleen (1976)">Hurricane Kathleen (1976)</a> and the remains of Hurricane Olivia of the <a href="/wiki/1982_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1982 Pacific hurricane season">1982 Pacific hurricane season</a>. Some records across the Midwest occurred during <a href="/wiki/1968_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Candy" title="1968 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Candy of 1968</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statemax_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-statemax-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Average_and_record_statistics_per_time_frame_for_the_lower_48">Average and record statistics per time frame for the lower 48</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Average and record statistics per time frame for the lower 48">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To the right is a graphic showing averages and extremes for a 15-year sample of tropical cyclones and their remnants affecting the contiguous United States. The units of the rainfall amounts are in inches, while the time units are in hours. The bars in the graph express the percent of the storm total rainfall, which is defined to be 100 percent in the final column. Note that, on average, as much as one-fourth of the total occurs in 2–3 hours, while half falls within 12 hours, and almost three-quarters of the storm total falls within a 24‑hour period. Cases where a cyclone scraped the coast were not separated out from those that made a more direct landfall. Also, Pacific and Atlantic cases were not separated. This all explains the average storm total of the sample being depressed to 338.8 mm/13.34". On the bottom of the graphic are listed the averages per time frame and the records. The records were mainly set during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Allison_(2001)" title="Tropical Storm Allison (2001)">Tropical Storm Allison (2001)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Danny_(1997)" title="Hurricane Danny (1997)">Hurricane Danny (1997)</a>. This graphic will be updated as the climatology pushes farther back in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROTHUSTCRAIN_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ROTHUSTCRAIN-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_rules_of_thumb_for_forecasting">United States rules of thumb for forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States rules of thumb for forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting">Tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kraft_rule">Kraft rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kraft rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the late 1950s, this <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_thumb" title="Rule of thumb">rule of thumb</a> came into being, developed by R. H. Kraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was noted from rainfall amounts (in imperial units) reported by the first order rainfall network in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> that the storm total rainfall fit a simple equation: 100 divided by the speed of motion in knots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WES_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WES-6">[6]</a></sup> This rule works as long as a tropical cyclone is moving and only the first order or synoptic station network (with observations spaced about 60 miles/100 km apart) are used to derive storm totals. Canada uses a modified version of the Kraft rule which divides the results by a factor of two, which takes into account the lower sea surface temperatures seen around Atlantic Canada and the prevalence of systems undergoing vertical wind shear at their northerly latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The main problem with this rule is that the rainfall observing network is denser than either the synoptic reporting network or the first order station networks, which means the absolute maximum is likely to be underestimated. Another problem is that it does not take the size of the tropical cyclone or topography into account.</p>
<h3><span id="Eight inch_or_203_mm rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Eight.C2.A0inch_or_203_mm.C2.A0rule">Eight inch or 203 mm rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eight inch or 203 mm rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rusty Pfost, now the head of the Miami <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> Forecast Office, did a study in 1999 reviewing rainfall totals from tropical systems affecting Florida between 1960 and 1998. He found that for tropical cyclones moving at greater than 6 knots, the average storm total was normally in the 5–10 inch (127–254 mm) range. Slower moving storms usually forced greater than 15 inches (381 mm) of rain to fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WES_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-WES-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Sixteen_inch_or_406_mm rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sixteen_inch_or_406_mm.C2.A0rule">Sixteen inch or 406 mm rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sixteen inch or 406 mm rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg/200px-ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg/300px-ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg/400px-ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ShortTermRainfallAccumulations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone" title="Tropical Cyclone">Tropical Cyclone</a> Rainfall Accumulations per time frame</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>David Roth, a forecaster at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrometeorological_Prediction_Center" title="Hydrometeorological Prediction Center">Hydrometeorological Prediction Center</a>, determined that the average amount for all tropical cyclones impacting the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> was 13.34 inches (339 mm) between 1991 and 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> When removing the storms that grazed the domain, an average of near 16 inches/406 mm was obtained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Using this latter amount appears to work best for systems that experience little vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and are of at least average size. Amounts measured in small/midget tropical cyclones showed storm total amounts closer to 6 inches/152 mm. Operationally, variations to these amounts are introduced if the cyclone encounters mountain zones, interacts with a nearby front, or the storm is significantly sheared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WES_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-WES-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
