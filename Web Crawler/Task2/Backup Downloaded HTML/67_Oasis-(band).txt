<p><b>Oasis</b> were an English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band formed in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> in 1991. Developed from an earlier group, the Rain, the band originally consisted of <a href="/wiki/Liam_Gallagher" title="Liam Gallagher">Liam Gallagher</a> (vocals and tambourine), <a href="/wiki/Paul_Arthurs" title="Paul Arthurs">Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs</a> (guitar), <a href="/wiki/Paul_McGuigan_(musician)" title="Paul McGuigan (musician)">Paul "Guigsy" McGuigan</a> (bass guitar), and <a href="/wiki/Tony_McCarroll" title="Tony McCarroll">Tony McCarroll</a> (drums, percussion). They were later joined by Liam's older brother <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher" title="Noel Gallagher">Noel Gallagher</a> (lead guitar and vocals) as a fifth member, becoming the band's settled line-up until April 1995.</p>
<p>Oasis signed to <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent record label</a> <a href="/wiki/Creation_Records" title="Creation Records">Creation Records</a> in 1993 and released their record-setting debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Definitely_Maybe" title="Definitely Maybe">Definitely Maybe</a></i> (1994). The following year the band recorded <i><a href="/wiki/(What%27s_the_Story)_Morning_Glory%3F" title="(What's the Story) Morning Glory?">(What's the Story) Morning Glory?</a></i> (1995) with drummer <a href="/wiki/Alan_White_(Oasis_drummer)" title="Alan White (Oasis drummer)">Alan White</a>, in the midst of a chart rivalry with <a href="/wiki/Britpop" title="Britpop">Britpop</a> peers <a href="/wiki/Blur_(band)" title="Blur (band)">Blur</a>. Along with <a href="/wiki/Suede_(band)" title="Suede (band)">Suede</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(band)" title="Pulp (band)">Pulp</a>, Blur and others, Oasis came to be regarded as one of the major bands of the Britpop movement and the Gallagher brothers were featured regularly in <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid newspapers</a> for their sibling disputes and wild lifestyles. In 1997 Oasis released their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Be_Here_Now_(album)" title="Be Here Now (album)">Be Here Now</a></i> (1997), and although it was the fastest-selling album in UK chart history, the album's popularity tapered off quickly, but not before selling 8 million copies. McGuigan and Arthurs left Oasis in 1999 as the band released <i><a href="/wiki/Standing_on_the_Shoulder_of_Giants" title="Standing on the Shoulder of Giants">Standing on the Shoulder of Giants</a></i> (2000). After their departures, they were replaced by former <a href="/wiki/Heavy_Stereo" title="Heavy Stereo">Heavy Stereo</a> guitarist/frontman <a href="/wiki/Gem_Archer" title="Gem Archer">Gem Archer</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Ride_(band)" title="Ride (band)">Ride</a> guitarist/frontman <a href="/wiki/Andy_Bell_(musician)" title="Andy Bell (musician)">Andy Bell</a>. Their fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Heathen_Chemistry" title="Heathen Chemistry">Heathen Chemistry</a></i> was released in 2002. In 2004 drummer <a href="/wiki/Alan_White_(Oasis_drummer)" title="Alan White (Oasis drummer)">Alan White</a> left the band leaving them as a four-piece with addition of <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Zak_Starkey" title="Zak Starkey">Zak Starkey</a> as recording and touring unofficial fifth member and found renewed success and popularity with <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Believe_the_Truth" title="Don't Believe the Truth">Don't Believe the Truth</a></i> (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the recording of the band's seventh album <i><a href="/wiki/Dig_Out_Your_Soul" title="Dig Out Your Soul">Dig Out Your Soul</a></i> in May 2008, after being made official member duiring sessions, Starkey departed the band and with <a href="/wiki/Chris_Sharrock" title="Chris Sharrock">Chris Sharrock</a> as touring member, Oasis did their last tour as a collective band. During the tour the brothers' increasingly deteriorating relationship ultimately led to <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher" title="Noel Gallagher">Noel Gallagher</a> announcing in August 2009 that he would be leaving the band after a backstage altercation with <a href="/wiki/Liam_Gallagher" title="Liam Gallagher">Liam</a> before a <a href="/wiki/Festival" title="Festival">festival</a> appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCNoelGleaves_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNoelGleaves-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leparisien-split_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-leparisien-split-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ouestfrance-split_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ouestfrance-split-4">[4]</a></sup> The band, comprising the remaining members of Oasis and led by Liam Gallagher, decided to continue working together under the name <a href="/wiki/Beady_Eye" title="Beady Eye">Beady Eye</a> until breaking up in 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_of_Oasis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-End_of_Oasis-5">[5]</a></sup> while Noel went on to form his solo project <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher%27s_High_Flying_Birds" title="Noel Gallagher's High Flying Birds">Noel Gallagher's High Flying Birds</a>.</p>
<p>As well as singing in <a href="/wiki/Manchester_dialect" title="Manchester dialect">Mancunian</a> accents and references to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_culture" title="British culture">British culture</a> in their lyrics, Oasis were influenced by British guitar bands from the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They have had eight UK number-one singles and eight UK number-one albums, and won seventeen <a href="/wiki/NME_Awards" title="NME Awards">NME Awards</a>, nine <a href="/wiki/Q_Awards" title="Q Awards">Q Awards</a>, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Awards" title="MTV Europe Music Awards">MTV Europe Music Awards</a> and six <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Awards</a>, including one in 2007 for <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Outstanding Contribution to Music</a> and one for the Best Album of the Last 30 Years as voted by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> listeners; they have been nominated for three <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a>. As of 2009 Oasis have sold over 70 million records worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The band were listed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> book in 2010 for "Longest Top 10 UK Chart Run by a Group" after an unprecedented run of 22 top 10 hits in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The band also holds the <i>Guinness World Record</i> for being the most successful act in the UK between the years 1995 and 2005, spending 765 weeks in the top 75 singles and albums charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Formation_and_early_years:_1991–1992"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_years:_1991.E2.80.931992">Formation and early years: 1991–1992 <span id="the_rain"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and early years: 1991–1992">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oasis evolved from an earlier band called the Rain, composed of <a href="/wiki/Paul_McGuigan_(musician)" title="Paul McGuigan (musician)">Paul McGuigan</a> (bass guitar), <a href="/wiki/Paul_Arthurs" title="Paul Arthurs">Paul Arthurs</a> (guitar), <a href="/wiki/Tony_McCarroll" title="Tony McCarroll">Tony McCarroll</a> (drums) and Chris Hutton (vocals). Unsatisfied with Hutton, Arthurs invited and auditioned acquaintance <a href="/wiki/Liam_Gallagher" title="Liam Gallagher">Liam Gallagher</a> as a replacement. Liam suggested that the band name be changed to Oasis. This change was inspired by an <a href="/wiki/Inspiral_Carpets" title="Inspiral Carpets">Inspiral Carpets</a> tour poster that hung in the Gallagher brothers' bedroom. One of the venues the poster listed was the <a href="/wiki/Oasis_Leisure_Centre" title="Oasis Leisure Centre">Oasis Leisure Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oasis played their first ever live gig on 18 August 1991 at the <a href="/wiki/Boardwalk_(music_club)" title="Boardwalk (music club)">Boardwalk</a> club in Manchester. Liam's brother <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher" title="Noel Gallagher">Noel Gallagher</a>, who was a <a href="/wiki/Road_crew" title="Road crew">roadie</a> for Inspiral Carpets, went with the band to watch his younger brother's band play. Whilst Noel and his friends did not think Oasis sounded particularly spectacular, he did begin to consider the possibility of using his brother's group as a possible outlet for a series of songs he had been writing for several years. Noel approached the group about joining with the proviso that he would become the band's sole songwriter and leader, and that they would commit to an earnest pursuit of commercial success. "He had loads of stuff written," Arthurs recalled. "When he walked in, we were a band making a racket with four tunes. All of a sudden, there were loads of ideas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Oasis under Noel Gallagher crafted a musical approach that relied on simplicity: with Arthurs and McGuigan restricted to playing <a href="/wiki/Barre_chord" title="Barre chord">barre chords</a> and <a href="/wiki/Root_(chord)" title="Root (chord)">root bass notes</a>, McCarroll playing basic rhythms, and the band's amplifiers turned up to create distortion, Oasis created a sound "so devoid of finesse and complexity that it came out sounding pretty much unstoppable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Definitely_Maybe:_1993–1994"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitely_Maybe:_1993.E2.80.931994"><i>Definitely Maybe</i>: 1993–1994</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitely Maybe: 1993–1994">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After over a year of live shows, rehearsals and a recording of a demo (known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Live_Demonstration" title="Live Demonstration">Live Demonstration</a></i> tape), the band's big break came in May 1993 when they were spotted by Creation Records co-owner <a href="/wiki/Alan_McGee" title="Alan McGee">Alan McGee</a>. Oasis were invited to play a gig at <a href="/wiki/King_Tut%27s_Wah_Wah_Hut" title="King Tut's Wah Wah Hut">King Tut's Wah Wah Hut</a> club in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, Scotland, by a band called Sister Lovers, who shared their rehearsal rooms. Oasis, along with a group of friends, hired a van and made the journey to Glasgow. When they arrived, they were refused entry to the club as they were not on that night's set list, which reportedly caused the band to bully their way in (although the band and McGee have given contradicting statements about how they managed to get into the club).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> They were given the opening slot and impressed McGee, who was there to see <a href="/wiki/18_Wheeler_(band)" title="18 Wheeler (band)">18 Wheeler</a>, one of his own bands. McGee was so impressed he offered them a recording contract; they however did not sign until several months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Due to problems securing an American contract, Oasis ended up signing a worldwide contract with <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a>, which in turn licensed Oasis to Creation in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Following a limited white label release of the demo of their song "Columbia", their first single, "<a href="/wiki/Supersonic_(Oasis_song)" title="Supersonic (Oasis song)">Supersonic</a>", was released in April 1994, reaching number 31 in the charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The release was followed by "<a href="/wiki/Shakermaker" title="Shakermaker">Shakermaker</a>". This song would become the subject of a plagiarism suit, with Oasis paying $500,000 in damages. Their third single, "<a href="/wiki/Live_Forever" title="Live Forever">Live Forever</a>", was their first to enter the top ten of the UK charts. After troubled recording and mixing sessions, their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Definitely_Maybe" title="Definitely Maybe">Definitely Maybe</a></i>, was released on 29 August 1994, entering the charts at number one within a week of its release, and at the time becoming the fastest selling debut album in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nearly a year of constant live performances and recordings, along with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedonistic" title="Hedonistic">hedonistic</a> lifestyle, were taking their toll on the band. This behaviour culminated during a gig in Los Angeles in September 1994, leading to an inept performance by Liam during which he made offensive remarks about American audiences and hit Noel with a <a href="/wiki/Tambourine" title="Tambourine">tambourine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The incident upset Noel to such an extent he temporarily quit the band and flew to San Francisco (it was from this incident the song "<a href="/wiki/Talk_Tonight" title="Talk Tonight">Talk Tonight</a>" was written). He was tracked down by Creation's Tim Abbot and they made a trip to <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas</a>. Once there, the elder Gallagher was persuaded to continue with the band. He reconciled with his brother and the tour resumed in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The group followed up with the fourth single from <i>Definitely Maybe</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Cigarettes_%26_Alcohol" title="Cigarettes &amp; Alcohol">Cigarettes &amp; Alcohol</a>", and the Christmas single "<a href="/wiki/Whatever_(Oasis_song)" title="Whatever (Oasis song)">Whatever</a>", issued in December 1994 which entered the British charts at number three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="(What's_the_Story)_Morning_Glory?:_1995"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".28What.27s_the_Story.29_Morning_Glory.3F:_1995"><i>(What's the Story) Morning Glory?</i>: 1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: (What's the Story) Morning Glory?: 1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oasis had their first UK number one single in April 1995 with "<a href="/wiki/Some_Might_Say" title="Some Might Say">Some Might Say</a>". At the same time, drummer Tony McCarroll was ousted from the band. McCarroll said, on leaving Oasis, that he was "unlawfully expelled from the partnership" for what he called a "<a href="/wiki/Personality_clash" title="Personality clash">personality clash</a>" with the brothers. The Gallaghers, on the other hand, doubted McCarroll's musical ability, with Noel saying: "I like Tony as a geezer but he wouldn't have been able to drum the new songs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> McCarroll was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Alan_White_(Oasis_drummer)" title="Alan White (Oasis drummer)">Alan White</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Starclub" title="Starclub">Starclub</a> and younger brother of renowned studio percussionist <a href="/wiki/Steve_White_(drummer)" title="Steve White (drummer)">Steve White</a> and recommended to Noel by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Weller_(singer)" title="Paul Weller (singer)">Paul Weller</a>. White made his debut for the band at a <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i> performance of "Some Might Say". Oasis began recording material for their second album in May of that year in <a href="/wiki/Rockfield_Studios" title="Rockfield Studios">Rockfield Studios</a> near <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The band, by this point, had recorded the concert that would see release in August as <i><a href="/wiki/Live_by_the_Sea" title="Live by the Sea">Live by the Sea</a></i>.</p>
<p>During this period, the British press seized upon a supposed rivalry between Oasis and Britpop band <a href="/wiki/Blur_(band)" title="Blur (band)">Blur</a>. Previously, Oasis did not associate themselves with the Britpop movement and were not invited to perform on the BBC's "Britpop Now" programme introduced by Blur singer Damon Albarn. On 14 August 1995, Blur and Oasis released new singles on the same day, setting up "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_Britpop" title="The Battle of Britpop">The Battle of Britpop</a>" that dominated the national news. Blur's "<a href="/wiki/Country_House" title="Country House">Country House</a>" outsold Oasis' "<a href="/wiki/Roll_with_It_(Oasis_song)" title="Roll with It (Oasis song)">Roll with It</a>" 274,000 copies to 216,000 during the week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Oasis' management came up with several reasons for this, claiming "Country House" sold more because it was less expensive (£1.99 vs £3.99) and because there were two different versions of "Country House" with different B-sides forcing serious fans to buy two copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> An alternative explanation given at the time by Creation was that there were problems associated with the barcode on the "Roll with It" single case, which did not record all sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Noel Gallagher told <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> in September that he hoped <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alex_James_(musician)" title="Alex James (musician)">Alex James</a> of Blur would "catch AIDS and die", which caused a media furore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He subsequently apologised for this in a formal letter to various publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bassist Paul McGuigan briefly left the band in September 1995, citing nervous exhaustion. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Scott_McLeod_(bassist)" title="Scott McLeod (bassist)">Scott McLeod</a>, formerly of The Ya Ya's, who was featured on some of the tour dates as well as in the "Wonderwall" video before leaving abruptly while on tour in the US. McLeod later contacted Noel Gallagher claiming he felt he had made the wrong decision. Gallagher curtly replied "I think you have too. Good luck <a href="/wiki/Jobseeker%27s_Allowance" title="Jobseeker's Allowance">signing on</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> To complete the tour, McGuigan was successfully convinced to return to the band.</p>
<p>Although a softer sound led to mixed reviews, Oasis' second album, <i><a href="/wiki/(What%27s_the_Story)_Morning_Glory%3F" title="(What's the Story) Morning Glory?">(What's the Story) Morning Glory?</a></i> was a worldwide commercial success, selling over four million copies thus becoming the <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_albums_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of best-selling albums in the United Kingdom">fourth best-selling album</a> in UK Chart history and to this day, selling over 22 million units worldwide. This makes it one of the best selling albums of all time. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The album spawned two further hit singles, "<a href="/wiki/Wonderwall_(song)" title="Wonderwall (song)">Wonderwall</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Look_Back_in_Anger" title="Don't Look Back in Anger">Don't Look Back in Anger</a>", which reached numbers two and one respectively. It also contained the non-UK single "<a href="/wiki/Champagne_Supernova" title="Champagne Supernova">Champagne Supernova</a>"—featuring guitar and backing vocals by Paul Weller—that received widespread critical acclaim and peaked at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Songs" title="Alternative Songs">US modern rock chart</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Be_Here_Now:_1996–1998"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Be_Here_Now:_1996.E2.80.931998"><i>Be Here Now</i>: 1996–1998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Be Here Now: 1996–1998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>"What Oasis has done in Britain, unifying an entire country under the banner of a single pop act, a band could no longer achieve in a country like the US. In Britain the band reigns unchallenged as the most popular act since the Beatles, there is an Oasis CD in roughly one of every three homes there. Last month, the band drew 250,000 people to Knebworth for the biggest outdoor concerts in the country's history; The group's battling brothers, Liam and Noel Gallagher, appear as regularly as royalty on tabloid covers."</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">— Neil Strauss writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> on the group's escalating popularity (September 1996)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The group played their first headline outdoor concerts at <a href="/wiki/Maine_Road" title="Maine Road">Maine Road</a> football stadium, home of <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_F.C." title="Manchester City F.C.">Manchester City F.C.</a> whom the Gallagher brothers have been fans of since childhood, on 27 and 28 April 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Highlights from the second night featured on the video <i><a href="/wiki/...There_and_Then" title="...There and Then">...There and Then</a></i>, released later the same year. As their career reached its zenith, Oasis performed back-to-back concerts at Knebworth on 10 and 11 August. The band sold out both shows within minutes. The audience of 125,000 people each night for two nights (2.5 million people applied for tickets, and 250,000 were actually sold, meaning the possibility of 20 sold out nights),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> was at the time a record-breaking number for an outdoor concert held in the UK, and to this day the largest demand for a show in British history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liveforever_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-liveforever-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rest of the month proved to be difficult for the group. Oasis were due to record an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/MTV_Unplugged" title="MTV Unplugged">MTV Unplugged</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a> but Liam pulled out, citing a sore throat. He watched the performance from a balcony with cold beer and cigarettes, heckling Noel's singing between songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Four days later the group left for a tour of American arenas but Liam refused to go; the band decided to continue the tour with Noel on vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Liam rejoined the tour on 30 August, and on 4 September 1996, Oasis performed "Champagne Supernova" at the <a href="/wiki/1996_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="1996 MTV Video Music Awards">1996 MTV Video Music Awards</a> at <a href="/wiki/Radio_City_Music_Hall" title="Radio City Music Hall">Radio City Music Hall</a> in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTVAwards_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTVAwards-39">[39]</a></sup> Liam made gestures at Noel during his guitar solo, then spat beer all over the stage before storming off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTVAwards_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTVAwards-39">[39]</a></sup> A few weeks later Noel flew home without the band, who followed on another flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This event prompted media speculation that the group were splitting up. The brothers soon reconciled and decided to complete the tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>Oasis spent the end of 1996 and the first quarter of 1997 at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> in London and Ridge Farm Studios in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> recording their third album. Quarrels between the Gallagher brothers plagued the recording sessions. <i><a href="/wiki/Be_Here_Now_(album)" title="Be Here Now (album)">Be Here Now</a></i> was released in August 1997. Preceded by the UK number one single "<a href="/wiki/D%27You_Know_What_I_Mean%3F" title="D'You Know What I Mean?">D'You Know What I Mean?</a>", the album was their most anticipated effort, and as such became the subject of considerable media attention. By the end of the first day of release, <i>Be Here Now</i> sold 424,000 units and by the end of business on Saturday of that week sales had reached 696,000, making it the fastest-selling album in British history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The album debuted at number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> in the US, but its first week sales of 152,000—below expected sales of 400,000 copies—were considered a disappointment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Although early media reviews were positive, once the hype had died down, the album was criticised for being bloated and derivative with most of the critics focused on the extensive length of several songs, the heavier sound, and overproduction.</p>
<p>By this time the Britpop movement was in decline, and the band had failed to meet expectations with their third album. After the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Be_Here_Now_Tour" title="Be Here Now Tour">Be Here Now Tour</a> in early 1998, amidst much media criticism the group kept a low profile. Later in the year, Oasis released a compilation album of fourteen B-sides, entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Masterplan_(Oasis_album)" title="The Masterplan (Oasis album)">The Masterplan</a></i>. "The really interesting stuff from around that period is the B-sides. There's a lot more inspired music on the B-sides than there is on <i>Be Here Now</i> itself, I think", related Noel in an interview in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Standing_on_the_Shoulder_of_Giants:_1999–2000"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Standing_on_the_Shoulder_of_Giants:_1999.E2.80.932000"><i>Standing on the Shoulder of Giants</i>: 1999–2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Standing on the Shoulder of Giants: 1999–2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1999, the band began work on their fourth studio album. First details were announced in February with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Stent" title="Mark Stent">Mark Stent</a> revealed to be taking a co-producing role. Things were not going well and the shock departure of founding member Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs was announced in August. This departure was reported at the time as amicable, with Noel stating that Arthurs wanted to spend more time with his family. Arthurs' statement clarified his leaving as "to concentrate on other things".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> However, Noel has since offered a contradicting version: that a series of violations of Noel's "no drink or drugs" policy (imposed by Noel so that Liam could sing properly) for the album's sessions resulted in a confrontation between the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-book-46">[46]</a></sup> Two weeks later the departure of bassist Paul McGuigan was announced. The Gallagher brothers held a press conference shortly thereafter where they assured reporters that "the future of Oasis is secure. The story and the glory will go on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The now three-piece Oasis chose to continue recording the album, with Noel Gallagher re-recording most of Arthurs' guitar and McGuigan's bass parts. After the completion of the recording sessions, the band began searching for replacement members. The first new member to be announced was new lead/rhythm guitarist <a href="/wiki/Gem_Archer" title="Gem Archer">Colin "Gem" Archer</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_Stereo" title="Heavy Stereo">Heavy Stereo</a>, who later claimed to have been approached by Noel Gallagher only a couple of days after Arthurs' departure was publicly announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Finding a replacement bassist took more time and effort. The band were rehearsing with <a href="/wiki/David_Potts_(singer)" title="David Potts (singer)">David Potts</a>, but he quickly resigned, and they brought in <a href="/wiki/Andy_Bell_(musician)" title="Andy Bell (musician)">Andy Bell</a>, former guitarist/songwriter of <a href="/wiki/Ride_(band)" title="Ride (band)">Ride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_No._1" title="Hurricane No. 1">Hurricane No. 1</a> as their new bassist. Bell had never played bass before and had to learn to play it (with Noel since saying that Liam said, "If he can play the guitar, he can play the fookin' bass"), along with a handful of songs from Oasis' back catalogue, in preparation for a scheduled tour of America in December 1999.</p>
<p>With the folding of Creation Records, Oasis formed their own label, <a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_Recordings" title="Big Brother Recordings">Big Brother</a>, which released all of Oasis' subsequent records in the UK and Ireland. Oasis' fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Standing_on_the_Shoulder_of_Giants" title="Standing on the Shoulder of Giants">Standing on the Shoulder of Giants</a></i>, was released in February 2000 to good first-week sales. It peaked at number one on the British charts and number 24 on the <i>Billboard</i> charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Three singles were released from the album: "<a href="/wiki/Go_Let_It_Out" title="Go Let It Out">Go Let It Out</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Who_Feels_Love%3F" title="Who Feels Love?">Who Feels Love?</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Sunday_Morning_Call" title="Sunday Morning Call">Sunday Morning Call</a>", all of which were top five UK singles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The "Go Let It Out" video was shot before Bell joined the group and therefore featured the unusual line-up of Liam on rhythm guitar, Archer on lead guitar and Noel on bass. With the departure of the founding members, the band made several small changes to their image and sound. The cover featured a new "Oasis" logo, designed by Gem Archer, and the album was also the first Oasis release to include a song written by Liam Gallagher, entitled "Little James". The songs also had more experimental, psychedelic influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicstanding_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicstanding-52">[52]</a></sup> <i>Standing on the Shoulder of Giants</i> received only lukewarm reviews<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicstanding_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicstanding-52">[52]</a></sup> and is the band's lowest selling studio album.</p>
<p>To support the record the band staged an eventful world tour. While touring in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> in 2000, Oasis were forced to cancel a gig when an attack of <a href="/wiki/Tendinitis" title="Tendinitis">tendinitis</a> caused Alan White's arm to seize up, and the band spent the night drinking instead. After a row between the two brothers, Noel declared he was quitting touring overseas altogether, and Oasis were supposed to finish the tour without him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Noel eventually returned for the Irish and British legs of the tour, which included two major shows at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium_(1923)" title="Wembley Stadium (1923)">Wembley Stadium</a>. A live album of the first show, called <i><a href="/wiki/Familiar_to_Millions" title="Familiar to Millions">Familiar to Millions</a></i>, was released in late 2000 to mixed reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Heathen_Chemistry:_2001–2003"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Heathen_Chemistry:_2001.E2.80.932003"><i>Heathen Chemistry</i>: 2001–2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heathen Chemistry: 2001–2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Throughout 2001, Oasis split time between sessions for their fifth studio album and live shows around the world. Some gigs included the month-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tour_of_Brotherly_Love" title="Tour of Brotherly Love">Tour of Brotherly Love</a> with <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Crowes" title="The Black Crowes">The Black Crowes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacehog" title="Spacehog">Spacehog</a> and a show in Paris supporting <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>. The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Heathen_Chemistry" title="Heathen Chemistry">Heathen Chemistry</a></i>, Oasis' first album with new members Andy Bell and Gem Archer, was released in July 2002. The album reached number 1 in the UK and number 23 in the US,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> although critics gave it mixed reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicheathen_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicheathen-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> There were four singles released from the album: "<a href="/wiki/The_Hindu_Times" title="The Hindu Times">The Hindu Times</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Stop_Crying_Your_Heart_Out" title="Stop Crying Your Heart Out">Stop Crying Your Heart Out</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Little_by_Little_(Oasis_song)" title="Little by Little (Oasis song)">Little by Little</a>/<a href="/wiki/She_Is_Love" title="She Is Love">She Is Love</a>" which were written by Noel, and "<a href="/wiki/Songbird_(Oasis_song)" title="Songbird (Oasis song)">Songbird</a>", written by Liam and the first single not to be written by Noel. The record blended the band's sonic experiments from their last albums, but also went for a more basic rock sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicheathen_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicheathen-57">[57]</a></sup> The recording of <i>Heathen Chemistry</i> was much more balanced for the band, with all of the members, apart from White, writing songs. <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Marr" title="Johnny Marr">Johnny Marr</a> provided additional guitar as well as backup vocals on a couple of songs.</p>
<p>After the album's release, the band embarked on a successful world tour that was once again filled with incidents. In late summer 2002, whilst the band were on tour in the US, Noel, Bell and touring keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Jay_Darlington" title="Jay Darlington">Jay Darlington</a> were involved in a car accident in Indianapolis. While none of the band members sustained any major injuries, some shows were cancelled as a result. In December 2002, the latter half of the German leg of the band's European tour had to be postponed after Liam Gallagher, Alan White and three other members of the band's entourage were arrested after a violent brawl at a <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> nightclub. The band had been drinking heavily and tests showed that Liam had used cocaine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Liam lost two front teeth and kicked a police officer in the ribs, while Alan suffered minor head injuries after getting hit with an <a href="/wiki/Ashtray" title="Ashtray">ashtray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-60">[60]</a></sup> Two years later Liam was fined around £40,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The band finished their tour in March 2003 after returning to those postponed dates.</p>
<h3><span id="Don't_Believe_the_Truth:_2004–2006"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Don.27t_Believe_the_Truth:_2004.E2.80.932006"><i>Don't Believe the Truth</i>: 2004–2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Don't Believe the Truth: 2004–2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Liam Gallagher said Oasis began recording a sixth album in late December 2003 with producers <a href="/wiki/Death_in_Vegas" title="Death in Vegas">Death in Vegas</a> at Sawmills Studios in Cornwall. The album was originally planned for a September 2004 release to coincide with the 10th anniversary of the release of <i>Definitely Maybe</i>. However, long-time drummer Alan White, who at this time had played on nearly all of the band's material, left the band in early January 2004. At the time, his brother Steve White stated on his own website that "the spirit of being in a band was kicked out of him" and he wanted to be with his current girlfriend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> White was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Zak_Starkey" title="Zak Starkey">Zak Starkey</a>, drummer of <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a> and the son of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>' <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a>. Though Starkey performed on studio recordings and toured with the band, he was not officially a member and the band were a four-piece for the first time in their career. Starkey played publicly for the first time at <a href="/wiki/The_Lighthouse_(Poole)" title="The Lighthouse (Poole)">Poole Lighthouse</a>.</p>
<p>A few days later, Oasis, with Starkey, headlined the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a> for the second time in their career and performed a largely greatest hits set, which included two new songs — Gem Archer's "A Bell Will Ring" and Liam Gallagher's "The Meaning of Soul". The performance received negative reviews, with <i>NME</i> calling it a "disaster."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The <i>BBC'</i>s Tom Bishop called Oasis' set "lacklustre and uneventful ... prompting a mixed reception from fans", mainly because of Liam's uninspired singing and Starkey's lack of experience with the band's material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>After much turbulence, the band's sixth album was finally recorded in Los Angeles-based Capitol Studios from October to December the same year. Producer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Sardy" title="Dave Sardy">Dave Sardy</a> took over the lead producing role from Noel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> who decided to step back from these duties after a decade of producing leadership over the band. In May 2005, after three years and as many scrapped recording sessions, the band released their sixth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Believe_the_Truth" title="Don't Believe the Truth">Don't Believe the Truth</a></i>, fulfilling their contract with <a href="/wiki/Sony_BMG" title="Sony BMG">Sony BMG</a>. It followed the path of <i>Heathen Chemistry</i> as being a collaborative project again, rather than a Noel-written album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The album was the first in a decade not to feature drumming by Alan White, marking the recording debut of Starkey. The record was generally hailed as the band's best effort since <i>Morning Glory</i> by fans and critics alike, spawning two UK number one singles: "<a href="/wiki/Lyla_(song)" title="Lyla (song)">Lyla</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Importance_of_Being_Idle_(song)" title="The Importance of Being Idle (song)">The Importance of Being Idle</a>", whilst "<a href="/wiki/Let_There_Be_Love_(Oasis_song)" title="Let There Be Love (Oasis song)">Let There Be Love</a>" entered at number 2. Oasis picked up two awards at the Q Awards: one People's Choice Award and the second for <i>Don't Believe the Truth</i> as Best Album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Following in the footsteps of Oasis' previous five albums, <i>Don't Believe the Truth</i> also entered the UK album charts at number one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_Charts_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK_Charts-68">[68]</a></sup> To date the album has sold more than 7 million copies worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<p>In May 2005, the band's new line-up embarked on a large scale world tour. Beginning on 10 May 2005 at the <a href="/wiki/London_Astoria" title="London Astoria">London Astoria</a>, and finishing on 31 March 2006 in front of a sold out gig in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, Oasis played more live shows than at any time since the <i>Definitely Maybe</i> tour, visiting 26 countries and headlining 113 shows for over 3.2 million people. The tour passed without any major incidents and was the band's most successful in more than a decade. The tour included sold out shows at New York's <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> and LA's <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Bowl" title="Hollywood Bowl">Hollywood Bowl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rockumentary" title="Rockumentary">rockumentary</a> film made during the tour, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Lord_Don%27t_Slow_Me_Down" title="Lord Don't Slow Me Down">Lord Don't Slow Me Down</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Dick_Carruthers" title="Dick Carruthers">Dick Carruthers</a> was released in October 2007. A second DVD included live footage from an Oasis gig in Manchester from 2 July 2005.</p>
<p>Oasis released a compilation double album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Stop_the_Clocks" title="Stop the Clocks">Stop the Clocks</a></i> in 2006, featuring what the band considers to be their "definitive" songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The band received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brit_Award" title="Brit Award">Brit Award</a> for outstanding contribution to music in February 2007, playing several of their most famous songs afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Oasis released their first ever digital-only release, "<a href="/wiki/Lord_Don%27t_Slow_Me_Down_(song)" title="Lord Don't Slow Me Down (song)">Lord Don't Slow Me Down</a>", in October 2007. The song debuted at number ten in the UK singles chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Dig_Out_Your_Soul:_2007–2009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dig_Out_Your_Soul:_2007.E2.80.932009"><i>Dig Out Your Soul</i>: 2007–2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dig Out Your Soul: 2007–2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band's resurgence in popularity since the success of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don%27t_Believe_The_Truth" title="Don't Believe The Truth">Don't Believe The Truth</a></i> was highlighted in February 2008 when, in a poll to find the fifty greatest British albums of the last fifty years conducted by <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> magazine and <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a>, two Oasis albums were voted first and second (<i>Definitely Maybe</i> and <i>(What's The Story) Morning Glory?</i> respectively). Two other albums by the band appeared in the list – <i>Don't Believe The Truth</i> came in at number fourteen, and the album that has previously been heavily criticised by some of the media, <i>Be Here Now</i>, made the list at No.22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oasis recorded for a couple of months in 2007 – between July and September — completing work on two new songs and demoing the rest. Zak Starkey was made official member of the band during sessions. They took a two-month break, because of the birth of Noel's son. The band re-entered the studio on 5 November 2007 and finished recording around March 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> with producer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Sardy" title="Dave Sardy">Dave Sardy</a>. In May 2008, Zak Starkey left the band after recording <i><a href="/wiki/Dig_Out_Your_Soul" title="Dig Out Your Soul">Dig Out Your Soul</a></i>, the band's seventh studio album. He was replaced by former <a href="/wiki/The_Icicle_Works" title="The Icicle Works">Icicle Works</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Chris_Sharrock" title="Chris Sharrock">Chris Sharrock</a> on their tour but Chris was not an official member of the band and Oasis remained as a four-piece. The first single from the record was "<a href="/wiki/The_Shock_of_the_Lightning" title="The Shock of the Lightning">The Shock of the Lightning</a>" written by Noel Gallagher, and was pre-released on 29 September 2008. <i><a href="/wiki/Dig_Out_Your_Soul" title="Dig Out Your Soul">Dig Out Your Soul</a></i>, the band's seventh studio album, was released on 6 October and went to number one in the UK and number five on the Billboard 200. The band started touring for a projected 18-month-long tour expected to last till September 2009, with support from <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Enemy_(UK_band)" title="The Enemy (UK band)">The Enemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twisted_Wheel_(band)" title="Twisted Wheel (band)">Twisted Wheel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tourdates_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-tourdates-76">[76]</a></sup> On 7 September 2008, while performing at <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Festival" title="Virgin Festival">Virgin Festival</a> in Toronto, a member of the audience ran on stage and physically assaulted Noel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attack.21_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Attack.21-77">[77]</a></sup> Noel suffered three broken and dislodged ribs as a result from the attack, and the group had to cancel several shows while he recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attack.21_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-Attack.21-77">[77]</a></sup> In June 2008, the band re-signed with Sony BMG for a three-album deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 25 February 2009, Oasis received the <a href="/wiki/List_of_NME_Award_winners#Shockwaves_NME_Awards_2009" title="List of NME Award winners">NME Award</a> for Best British Band of 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> as well as best blog for Noel's 'Tales from the Middle of Nowhere'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 June 2009, Oasis played the first of three concerts at Manchester's Heaton Park and after having to leave the stage twice due to a generator failure, came on the third time to declare the gig was now a free concert; it delighted the 70,000 ticket holders, 20,000 of whom claimed the refund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The band's two following gigs at the venue, on 6 and 7 June, proved a great success, with fans turning out in the thousands despite the changeable weather and first night's sound issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Split_and_aftermath:_2009–present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Split_and_aftermath:_2009.E2.80.93present">Split and aftermath: 2009–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Split and aftermath: 2009–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of Liam suffering <a href="/wiki/Laryngitis" title="Laryngitis">laryngitis</a>, Oasis cancelled a gig at <a href="/wiki/V_Festival" title="V Festival">V Festival</a> in Chelmsford on 23 August 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Noel Gallagher made a statement saying the gig was cancelled due to Liam having 'a hangover'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail.co.uk_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail.co.uk-84">[84]</a></sup> (Liam then sued Noel for lying on that matter and demanded an apology, stating "I want Oasis fans, and others who were at V, to know the truth. The truth is I had <a href="/wiki/Laryngitis" title="Laryngitis">laryngitis</a>, which Noel was made fully aware of that morning, diagnosed by a doctor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Noel later issued an apology and the lawsuit was dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>) Tension between the brothers rose and a fight between them in a backstage area on 28 August 2009 reportedly resulted in Liam throwing a plum and wielding Noel's guitar like an axe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail.co.uk_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail.co.uk-84">[84]</a></sup> The group's manager announced the cancellation of their concert at the <a href="/wiki/Rock_en_Seine" title="Rock en Seine">Rock en Seine</a> festival near Paris just minutes before it was about to begin, along with the cancellation of the last date at I-Day Festival and a statement that the group "does not exist anymore".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leparisien-split_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-leparisien-split-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ouestfrance-split_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ouestfrance-split-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noelquitstatement_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-noelquitstatement-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Two hours later, a statement from Noel appeared on the band's website:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"It is with some sadness and great relief...I quit Oasis tonight. People will write and say what they like, but I simply could not go on working with Liam a day longer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 6 July 2011, Absolute Radio uploaded a video to YouTube where Noel Gallagher speaks about the night Oasis ended. Noel states within this video. 'If I had my time again I would have gone back and done the gig. I'd have done that gig and I'd have done the next gig and we'd have all gone away and we could have probably discussed it. We may never have split up.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 February 2010, Oasis won the award for Best Brit Album of the Last 30 Years at the <a href="/wiki/2010_Brit_Awards" title="2010 Brit Awards">2010 Brit Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Liam Gallagher collected the award alone before presenting his speech, which thanked Bonehead, McGuigan and Alan White but not his brother, Noel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Liam threw his microphone and the band's award into the crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> On 15 March 2010, Liam defended his actions at the awards ceremony, saying "I'm sick of it all being about me and Noel, the last couple of months has pretty much been all about me and him so I thought it was only right to mention the other lads who played on the album and the best fans in the world," and "I thought it was a nice gesture to give this to the fans, obviously it was misinterpreted as per usual." about throwing the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Time_Flies..._1994%E2%80%932009" title="Time Flies... 1994–2009">Time Flies... 1994–2009</a></i>, a compilation album containing singles by the group, was released on 14 June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The decision to make the compilation a singles collection, and the track order, was decided by Noel Gallagher, who released a number of videos to the Oasis <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> account relating to the collection.</p>
<p>On 26 February 2014, Noel via the band's official website announced that the first three studio albums would be reissued, remastered and re-released throughout the remainder of 2014 to commemorate the 20th anniversary of <i>Definitely Maybe</i>. A remastered 3-disc version of <i>Definitely Maybe</i> was released on 19 May 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_Media_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork_Media-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Oasis:_Supersonic" title="Oasis: Supersonic">Oasis: Supersonic</a></i> was released on 26 October 2016, which tells the story of Oasis from their beginnings to the height of their fame during the summer of 1996. Produced by the same team behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>-winning biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Amy_(2015_film)" title="Amy (2015 film)">Amy</a></i>, <i>Oasis: Supersonic</i> features up close and personal footage, as well as never before seen archive material and interviews with the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oasis were most heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, an influence that was frequently labelled as an "obsession" by British media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> In addition, members of Oasis have cited <a href="/wiki/The_Stone_Roses" title="The Stone Roses">the Stone Roses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a><i>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/T._Rex_(band)" title="T. Rex (band)">T. Rex</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slade" title="Slade">Slade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Small_Faces" title="Small Faces">Small Faces</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">Nirvana</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Stooges" title="The Stooges">the Stooges</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_La%27s" title="The La's">the La's</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">the Doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Green_(musician)" title="Peter Green (musician)">Peter Green-era</a> <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood_Mac" title="Fleetwood Mac">Fleetwood Mac</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">the Kinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">the Jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Verve" title="The Verve">the Verve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Velvet_Underground" title="The Velvet Underground">the Velvet Underground</a>, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">the Smiths</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> as an influence or inspiration.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_battles_over_songwriter_credits">Legal battles over songwriter credits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Legal battles over songwriter credits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legal action has been taken against Noel Gallagher and Oasis for <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a> on three separate occasions. The first was the case of <a href="/wiki/Neil_Innes" title="Neil Innes">Neil Innes</a> (formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Bonzo_Dog_Doo-Dah_Band" title="Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band">Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Rutles" title="The Rutles">the Rutles</a>) suing to prove the Oasis song "<a href="/wiki/Whatever_(Oasis_song)" title="Whatever (Oasis song)">Whatever</a>" borrowed from his song "How Sweet to Be an Idiot". Innes was eventually awarded royalties and a co-writer credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-109">[109]</a></sup> Noel Gallagher claimed in 2010 that the plagiarism was unintentional and he was unaware of the similarities until informed of Innes's legal case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> In the second incident, Oasis were also sued by <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> and forced to pay $500,000 in damages to <a href="/wiki/The_New_Seekers" title="The New Seekers">the New Seekers</a> after it was alleged that the Oasis song "<a href="/wiki/Shakermaker" title="Shakermaker">Shakermaker</a>" had lifted words and melody from "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I%27d_Like_to_Teach_the_World_to_Sing_(in_Perfect_Harmony)" title="I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing (in Perfect Harmony)">I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-109">[109]</a></sup> When asked about the incident, Noel Gallagher joked "Now we all drink <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> On the third and final occasion, when promotional copies of <i>(What's the Story) Morning Glory?</i> were originally distributed, they contained a previously unreleased bonus song called "Step Out". This promotional CD was quickly withdrawn and replaced with a version that omitted the controversial song, which was allegedly similar to the <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Uptight_(Everything%27s_Alright)" title="Uptight (Everything's Alright)">Uptight (Everything's Alright)</a>". "Step Out" later reappeared as the B-side to "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Look_Back_in_Anger" title="Don't Look Back in Anger">Don't Look Back in Anger</a>", albeit now listing "Wonder, et. al" as co-writers.</p>
<p>On the flip side, the 2003 song "<a href="/wiki/Life_Got_Cold" title="Life Got Cold">Life Got Cold</a>" by UK girl band <a href="/wiki/Girls_Aloud" title="Girls Aloud">Girls Aloud</a> received attention due to similarities between the guitar riff and melody of "Life Got Cold" and that of the song "<a href="/wiki/Wonderwall_(song)" title="Wonderwall (song)">Wonderwall</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> A BBC review stated "part of the chorus sounds like it is going to turn into Wonderwall by Oasis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-114">[114]</a></sup> A source told <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i> that Girls Aloud "are all big Oasis fans so I'm sure they won't mind comparisons with their classic love song."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Warner/Chappell_Music" title="Warner/Chappell Music">Warner/Chappell Music</a> has since credited Oasis songwriter <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher" title="Noel Gallagher">Noel Gallagher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_influence">Legacy and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oasis_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
