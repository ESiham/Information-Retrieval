<p>"<b>Rain</b>" is a song by the English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a> first released in May 1966 as the <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-side</a> of the "<a href="/wiki/Paperback_Writer" title="Paperback Writer">Paperback Writer</a>" single. Both songs were recorded during the sessions for the album <i><a href="/wiki/Revolver_(Beatles_album)" title="Revolver (Beatles album)">Revolver</a></i> although neither appears on that album.</p>
<p>Written by <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> although credited to <a href="/wiki/Lennon%E2%80%93McCartney" title="Lennon–McCartney">Lennon–McCartney</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> "Rain" has been called<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> the Beatles' finest B-side, noted<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> for its slowed-down rhythm track and backwards vocals, both of which were a hint<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> of things to come on <i>Revolver</i>, released two months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheff2000197_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheff2000197-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005196_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005196-4">[4]</a></sup> Music critic <a href="/wiki/Jim_DeRogatis" title="Jim DeRogatis">Jim DeRogatis</a> describes it as "the Beatles' first great psychedelic rock song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDeRogatis200343_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDeRogatis200343-5">[5]</a></sup> The single's release marked the first time that reversed sounds had appeared in a pop song, although the Beatles used the same technique on the <i>Revolver</i> track "<a href="/wiki/Tomorrow_Never_Knows" title="Tomorrow Never Knows">Tomorrow Never Knows</a>", released in August 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReisingLeBlanc200994.2C_95_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReisingLeBlanc200994.2C_95-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three promotional films were made for the song "Rain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009-7">[7]</a></sup> These videos, along with other Beatles videos at the time, sparked <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> to say during the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Beatles_Anthology_(film)" title="The Beatles Anthology (film)">Beatles Anthology</a></i>, "So I suppose, in a way, we invented <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000214_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000214-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_lyrics">Music and lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Music and lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inspiration for "Rain" is agreed on by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Aspinall" title="Neil Aspinall">Neil Aspinall</a>, the Beatles' <a href="/wiki/Road_crew" title="Road crew">roadie</a>, and John Lennon. They both described the band's arrival in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, Australia, marked by rain and poor weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000140_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000140-9">[9]</a></sup> Lennon said, "I've never seen rain as hard as that, except in <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>", and later explained that "Rain" was "about people moaning about the weather all the time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000212_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles2000212-10">[10]</a></sup> Another interpretation is that the song's "rain" and "sun" are phenomena experienced during a benign LSD trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERevolution_in_the_Head_-_MacDonald_2005196-7_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERevolution_in_the_Head_-_MacDonald_2005196-7-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While technologically elaborate,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> "Rain" has a simple musical structure. Set in the key of G major (the final mix pitches it about a quarter of a semitone below this, while the backing track was taped in G sharp), it begins with what <a href="/wiki/Alan_W._Pollack" title="Alan W. Pollack">Alan W. Pollack</a> calls, "a ra-ta-tat half-measure's fanfare of solo <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drums</a>", followed by a guitar intro of the first chord. The verses are nine measures long, and the song is in <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>4</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span> time. Each verse is based on the G, C, and D chords <a href="/wiki/Chord_progression#Three-chord_progressions" title="Chord progression">(I, IV, and V)</a>. The refrain contains only I and IV chords, and is twelve measures long (the repetition of a six-measure pattern). The first two measures are the G chord. The third and fourth measures are the C chord. The third measure has the C chord in the so-called <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">6<br/>
4</span> (second) <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(music)#Inverted_chords" title="Inversion (music)">inversion</a>. The fifth and sixth measures return to the G chord. Pollack says the <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">refrain</a> seems slower than the verse, though it is at the same tempo, an illusion achieved by "the change of beat for the first four measures from its erstwhile bounce to something more plodding and regular". After four verses and two refrains, a short solo for guitar and drums is played, with complete silence for one beat. Following this, the music returns accompanied by what Pollack terms "historically significant" reverse lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollack1993_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollack1993-12">[12]</a></sup> Musicologist <a href="/wiki/Walter_Everett_(musicologist)" title="Walter Everett (musicologist)">Walter Everett</a> cites this closing section as an example of how the Beatles pioneered the "fade-out–fade-in <a href="/wiki/Coda_(music)" title="Coda (music)">coda</a>", a device used again by them on "<a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Fields_Forever" title="Strawberry Fields Forever">Strawberry Fields Forever</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Helter_Skelter_(song)" title="Helter Skelter (song)">Helter Skelter</a>", and by Led Zeppelin on "<a href="/wiki/Thank_You_(Led_Zeppelin_song)" title="Thank You (Led Zeppelin song)">Thank You</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEverett2009154_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEverett2009154-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allan_Kozinn" title="Allan Kozinn">Allan Kozinn</a> describes the unusual bass line in "Rain":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>McCartney's <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass</a>, placed in front of the mix, is an ingenious counterpoint that takes him all over the fretboard ... At the chorus, for example, while Lennon and McCartney harmonize in <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fourth" title="Perfect fourth">fourths</a> on a melody with a slightly <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> tinge, McCartney first points up the song's droning character by hammering on a high G (approached with a quick slide from the F natural just below it), playing it steadily on the beat for twenty successive beats. The next time the chorus comes around, though, he plays something entirely different, a slightly <a href="/wiki/Syncopation" title="Syncopation">syncopated</a> descending three-note pattern that almost seems to evoke the falling rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recording began on 14 April 1966, in the same session as "Paperback Writer", and concluded on 16 April, with a series of <a href="/wiki/Overdubbing" title="Overdubbing">overdubs</a> before mixing on the same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup> At that time, The Beatles were enthusiastic about experimenting in the studio to achieve new sounds and effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198870_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198870-16">[16]</a></sup> These experiments were showcased in their seventh album, <a href="/wiki/Revolver_(Beatles_album)" title="Revolver (Beatles album)"><i>Revolver</i></a>. <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Emerick" title="Geoff Emerick">Geoff Emerick</a>, who was the engineer for both sessions, described one technique he used to alter the sonic texture of the track by recording the backing track "faster than normal." When played back, slightly slower than the usual speed, "the music had a radically different tonal quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117-17">[17]</a></sup> The opposite technique was used to alter the tone of Lennon's lead vocal: it was recorded with the tape machine slowed down, making Lennon's voice sound higher when played back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198883_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198883-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The last verse of "Rain" includes backwards vocals, the first use of this technique on a record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlayboy_MagazineApril.2C_1981194_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlayboy_MagazineApril.2C_1981194-19">[19]</a></sup> (the hit <a href="/wiki/Novelty_song" title="Novelty song">novelty song</a> "<a href="/wiki/They%27re_Coming_to_Take_Me_Away,_Ha-Haaa!" title="They're Coming to Take Me Away, Ha-Haaa!">They're Coming to Take Me Away, Ha-Haaa!</a> ", where side B is side A played backwards, was released later that year of 1966). The backwards vocals are Lennon singing the lyrics of the song: "When the sun shines," "Rain," and "If the rain comes, they run and hide their heads."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYouTube:_Rain_Finale_backwards1995_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYouTube:_Rain_Finale_backwards1995-20">[20]</a></sup> Both Lennon and producer <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a> have claimed credit for the idea; Lennon said:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">After we'd done the session on that particular song—it ended at about four or five in the morning—I went home with a tape to see what else you could do with it. And I was sort of very stoned and tired, you know, not knowing what I was doing, and I just happened to put it on my own tape recorder and it came out backwards. And I liked it better. So that's how it happened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles_Interview_Database2007_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Beatles_Interview_Database2007-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilliland1969show_37.2C_track_4_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilliland1969show_37.2C_track_4-22">[22]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Emerick confirms Lennon's creative accident, but Martin remembers it differently:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I was always playing around with tapes and I thought it might be fun to do something extra with John's voice. So I lifted a bit of his main vocal off the four-track, put it on another spool, turned it around and then slid it back and forth until it fitted. John was out at the time but when he came back he was amazed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewisohn198874-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117-17">[17]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Later, in 1980, John claimed:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I got home from the studio and I was stoned out of my mind on <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">marijuana</a> and, as I usually do, I listened to what I'd recorded that day. Somehow I got it on backwards and I sat there, transfixed, with the earphones on, with a big hash joint. I ran in the next day and said, 'I know what to do with it, I know... Listen to this!' So I made them all play it backwards. The fade is me actually singing backwards with the guitars going backwards. [Singing backwards] Sharethsmnowthsmeaness... [Laughter] That one was the gift of God, of <a href="/wiki/Jah" title="Jah">Jah</a>, actually, the god of marijuana, right? So Jah gave me that one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Regardless of who is credited for the technique, "from that point on," Emerick wrote, "almost every overdub we did on <i><a href="/wiki/Revolver_(Beatles_album)" title="Revolver (Beatles album)">Revolver</a></i> had to be tried backwards as well as forwards."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "Paperback Writer"/"Rain" single was the first release to use a new device invented by the maintenance department at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road</a> called "ATOC" for "Automatic Transient Overload Control". The new device allowed the record to be cut at a louder volume, louder than any other single up to that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmerick2006117-17">[17]</a></sup> On the final mix of the single, Lennon played a 1965 <a href="/wiki/Gretsch" title="Gretsch">Gretsch Nashville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> a 1964 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rickenbacker_4001S" title="Rickenbacker 4001S">Rickenbacker 4001S</a> bass, Harrison a 1964 <a href="/wiki/Gibson_SG" title="Gibson SG">Gibson SG</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a> used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig-Musser" title="Ludwig-Musser">Ludwig</a> drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005196_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005196-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup> Both McCartney and Starr have been praised for their performances on their instruments in this song. Starr particularly liked his drumming effort.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>First released as a B-side to "Paperback Writer" in the United States (Capitol 5651) on 30 May 1966 and in the UK on 10 June 1966 (Parlophone R5452), the single was later released part of a <a href="/wiki/Record_Store_Day" title="Record Store Day">Record Store Day</a> reissue in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It later appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilations</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hey_Jude_(album)" title="Hey Jude (album)">Hey Jude</a></i> in the US and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rarities_(1978_The_Beatles_album)" title="Rarities (1978 The Beatles album)">Rarities</a></i> in the UK. It also appeared on the <i><a href="/wiki/Past_Masters" title="Past Masters">Past Masters</a></i> CD (Parlophone CDP 7 90044 2).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotional_films">Promotional films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Promotional films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Beatles created three promotional films for "Rain"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009-7">[7]</a></sup> which are considered among the early precursors of music videos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC2008_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC2008-26">[26]</a></sup> The films were directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lindsay-Hogg" title="Michael Lindsay-Hogg">Michael Lindsay-Hogg</a> who worked with them earlier on the pop 1960 television programme <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_Steady_Go!" title="Ready Steady Go!">Ready Steady Go!</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1998_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1998-27">[27]</a></sup> One features the Beatles walking and singing in a garden and a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> (filmed 20 May 1966 at <a href="/wiki/Chiswick_House" title="Chiswick House">Chiswick House</a> in London).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitchie2008_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitchie2008-28">[28]</a></sup> The other two feature the band performing on a <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stage</a> (filmed 19 May 1966, one in colour for Ed Sullivan and the other in black and white for the UK).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeaverson2009-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthology_DVD2003Episode_3.2C_.22Rain.22_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthology_DVD2003Episode_3.2C_.22Rain.22-29">[29]</a></sup> McCartney was injured in a <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a> accident on 26 December 1965, six months prior to the filming of "Rain" and closeups in the film reveal a scarred lip and a chipped tooth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1998_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1998-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnthology_DVD2003Episode_3.2C_.22Rain.22_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnthology_DVD2003Episode_3.2C_.22Rain.22-29">[29]</a></sup> McCartney's appearance in the film played a role in the "<a href="/wiki/Paul_is_dead" title="Paul is dead">Paul is dead</a>" rumours from 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Beatles' Anthology</i> documentary video includes a re-edit of two of these three clips, full of rhythmic fast cuts and several shots that went unused in the original videos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006320_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006320-30">[30]</a></sup> This creates an impression that the videos were more technically complex, fast-paced, and innovative than was the case. For example, the backwards film effects shown here are 1990s creations. Such effects were actually first deployed in the "Strawberry Fields Forever" promotional film of January 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006322_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2006322-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Beatles_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, the song peaked at number 23 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> on 9 July 1966,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and remained in that position the following week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The "Paperback Writer" single reached number one in the UK (for two weeks starting on 23 June 1966).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine ranks "Rain" 463rd in its list of "<a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone%27s_500_Greatest_Songs_of_All_Time" title="Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Songs of All Time">The 500 Greatest Songs of All Time</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2004b_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2004b-34">[34]</a></sup> On a similar list compiled by the New York radio station <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q104.3" title="Q104.3">Q104.3</a>, the song appeared at number 382.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Rain" is notable for <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a>'s drumming, which Starr rates as his best recorded performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiles1997280_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiles1997280-36">[36]</a></sup> Critics agreed: both <a href="/wiki/Ian_MacDonald" title="Ian MacDonald">Ian MacDonald</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> described his drumming on the track as "superb", while <a href="/wiki/Richie_Unterberger" title="Richie Unterberger">Richie Unterberger</a> of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> praised his "creative drum breaks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2007_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger2007-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005198_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacDonald2005198-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2004a_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERolling_Stone2004a-39">[39]</a></sup> In 1984, Starr said: "I think it's the best out of all the records I've ever made. 'Rain' blows me away … I know me and I know my playing … and then there's 'Rain'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEwww.beatleswiki.com.2Fwiki.2Findex.php.2FRain_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEwww.beatleswiki.com.2Fwiki.2Findex.php.2FRain-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>"Rain" cover versions include <a href="/wiki/I_Couldn%27t_Live_Without_Your_Love_(album)" title="I Couldn't Live Without Your Love (album)">Petula Clark</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ibex_(Queen)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ibex (Queen) (page does not exist)">Ibex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bongwater_(band)" title="Bongwater (band)">Bongwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humble_Pie_(band)" title="Humble Pie (band)">Humble Pie</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">The Jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shonen_Knife" title="Shonen Knife">Shonen Knife</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Punkles" title="The Punkles">The Punkles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxie_500" title="Galaxie 500">Galaxie 500</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hard_Meat" title="Hard Meat">Hard Meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyrock" title="Polyrock">Polyrock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Allman" title="Gregg Allman">Gregg Allman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antietam_(band)" title="Antietam (band)">Antietam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Chung_(band)" title="Wang Chung (band)">Wang Chung</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a> performed the song throughout the 1990s, often as an encore. <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a> has played the song in whole or in part throughout many of their tours, usually during outdoor concerts when it has started to rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEU2gigs.com2010_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEU2gigs.com2010-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam" title="Pearl Jam">Pearl Jam</a> improvised the song into their song "<a href="/wiki/Jeremy_(song)" title="Jeremy (song)">Jeremy</a>" during their 1992 <a href="/wiki/Pinkpop_Festival" title="Pinkpop Festival">Pinkpop Festival</a> show and played it in full at the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Festival" title="Isle of Wight Festival">Isle of Wight Festival</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kula_Shaker" title="Kula Shaker">Kula Shaker</a> covered the song live at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_Festival" title="Reading Festival">Reading Festival</a> in 1996 as did <a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a>, featuring <a href="/wiki/Dan_Ar_Braz" title="Dan Ar Braz">Dan Ar Braz</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/The_Cropredy_Box" title="The Cropredy Box">Cropredy Festival</a> in 1997. <a href="/wiki/Todd_Rundgren" title="Todd Rundgren">Todd Rundgren</a> has also covered the song,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFontenot2008-15">[15]</a></sup> as has the late <a href="/wiki/Dan_Fogelberg" title="Dan Fogelberg">Dan Fogelberg</a>, who reprised it as part of his own cover of "<a href="/wiki/Rhythm_of_the_Rain" title="Rhythm of the Rain">Rhythm of the Rain</a>".</p>
<p>The Beatles <a href="/wiki/Tribute_act" title="Tribute act">tribute act</a> <a href="/wiki/Rain:_A_Tribute_to_the_Beatles" title="Rain: A Tribute to the Beatles">Rain</a> derives its name from the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isherwood_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-isherwood-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
