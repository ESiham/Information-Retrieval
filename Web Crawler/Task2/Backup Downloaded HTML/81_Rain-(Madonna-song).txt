<p>"<b>Rain</b>" is a song by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_people" title="American people">American</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> from her fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Erotica_(Madonna_album)" title="Erotica (Madonna album)">Erotica</a></i> (1992). The song was released on July 6, 1993 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maverick_Records" title="Maverick Records">Maverick Records</a> as the album's fifth single internationally and the fourth single in North America. It was later included on her ballad compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Something_to_Remember" title="Something to Remember">Something to Remember</a></i> (1995). The song was written and produced by <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shep_Pettibone" title="Shep Pettibone">Shep Pettibone</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> ballad with influence from <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/New-age_music" title="New-age music">new-age music</a>, "Rain" features a more "friendly" composition than the other singles released from the album. Lyrically the song likens rain to the empowering effect of love, and as with water's ability to clean and wash away pain. Like the other songs on <i>Erotica</i>, sexual contact is also a possible interpretation of the song.</p>
<p>"Rain" received positive response from music critics, who noted it as an exceptional ballad amongst the overtly sexual content on <i>Erotica</i>. It peaked at number 14 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in the United States, while becoming a top-ten hit in Australia, Canada, Ireland, Italy, Japan, and the United Kingdom. The accompanying <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> was shot in <a href="/wiki/Black_and_white" title="Black and white">black and white</a> by director <a href="/wiki/Mark_Romanek" title="Mark Romanek">Mark Romanek</a> and colored manually with blue tones. It features Madonna singing the song against various backdrops on a set. The music video was praised by many critics for its innovation and cinematography. Madonna has only performed the song during <a href="/wiki/The_Girlie_Show_World_Tour" title="The Girlie Show World Tour">The Girlie Show World Tour</a> in 1993, while a remixed version of the song was used as a video interlude during her 2008–09 <a href="/wiki/Sticky_%26_Sweet_Tour" title="Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour">Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_release">Background and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Madonna_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the completion of filming <i><a href="/wiki/A_League_of_Their_Own" title="A League of Their Own">A League of Their Own</a></i>, Madonna began working on her fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Erotica_(Madonna_album)" title="Erotica (Madonna album)">Erotica</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Shep_Pettibone" title="Shep Pettibone">Shep Pettibone</a>. "Rain" was one of the first songs developed for the album, alongside "<a href="/wiki/Deeper_and_Deeper" title="Deeper and Deeper">Deeper and Deeper</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Erotica_(song)" title="Erotica (song)">Erotica</a>", and "Thief of Hearts", during the writing session in October and November 1991. According to Pettibone, these songs were essentially Madonna's stories and the things she wanted to say.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eroticadiaries_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eroticadiaries-1">[1]</a></sup> "Rain" was written and produced together by Madonna and Pettibone. She had initially written the song for a musical version to be directed by <a href="/wiki/Alek_Keshishian" title="Alek Keshishian">Alek Keshishian</a> based on the 1939 film, <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights_(1939_film)" title="Wuthering Heights (1939 film)">Wuthering Heights</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retten_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-retten-2">[2]</a></sup> Pettibone also sequenced, programmed, and played keyboards for the song. The recording process was engineered by P. Dennis Mitchell and Robin Hancock, while the mixing was engineered by Goh Hotoda. Another musician involved was Tony Shimkin, who provided the drum programming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_credits_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-single_credits-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Rain" was released as the fifth single from <i>Erotica</i> on July 6, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The song served as the album's fourth single in the United States and Canada since the previous single, "<a href="/wiki/Fever_(Little_Willie_John_song)#Madonna_version" title="Fever (Little Willie John song)">Fever</a>", was not commercially released there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The single release featured a previously unreleased track, "Up Down Suite", which is a 12-minute dub version of "Goodbye to Innocence", an omitted track recorded during the <i>Erotica</i> sessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This version of the single also included a <a href="/wiki/Danny_Saber" title="Danny Saber">Danny Saber</a> remix of the album track "Waiting" which features a rap by <a href="/wiki/Everlast_(musician)" title="Everlast (musician)">Everlast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicrain_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrain-7">[7]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the single included the 1986 track "<a href="/wiki/Open_Your_Heart_(Madonna_song)" title="Open Your Heart (Madonna song)">Open Your Heart</a>" which was being used in a <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> car commercial at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uksingle_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-uksingle-8">[8]</a></sup> "Rain" was later included on Madona's ballads compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Something_to_Remember" title="Something to Remember">Something to Remember</a></i> (1995) and has not appeared on any other compilation since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicrain_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrain-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>"Rain" is a <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> ballad with influence from <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/New-age_music" title="New-age music">new-age music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hear_this_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hear_this-9">[9]</a></sup> Styled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a> format, the song is more "friendly" in its sound than the other singles released from <i>Erotica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicrain_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrain-7">[7]</a></sup> According to the sheet music published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Music_Publishing" title="Alfred Music Publishing">Alfred Music Publishing</a>, the song is written in the key note of <a href="/wiki/E-flat_major" title="E-flat major">E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> major</a>. The song's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tempo_(music)" title="Tempo (music)">tempo</a> is set in a moderate pace, but not too fast and has a <a href="/wiki/Metronome" title="Metronome">metronome</a> of 92 beats per minute. "Rain" begins with Madonna singing "I feel it, it's coming", and followed by the <a href="/wiki/Chord_progression" title="Chord progression">chord progression</a> of E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span><sub>sus2</sub>–A<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span><sub>6/9</sub>–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span><sub>sus</sub> in the chorus and later A<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>–B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>–E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> in the verse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicnotes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-musicnotes-10">[10]</a></sup> A dark sounding <a href="/wiki/C_minor" title="C minor">C minor</a> string and an echoing <a href="/wiki/Hi-hat" title="Hi-hat">hi-hat</a> accompanies Madonna's vocals, which are sung in her lowest register.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rik_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-rik-11">[11]</a></sup> The song's arrangement captures turbulent elements associated with rain (such as thunder), orchestral stabs that invokes crisp lightning bolts, and a surging bridge segue driven by what sounds like electric guitar snarls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hear_this_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hear_this-9">[9]</a></sup> A key change happens towards the end from <a href="/wiki/B-flat_major" title="B-flat major">B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> major</a> to <a href="/wiki/C_major" title="C major">C major</a>, followed by two spoken parts and a harmony alongside it. The <a href="/wiki/Coda_(music)" title="Coda (music)">coda</a> has another melody with it, and the song ends in a group chorus without the harmonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rik_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-rik-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lyrically, Madonna compares rain with feelings of love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metz-12">[12]</a></sup> Just as rain washes away dirt, love washes clean the sorrows of the past, leaving a person and their lover to focus on their feelings of the present moment. Richard Harrington from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> described "Rain" as "an optimistic <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>-like ballad in which water is a metaphor for love ('Wash away my sorrow, take away my pain')."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metz-12">[12]</a></sup> According to Stephen Sears from music website <a href="/wiki/Idolator_(website)" title="Idolator (website)">Idolator</a>, "the synths turbo-charge at 2:46 into a sleek middle eight that features dueling Madonnas reciting lines of poetry out of the left and right channels [play it on earbuds]. 'By sheer force of will / I'll raise you from the ground', she says softly, 'And without a sound, you'll appear and surrender to me, to love'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idolator_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-idolator-13">[13]</a></sup> Sal Cinquemani from <i><a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a></i> thought lyrically the ballads on <i>Erotica</i> to be primarily concerned with sex, saying that some might even view "Rain" as an extended metaphor for <a href="/wiki/Ejaculation" title="Ejaculation">ejaculation</a>, though he didn't subscribe to that knee-jerk interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slantreview_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-slantreview-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Madonna_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="632" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg/220px-RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg/330px-RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg/440px-RainGirlieShowUnderGround.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Madonna and her backup singers, <a href="/wiki/Niki_Haris" title="Niki Haris">Niki Haris</a> (right) and <a href="/wiki/Donna_De_Lory" title="Donna De Lory">Donna De Lory</a> (left), performing "Rain" during <a href="/wiki/The_Girlie_Show_World_Tour" title="The Girlie Show World Tour">The Girlie Show World Tour</a> in 1993</div>
</div>

<p>Upon release, "Rain" received generally positive feedback. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> from <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> called "Rain" among "Madonna's best and most accomplished music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Jose F. Promis from the same media credited the song in "paving the way for the "softer" Madonna to emerge in the mid-'90s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicrain_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrain-7">[7]</a></sup> Arion Berger from <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> described the song as a "yearning ballad".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Tony Power from <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i> picked it as one of the stand-out tracks from <i>Erotica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Sal Cinquemani from <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> wrote, "Madonna's rarely acknowledged harmonies glide atop the frosty beats, thunder-claps of percussion, and skyward drone of the sonorous 'Rain'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slantreview_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-slantreview-14">[14]</a></sup> Annie Zaleski from <a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a> stated that "Rain" established Madonna as "a sensual New Age goddess."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hear_this_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-hear_this-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Holden" title="Stephen Holden">Stephen Holden</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described it as "one happy, open-hearted love song."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> While reviewing the album for <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i>, Paul Verna called the song "a lovely pop ballad."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In a separate review of the single on the magazine, Larry Flick wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A georgeous, romantic moment from [Madonna]'s sorely underappreciated <i>Erotica</i> opus. A slow and seductive rhyme base surrounded by cascading, sparkling synths inspires a sweet and charming vocal. Though not as lyrically daring as the previous "<a href="/wiki/Bad_Girl_(Madonna_song)" title="Bad Girl (Madonna song)">Bad Girl</a>", this is a wonderfully constructed, memorable tune that deserves as much attention (and airplay) as it can garner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Writing for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i>, Matthew Jacobs placed the track at number 43 of his list "The Definitive Ranking Of Madonna Singles" and felt the track was a departure from the previous "carnal" releases from <i>Erotica</i>; "It's not terribly distinctive from the other ballads Madonna released in the early '90s, but then there's the sultry chorus with the uplifting lilt 'Here comes the sun'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Stephen Sears from music website <a href="/wiki/Idolator_(website)" title="Idolator (website)">Idolator</a> called the song as "the album's sole expression of pure love", which "revisits the oceanic sonic landscape of her epic 1986 ballad "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_To_Tell" title="Live To Tell">Live To Tell</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idolator_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-idolator-13">[13]</a></sup> Charles Aaron from <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> wrote that the song "works a timeless, wrenching scenario: Our heroine waits on a mountaintop for love to wash away her sorrow."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Scott Kearnan from website <a href="/wiki/Boston.com" title="Boston.com">Boston.com</a> felt that the song is an example of "what she lacks in [singing] technique she's always tried to make up for with earnestness." He also praised her phrasing, saying that "Madonna sings like she believes in every word."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Rettenmund" title="Matthew Rettenmund">Matthew Rettenmund</a>, author of <i>Encyclopedia Madonnica</i> complimented the vocals on the song, calling them "elegantly layered... and an inventive, overlapping vocal rap that makes it a standout production".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retten_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-retten-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dodson_and_Fogg" title="Dodson and Fogg">Chris Wade</a>, author of <i>The Music of Madonna</i> felt that "Rain" was placed in the perfect sequence on <i>Erotica</i>, "to lift the album from its arguable slight drop in quality [with previous tracks 'Waiting' and 'Words']".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Rikky Rooksby, author of <i>The Complete Guide to the Music of Madonna</i> complimented the radio friendly sound of the song, but felt that the lyrics "had been used in countless songs", rendering them incoherent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rik_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-rik-11">[11]</a></sup> Chris Willman from <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> criticized the song's lyrics, saying: "Despite having crafted some of the best singles of the '80s, and despite being a genuine wit, Madonna can tend toward terribly banal rhymes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Alfred Soto from <i><a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a></i> dismissed the song as a "slushy rewrite of that year's '<a href="/wiki/This_Used_to_Be_My_Playground" title="This Used to Be My Playground">This Used to Be My Playground</a>', itself a slushy rewrite of <i><a href="/wiki/Like_a_Prayer_(album)" title="Like a Prayer (album)">Like a Prayer</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 'Promise to Try'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chart_performance">Chart performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Madonna_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chart performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, "Rain" peaked at number 14 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and spent a total of 20 weeks on the chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb100_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb100-27">[27]</a></sup> On its component chart, the song reached number 11 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_100_Airplay_(Radio_Songs)" title="Hot 100 Airplay (Radio Songs)">Hot 100 Airplay</a> and number 31 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Singles_Sales" title="Hot Singles Sales">Hot Singles Sales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> "Rain" was a considerable hit on <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a> radio stations, reaching number seven on both the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_Top_40" title="Mainstream Top 40">Mainstream Top 40</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adult_Contemporary_(chart)" title="Adult Contemporary (chart)">Adult Contemporary</a> charts. Additionally, the single also peaked at number 38 on the <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_(chart)" title="Rhythmic (chart)">Rhythmic Top 40</a> and number 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Dance/Electronic_Singles_Sales" title="Dance/Electronic Singles Sales">Hot Dance Music/Maxi-Singles Sales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusiccharts_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusiccharts-30">[30]</a></sup> The song was eventually allocated the number 67 position on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Year-End_Hot_100_singles_of_1993" title="Billboard Year-End Hot 100 singles of 1993">Billboard Hot 100 year-end</a> chart for 1993. In 2013, <i>Billboard</i> ranked it as the 40th Madonna's biggest hit in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In Canada, "Rain" achieved its highest chart placement, peaking at number two on the weekly Top Singles chart compiled by <i><a href="/wiki/RPM_(magazine)" title="RPM (magazine)">RPM</a></i> magazine on September 18, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rpm_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-rpm-32">[32]</a></sup> It also became a top-ten hit on the <i>RPM</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Adult Contemporary chart, reaching number seven on October 23, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rpmac_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-rpmac-33">[33]</a></sup> It became the 15th best-selling single of 1993 in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPM_yearly_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-RPM_yearly-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Rain" also found commercial success on several countries outside North America. In Australia, the song debuted at number 21 on the charts before rising and peaking at number five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-aus-35">[35]</a></sup> The song remained her longest-charting single, staying in the charts for 20 weeks, until "<a href="/wiki/Hung_Up" title="Hung Up">Hung Up</a>" (2005) remained on the charts for a total of 23 weeks. It was certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA) for shipments of 35,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-36">[36]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the song was also a success, peaking at number seven on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uk_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-uk-37">[37]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Official_Charts_Company" title="Official Charts Company">Official Charts Company</a>, "Rain" has sold 130,771 copies in the United Kingdom as of August 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uksales_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-uksales-38">[38]</a></sup> "Rain" also reached the top ten in Ireland and Italy, as well as the top twenty in New Zealand, Sweden and Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ire_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ire-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ita_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ita-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Madonna_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Madonna_Rain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="250" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Madonna_Rain.jpg/220px-Madonna_Rain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c9/Madonna_Rain.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Madonna_Rain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Madonna's completely new persona for the "Rain" music video. The blue tone of the video was done manually after being shot in <a href="/wiki/Black_and_white" title="Black and white">black and white</a>.</div>

<p>The music video for "Rain" was filmed by director <a href="/wiki/Mark_Romanek" title="Mark Romanek">Mark Romanek</a> during May 16–19, 1993 at a <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Airport" title="Santa Monica Airport">Santa Monica Airport</a> hangar in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romanek_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-romanek-41">[41]</a></sup> Other production crews include Krista Montagna as the producer, <a href="/wiki/Harris_Savides" title="Harris Savides">Harris Savides</a> as the director of photography, Jon Peter Flack as the production designer, Robert Duffy as the editor, and David Bradshaw as the wardrobe stylist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The video was entirely shot in <a href="/wiki/Black_and_white" title="Black and white">black and white</a> and then hand-painted with blue tones. It displays Madonna during a film shoot, with Japanese composer <a href="/wiki/Ryuichi_Sakamoto" title="Ryuichi Sakamoto">Ryuichi Sakamoto</a> playing the director alongside a Japanese film crew. Romanek and Madonna initially wanted to get <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard" title="Jean-Luc Godard">Jean-Luc Godard</a> or <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a> to play the director in the video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romanek_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-romanek-41">[41]</a></sup> Romanek commented: "I came up with the basic idea of setting it in Tokyo and showing the film crew. It was very Zen, very stripped away. She was this accessible, vulnerable creature surrounded by the high-tech and the global."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-video_vogue_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-video_vogue-43">[43]</a></sup> The director's primary inspiration came from watching a commercial by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Mondino" title="Jean-Baptiste Mondino">Jean-Baptiste Mondino</a>, starring actress <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Deneuve" title="Catherine Deneuve">Catherine Deneuve</a>. Madonna was also seen reclining on a riveted aluminium <a href="/wiki/Chaise_longue" title="Chaise longue">chaise lounge</a> known as the Lockheed Lounge, designed by then unknown <a href="/wiki/Marc_Newson" title="Marc Newson">Marc Newson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retten_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-retten-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The video begins with Madonna in a studio, lying on a sofa with headphones on her ears composing a song, following a sequence in which she sings in front of a microphone, which alternates with those of her receiving instructions from the director. She then appears in front of a background of bright lights, representing the sun-lit sky, and also in a scene of her kissing a man behind glass on which water falls. The video ends with an air view of open umbrellas covering the entire floor. Madonna introduced another completely new persona for the video. She wore a wig which provides "a waif-like cap of short black hair with spiky bangs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-video_vogue_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-video_vogue-43">[43]</a></sup> She also removed her trademark <a href="/wiki/Melanocytic_nevus" title="Melanocytic nevus">mole</a> and got her eyebrows back, which had been virtually invisible in her previous videos. The look took inspirations from Paris in the 1940s, cabaret singer <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dith_Piaf" title="Édith Piaf">Édith Piaf</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingenue_(stock_character)" title="Ingenue (stock character)">ingenues</a> in general. <a href="/wiki/Rei_Kawakubo" title="Rei Kawakubo">Rei Kawakubo</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comme_des_Garcons" title="Comme des Garcons">Comme des Garcons</a> provided the minimalist black clothes in the video, while the ethereal white clothes was designed by <a href="/wiki/Vivienne_Westwood" title="Vivienne Westwood">Vivienne Westwood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-video_vogue_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-video_vogue-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The video premiered on June 21, 1993 on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>, and later won two categories for <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award_for_Best_Art_Direction" title="MTV Video Music Award for Best Art Direction">Best Art Direction</a> and <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award_for_Best_Cinematography" title="MTV Video Music Award for Best Cinematography">Best Cinematography</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1993_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="1993 MTV Video Music Awards">1993 MTV Video Music Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retten_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-retten-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It was ranked number 70 on <i><a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "100 Greatest Music Videos" list. Sal Cinquemani from the publication called it as one of Madonna's most beautiful music video and said, "The waterlogged clip was a simple and refreshing break from the singer's visually sex-drenched <i>Erotica</i> period." He also pointed out that despite its innocent look, "it's difficult to separate the images from the song's double entendres."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Jef Rouner from <i><a href="/wiki/Houston_Press" title="Houston Press">Houston Press</a></i> called the video "one of Romanek's more light-hearted and definitely the most... purple of his work," as well as "a fascinating treatise on the act of creating a music video itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Bryant Frazer from <i>Studio Daily</i> described it as a futuristic, overexposed look influenced by Japanese fashion imagery." He noted that the video "pushed the boundaries of telecine work at the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Rettenmund called the video a "masterpiece about art and artifice". He noted that although Romanek expressed to create a video "devoid of nostalgia", instead the "Rain" clip "created a modern enigma [out of Madonna] who herself was no stranger to overexposure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retten_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-retten-2">[2]</a></sup> The video was later commercially available on Madonna's video albums <i><a href="/wiki/The_Video_Collection_93:99" title="The Video Collection 93:99">The Video Collection 93:99</a></i> (1999) and <i><a href="/wiki/Celebration:_The_Video_Collection" title="Celebration: The Video Collection">Celebration: The Video Collection</a></i> (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_performances_and_covers">Live performances and covers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_(Madonna_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Live performances and covers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Madonna has only performed the song during <a href="/wiki/The_Girlie_Show_World_Tour" title="The Girlie Show World Tour">The Girlie Show World Tour</a> in 1993. For the performance, Madonna and her back up singers, <a href="/wiki/Niki_Haris" title="Niki Haris">Niki Haris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donna_De_Lory" title="Donna De Lory">Donna De Lory</a>, appeared onstage wearing long black see-through robes. Haris recalled in <a href="/wiki/Lucy_O%27Brien" title="Lucy O'Brien">Lucy O'Brien</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Madonna:_Like_an_Icon" title="Madonna: Like an Icon">Madonna: Like an Icon</a></i>, "that was the first time [on stage] we sat down together and felt our harmonies. Madonna's voice was starting to get strong and she was into trying new things."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> During the bridge, they performed a fragment of <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> group <a href="/wiki/The_Temptations" title="The Temptations">The Temptations</a>' 1971 hit "<a href="/wiki/Just_My_Imagination_(Running_Away_with_Me)" title="Just My Imagination (Running Away with Me)">Just My Imagination (Running Away with Me)</a>". In his review of the concert in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Jon_Pareles" title="Jon Pareles">Jon Pareles</a> felt that during the performance the music was reminiscent of "Motown, girl groups and <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-50">[50]</a></sup> After the song was finished, an interlude, featuring a <a href="/wiki/Pierrot" title="Pierrot">pierrot</a> and several dancers dressed in black outfits and holding <a href="/wiki/Umbrella" title="Umbrella">umbrellas</a> began. Set to an instrumental version of the song, the interlude featured a choreography partially inspired in the classic <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kelly" title="Gene Kelly">Gene Kelly</a> musical <i><a href="/wiki/Singin%27_in_the_Rain" title="Singin' in the Rain">Singin' in the Rain</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-50">[50]</a></sup> According to author Michelle Morgan, Kelly himself advised Madonna on some of the choreography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The performance on the November 19, 1993 show at <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Cricket_Ground" title="Sydney Cricket Ground">Sydney Cricket Ground</a> was recorded and released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laserdisc" title="Laserdisc">Laserdisc</a> on April 26, 1994, as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girlie_Show:_Live_Down_Under" title="The Girlie Show: Live Down Under">The Girlie Show: Live Down Under</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fifteen years later, "Rain" was used as a video interlude for Madonna's <a href="/wiki/Sticky_%26_Sweet_Tour" title="Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour">Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour</a>. The song was remixed using elements of <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a>' 1984 hit "<a href="/wiki/Here_Comes_the_Rain_Again" title="Here Comes the Rain Again">Here Comes the Rain Again</a>" and featured the Japanese dancing duo <a href="/wiki/Hamutsun_Serve" title="Hamutsun Serve">Hamutsun Serve</a>. The video featured a pixie finding shade under a petal during a rainstorm and witnessing the transformation of a caterpillar into a butterfly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.26S_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.26S-54">[54]</a></sup> This performance was included on the <i><a href="/wiki/Sticky_%26_Sweet_Tour_(album)" title="Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour (album)">Sticky &amp; Sweet Tour</a></i> live CD and DVD release, recorded during Madonna's shows in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires, Argentina</a>, in December 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.26S_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-S.26S-54">[54]</a></sup> In 2000, British <a href="/wiki/Gothic_rock" title="Gothic rock">gothic rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_Stone_(band)" title="Rosetta Stone (band)">Rosetta Stone</a> covered "Rain" for Madonna's tribute album, <i>Virgin Voices: A Tribute to Madonna, Vol. 2</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The following year, a <a href="/wiki/Hi-NRG" title="Hi-NRG">hi-NRG</a>/<a href="/wiki/Eurodance" title="Eurodance">Eurodance</a> cover by Who's That Girl! was released through <a href="/wiki/Almighty_Records" title="Almighty Records">Almighty Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 2016, Madonna's former backup singers Niki Haris and Donna De Lory covered "Rain" and released it as a <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital single</a>. Their single release consists of the acoustic version and the Willie Ray Lewis remix version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
