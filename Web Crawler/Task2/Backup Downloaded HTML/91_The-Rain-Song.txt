<p>"<b>The Rain Song</b>" is a song by English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, and the second track from their fifth album <i><a href="/wiki/Houses_of_the_Holy" title="Houses of the Holy">Houses of the Holy</a></i>, released in 1973.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rain_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"The Rain Song" is a <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> of over seven minutes in length. Guitarist <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a> originally constructed the melody of this song at his home in <a href="/wiki/Plumpton,_East_Sussex" title="Plumpton, East Sussex">Plumpton</a>, England, where he had recently installed a studio <a href="/wiki/Mixing_console" title="Mixing console">mixing console</a>. A new Vista model, it was partly made up from the Pye Mobile Studio which had been used to record the group's 1970 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a> performance and <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Live_at_Leeds" title="Live at Leeds">Live at Leeds</a></i> album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>With a working title of "Slush", a reference to its easy listening simulated <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral</a> arrangement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-2">[2]</a></sup> Page was able to bring in a completed arrangement of the melody, for which singer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a> composed the words. Plant ranks his vocal performance on the track as one of his best.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q.26A_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q.26A-3">[3]</a></sup> The song also features a <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a> played by <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a> to add to the orchestral effect, while Page plays a <a href="/wiki/Danelectro" title="Danelectro">Danelectro</a> guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> was reportedly the inspiration for "The Rain Song" when he made a comment to Led Zeppelin drummer <a href="/wiki/John_Bonham" title="John Bonham">John Bonham</a>, about the fact that the group never wrote any ballads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lightshade_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lightshade-4">[4]</a></sup> In <i>Light and Shade: Conversations with Jimmy Page</i>, biographer <a href="/wiki/Brad_Tolinski" title="Brad Tolinski">Brad Tolinski</a> quotes Page:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>George [Harrison] was talking to Bonzo one evening and said, 'The problem with you guys is that you never do ballads.' I said, 'I'll give him a ballad,' and I wrote 'Rain Song,' which appears on <i>House of the Holy</i>. In fact, you'll notice I even quote 'Something' in the song's first two chords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lightshade_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-lightshade-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, there is some disagreement about exactly what was said by whom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsreview_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsreview-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_history">Live history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rain_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Live history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_concerts" title="Led Zeppelin concerts">Led Zeppelin concerts</a> from late 1972 until 1975, the band played this song immediately following "<a href="/wiki/The_Song_Remains_the_Same_(song)" title="The Song Remains the Same (song)">The Song Remains the Same</a>", presenting the songs in the same order as they appeared on the album. They organized their set list in this manner because Page used a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_EDS-1275" title="Gibson EDS-1275">Gibson EDS-1275</a> double-necked guitar for both songs: the top, 12-string neck for "The Song Remains the Same" and then switching to the bottom, 6-string neck for "The Rain Song". The song was dropped from the <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_North_American_Tour_1977" title="Led Zeppelin North American Tour 1977">1977 U.S. tour</a>, but returned for Led Zeppelin's <a href="/wiki/Knebworth_Festival_1979" title="Knebworth Festival 1979">1979 concerts</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen,_Denmark" title="Copenhagen, Denmark">Copenhagen, Denmark</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/Concerts_at_Knebworth_House" title="Concerts at Knebworth House">Knebworth Music Festival</a>, as well as their <a href="/wiki/Tour_Over_Europe_1980" title="Tour Over Europe 1980">European tour in 1980</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Complete_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Complete-2">[2]</a></sup> "The Rain Song" was the only song from <i>Houses of the Holy</i> performed on the 1980 European tour. In this incarnation, Page again utilized the double-neck, the only known time he used that guitar solely for the 6-string portion without using the 12-string portion on a preceding song. For all live versions of the song, the orchestral string sounds were played by Jones on either the <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">mellotron</a> (1972–1975) or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a> <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizer</a> (1979–1980), as Led Zeppelin never utilised a string section on-stage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rain_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a contemporary review for <i>Houses of the Holy</i>, Gordon Fletcher of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> gave "The Rain Song" a negative review, calling the track, along with "<a href="/wiki/No_Quarter_(song)" title="No Quarter (song)">No Quarter</a>", as "nothing more than drawn-out vehicles for the further display of Jones' unknowledgeable use of mellotron and synthesizer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rolling_stone_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rolling_stone-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a retrospective review of <i><a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_Deluxe_Edition#Houses_of_the_Holy" title="Led Zeppelin Deluxe Edition">Houses of the Holy (Deluxe Edition)</a></i>, Kristofer Lenz of <i><a href="/wiki/Consequence_of_Sound" title="Consequence of Sound">Consequence of Sound</a></i> gave "The Rain Song" a more positive review, described the track "one of the most sentimental tracks in Led Zeppelin’s catalog", along with calling it "patient and beautifully arranged".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoS_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoS-9">[9]</a></sup> Lenz further wrote that Plant's lyrics and vocals "infuse a sense of humanity, loss, and transcendence – a touch of emotional maturity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoS_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-CoS-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_versions">Other versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rain_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A different version of this song, which is almost exactly like the original but without the piano, is featured on the second disc of the remastered 2CD deluxe edition of <i>Houses of the Holy</i>. Titled "The Rain Song (Mix Minus Piano)", it was recorded on May 18, 1972, at the <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones_Mobile_Studio" title="Rolling Stones Mobile Studio">Rolling Stones Mobile Studio</a> at Stargroves with engineer Eddie Kramer and mix engineer <a href="/wiki/Keith_Harwood" title="Keith Harwood">Keith Harwood</a>. This version runs 7:45, while the original runs 7:39. <a href="/wiki/Page_and_Plant" title="Page and Plant">Page and Plant</a> recorded a version of the song in 1994 but it was not originally released on their album <i><a href="/wiki/No_Quarter:_Jimmy_Page_and_Robert_Plant_Unledded" title="No Quarter: Jimmy Page and Robert Plant Unledded">No Quarter: Jimmy Page and Robert Plant Unledded</a></i>. It was, however, released on the special tenth anniversary reissue of that album in 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_usage_in_other_media">Legacy and usage in other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rain_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy and usage in other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Record producer <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a> says, "I don't even know what kind of music this is ["The Rain Song"]. It defies classification. There's such tasteful, beautiful detail in the guitar, and a triumphant feel when the drums come in — it's sad and moody and strong, all at the same time. I could listen to this song all day. That would be a good day."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>"The Rain Song" has appeared in four films: The ending of <i><a href="/wiki/Cemetery_Junction_(film)" title="Cemetery Junction (film)">Cemetery Junction</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Almost_Famous" title="Almost Famous">Almost Famous</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Crowe" title="Cameron Crowe">Cameron Crowe</a> (who, as a teen reporter for <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine, covered Led Zeppelin), <i><a href="/wiki/It_Might_Get_Loud" title="It Might Get Loud">It Might Get Loud</a></i>, a documentary by filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Davis_Guggenheim" title="Davis Guggenheim">Davis Guggenheim</a>, and Led Zeppelin's own 1976 concert film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_Remains_the_Same_(film)" title="The Song Remains the Same (film)">The Song Remains the Same</a></i> (and <a href="/wiki/The_Song_Remains_the_Same_(album)" title="The Song Remains the Same (album)">accompanying soundtrack</a>), as part of lead singer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Song_Remains_the_Same_(film)#Fantasy_sequences" title="The Song Remains the Same (film)">fantasy sequence</a>.</p>
